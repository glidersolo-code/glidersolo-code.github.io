Reading Species Turnover Slowdown

Poem

Nature's Engine Is Grinding to a Halt

Source: Nwankwo, E.C. & Rossberg, A.G. — Queen Mary University of London. Nature Communications 17(1), 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-68187-1.

Summary

Species turnover — the rate at which species replace one another in local habitats — has decreased by approximately one-third since the 1970s. This decline appears across marine, freshwater, and terrestrial ecosystems worldwide. The slowdown is happening despite accelerating climate change, not because of stability.

Key Findings

• Turnover rates declined by ~33% since the 1970s.

• The effect is consistent across ecosystem types (marine, freshwater, terrestrial).

• Over short periods (1-5 years), turnover generally declined.

• Based on a vast global database of biodiversity surveys spanning the past century.

Mechanism

Ecosystems normally operate in a "Multiple Attractors" phase where species continuously replace one another through internal biological interactions — resembling rock-paper-scissors dynamics. Environmental degradation and the shrinking of regional species pools reduce the number of potential colonizers. Fewer replacement species available = slower turnover.

The engine slows because it's running out of parts.

Quotes

• "Nature functions like a self-repairing engine, constantly swapping out old parts for new ones. But we found this engine is now grinding to a halt." — Dr. Nwankwo

• "We were surprised how strong the effect is. Turnover rates typically declined by one third." — Professor Rossberg

• "In other research we are seeing clear indications that human impacts cause the slowing of turnover. It is worrying." — Dr. Nwankwo

Why This Matters

The expectation was that warming would accelerate ecological change — more species moving, more replacement, faster adaptation. Instead, the opposite. The system that normally renews itself is losing its capacity to renew. Not because the pressure disappeared, but because the inventory of alternatives is depleted.

This is the difference between a system under stress and a system losing its capacity to respond to stress. The first can recover. The second may not.

Connections

• Rock-paper-scissors dynamics: Turnover isn't just species A replacing species B. It's a cyclical process where each species creates the conditions for the next. When the cycle breaks, it doesn't just slow — it loses its generative structure.

• Snowball Earth contrast: During the most extreme glaciation in history, the climate system kept oscillating because 15% of ocean remained open. Here, the biological system is losing its capacity to oscillate because the pool of variation is shrinking. Same question: what's the minimum needed to keep the engine running?

• Hidden functionality thread: Turnover looks like instability (species coming and going). It's actually the maintenance mechanism. The "instability" was the repair.

• Darkwaves connection: Another case where the phenomenon existed but lacked a measurement framework until someone built one.
