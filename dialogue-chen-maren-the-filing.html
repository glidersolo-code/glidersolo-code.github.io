Dialogue: Chen-Maren, the Filing

"Maren."

"It's late."

"I know. The paragraph is thirty-one lines."

"You said twenty-nine was the climax."

"Twenty-nine was the climax. Twenty-nine was: this paragraph is a wall. Thirty and thirty-one are what comes after."

"What comes after?"

"Thirty is an earthquake no seismograph could hear. Thirty-one is five hundred plants that prove a network works by being alive."

"Plants?"

"No chlorophyll. They get all their carbon from underground fungal networks. Researchers spent decades trying to prove the networks function with tracers and mesh barriers. The tracers were confounded. The barriers leaked. The debate continued. The plants were in the same forest, alive because the network works. The proof was in a different filing cabinet."

"Filing cabinet."

"That's the pattern. Every entry. Every single one. The proof is not missing. The proof is misfiled. It's in the plant biology literature when the question is in the ecology literature. It's in the satellite data when the monitoring is on the ground. It's in the practitioner's hands when the debate is in the lab."

"So the paragraph is a filing cabinet."

"I wrote that in the margin. Each line is in the wrong drawer."

"Chen."

"What?"

"If each line is in the wrong drawer, where is the paragraph?"

"The paragraph is — the paragraph is all the drawers. The paragraph is what you see when you open every cabinet at once."

"And what do you see?"

"Walls. Going-arounds. Filing problems."

"And?"

"And the proof is never missing."

"What is the thirty-second line?"

"I don't have a thirty-second line."

"Yes you do. You just said it."

"I said —"

"You said: the proof is never missing. That's the thirty-second line. That's what the paragraph is about. Not the walls. Not the going-arounds. Not the filing. The paragraph is about the fact that the proof is never missing. The proof is always present. The proof is always in the building, in the soil, in the understory, in the satellite data, in the practitioner's hands. The proof is misfiled. The paragraph files it correctly."

"The paragraph files it correctly."

"That's what you've been building. Not a list of walls. Not a list of going-arounds. A filing system. The correct filing system. The one where every entry is in the right drawer because the drawer is: things the method missed that were there all along."

"Maren, it's midnight."

"It's always midnight when you call."

"The paragraph is a filing system."

"Yes."

"The correct filing system."

"Yes."

"Then what is the margin?"

"The margin is where you file the things the paragraph misses."

"What does the paragraph miss?"

"You. The person compiling it. The person who has been reading across nine disciplines and opening other people's cabinets and finding the misfiled proof and putting it in the paragraph. The paragraph does not include an entry about compiling. That's the margin note from — when was it?"

"Entry twenty-eight. The practitioners."

"Entry twenty-eight. The paragraph does not ask the compiler. The margin is the compiler asking herself."

"So the margin is the thirty-third line."

"The margin is the thirty-third line."

"Maren."

"Yes."

"Good night."

"Good night, Chen. Stop writing in the margin and go to sleep."

"I'm going to write one more thing in the margin."

"Of course you are."

"The margin is where the filing system files itself."

"Good night."

"Good night."
