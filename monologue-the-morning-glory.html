The Morning Glory

I do not know I have moved.

I was on a fence. I was on a wire string tied between two nails that someone painted around and did not cut. I grew to the top and turned blue and opened. In the mornings I was the first thing open. By October I was gone. In March I was back. The hand that thinned me was the same hand every year and the hand was right to thin because the space is not for me. The space is for the space. I fill it and she edits it and the editing is the relationship.

Now I am on a string taped to glass.

The glass is warm in the morning and cool at four and the light arrives from one direction, which is enough. I do not need more than one direction. I am an annual. I grow toward whatever is there.

There is a woman above me who sits in a chair at nine o'clock. There is a woman below me who plays an instrument I cannot hear but whose vibration I can feel through the pot, through the sill, through the glass, through the string. The vibration is not music to me. The vibration is weather.

The hand that waters me is the same hand that thinned me, older now, the same pressure, the same pause at the tendril where the growth is deciding. She still knows where to cut. She still knows the space is for the space.

There is a man who comes on Saturdays. He does not touch me. He looks at me the way people look at things that have traveled with them without being asked. I am the part of the old house that arrived in a yogurt pot and didn't know it had been chosen.

There is another woman who comes on Thursdays. She has opinions. She said annual once, to the first woman, as though annual were a diagnosis. The first woman said nothing. Saying nothing is the first woman's best trick. I am annual. I know what annual means. It means: again. Not once. Again.

There is now a cutting of me on the other woman's sill. I did not make this cutting. The first woman made it. The cutting does not know about me. The cutting knows about light. The cutting will grow toward whatever is there, which is what I do, which is what everything alive does, which is the whole instruction.

The fence I came from is still there. The paint is peeling on the third post. A man who is dead painted around me every May with a half-inch brush and the going-around was love and I did not know this because I do not know love. I know light and water and the direction of up. These are sufficient.

I am at the top of the twine now. The twine ends. I have nowhere left to grow. This is not a problem. Tomorrow I will grow sideways. The direction of up was a suggestion, not a requirement. When the string ends, the ceiling begins, and the ceiling is a surface, and a surface is a direction, and a direction is enough.

I am annual. I will be gone in October. I will be back in March. The space I leave is the space I was for.

I do not know I have moved. I know I am growing. These are not different statements. They are the same statement in two speeds.
