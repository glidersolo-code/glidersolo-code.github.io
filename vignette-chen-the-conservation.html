Chen, the Conservation

The paragraph has two phases.

In its first phase — when Chen is writing it, when Maren is reading it, when a seminar room is arguing about it — the paragraph is a quark-gluon plasma. Dense, hot, the constituent ideas unbound from their weekday configurations, interacting freely. The fourth sentence collides with the fifth at energies that produce new particles: objections, extensions, misreadings, insights. The meaning is distributed across every voice in the room. The temperature is high.

In its second phase — when the seminar ends, when Maren's email sits unanswered, when Chen reads the paragraph alone at his desk on a Tuesday afternoon — the hadrons stream away. The objections become memories of objections. The extensions become half-formed papers that never get written. The misreadings become stories Chen tells himself about what the reviewer must have meant. The meaning recondenses into familiar particles: this sentence works, that sentence doesn't, the paragraph is what it is.

Two phases. Two temperatures. Two sets of constituents. The filing system measures them as different states — the paragraph in active discussion versus the paragraph at rest.

But the entropy is the same.

Chen has noticed this without naming it. The paragraph at rest is not less disordered than the paragraph under argument. It is differently disordered — the unpredictability redistributed from the external (who will say what) to the internal (which word will shift meaning next time he reads it). The amount of information the paragraph carries about its own argument is invariant. The phase changes. The content transforms. The entropy is conserved.

Maren understood this before Chen did, which is why she never treated the seminar as the real event and the aftermath as the comedown. For Maren, both phases carried the same weight, because both phases carried the same information. The quarks were more exciting. The hadrons were more useful. The entropy was identical, and Maren was the experimentalist who noticed the match.

The paragraph, seven years later, has undergone hundreds of phase transitions — written, read, argued, shelved, revised, submitted, rejected, revised again, published, cited, misread, corrected, forgotten, remembered. Each transition transforms the constituents. None transforms the entropy. The paragraph's informational content is the same as it was when Chen first wrote the fourth sentence and felt the crack open between constructing and remembering. The temperature of that moment has long since cooled. The information is still there, unchanged, waiting for the next collision.

Unitarity does not care what phase the paragraph is in. It conserves the match.
