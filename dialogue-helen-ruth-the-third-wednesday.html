Dialogue Helen Ruth The Third Wednesday

Dialogue

Helen and Ruth, the Third Wednesday

RUTH: The boiler's quieter tonight.

HELEN: It's been serviced. The man came on Monday. He said the bearing was worn.

RUTH: I could hear it at night. Through the case.

HELEN: Through the case?

RUTH: When the cello's in the case. At night. The flat is very quiet. The boiler was company.

HELEN: Michael used to say that about the fridge. He said the fridge was the house talking to itself.

RUTH: That's good.

HELEN: He was good with things like that. He was not good with words generally. He was good with words for sounds.

RUTH: Like what?

HELEN: He said the kettle sounded like a cat deciding. He said the morning glory made a sound when it climbed — a sort of ticking. I never heard it. He said I didn't hear it because I wasn't the right height.

RUTH: Was he tall?

HELEN: He was Michael's height. Which was exactly right for hearing morning glories and too short for the top shelf.

(Pause.)

RUTH: My teacher in Bergen — Hallvard — he said the cello makes sounds the player can't hear. He said the back of the instrument vibrates at frequencies the player is too close to notice. He said you can only hear the whole cello from across the room.

HELEN: From across the floor.

RUTH: Yes.

HELEN: I hear the whole cello.

RUTH: I know.

(Pause.)

RUTH: Helen, can I ask you something?

HELEN: You can ask.

RUTH: When I play — through the floor — what is it like?

HELEN: It is like the fridge talking to itself.

RUTH: No, really.

HELEN: Really. It is like the building talking to itself. Not to me. The building is talking and I happen to be in the kitchen and the kitchen happens to be above the cello and the cello happens to be playing and the playing happens to arrive. I am not the audience. I am the location.

RUTH: That's why you didn't want me to play upstairs.

HELEN: I didn't say I didn't want you to play upstairs.

RUTH: You said you needed the Sarabande through the floor. Not in the room.

HELEN: Yes.

RUTH: Because through the floor, you're the location. In the room, you'd be the audience.

HELEN: In the room, I would have to listen. Through the floor, I get to hear.

(Pause.)

RUTH: Hallvard would have liked you.

HELEN: What was he like?

RUTH: He was — exact. He said what he meant. He never said "good" when he meant "adequate." He never said "fine" when he meant "wrong." He said: "The Third Suite is the one you play when you have forgiven yourself for not being able to play it."

HELEN: Michael said something like that about the fence. He said: "The fence is the thing you paint when you have accepted that the fence will need painting again."

RUTH: The same thing.

HELEN: The same thing.

(Pause. The kettle clicks.)

HELEN: More tea?

RUTH: Please.

HELEN: Ruth.

RUTH: Yes?

HELEN: The boiler being quiet is worse. I preferred the bearing.

RUTH: I'll ask the man to put it back.

HELEN: Don't be ridiculous.

RUTH: (smiling) I could tune it for you. Find a wolf tone in the pipes.

HELEN: The pipes have enough wolf tones. The pipes are entirely wolf tones. The pipes are a wolf.

(They laugh. The kettle pours. The third Wednesday is the Wednesday where they laugh.)
