The Wall

Thomas never enters Helen's week.

He arrives on Saturday. He sits in the chair. He drinks from the cup. He leaves after ninety minutes. Monday through Friday, Helen's week proceeds without him — the same routines, the same silences, the same crack in the stairwell wall that no one reports because reporting is an energy none of the surviving tenants can spare.

Thomas does not reorganize the week. Thomas does not add himself to the week's calendar. Thomas does not text on Wednesday to ask how she's doing or call on Thursday to fill the silence or appear on Tuesday with a bag of groceries and a worry in his voice.

Thomas stays outside the week and works on the wall.

The wall is Saturday. The wall is the boundary between Helen's interior life and the outside — the hallway, the other tenants, the crack, the heating that works on its own schedule, the world that leaks in through every gap in the aging infrastructure.

Helen's wall has been getting leaky.

Not dramatically. Not the catastrophic failure that would bring David rushing back with his reorganizations and his new kettle. A slow accumulation — like TNAP on the surfaces of blood-brain barrier cells — of small permeabilities. The cold that comes through the window frame where the seal has thinned. The noise from the street that used to be filtered by the double glazing and now arrives with the mid-range intact. Margaret's Tuesday voice, which used to stop at Helen's door and now seeps through the gap at the threshold with a clarity that suggests the door frame has shifted in its housing.

Things leak in that didn't used to leak in. And Helen's interior — the quiet, the thinking, the private relationship with the cup and the chair and the crack — becomes inflamed with outside. Small inflammations. Not enough to diagnose. Enough to erode.

Thomas trims TNAP.

Not the protein. The principle.

Thomas arrives, and for ninety minutes the wall tightens. Not because Thomas patches the window frame or adjusts the door or replaces the glazing. Because Thomas is the signal that the wall still works. The visit is the liver's enzyme — produced elsewhere, arriving through the bloodstream of the week, never crossing the barrier into Helen's interior life, but working on the barrier itself.

Thomas makes the wall less leaky by being the thing that the wall correctly stops.

This is the part the filing system cannot categorize. Thomas helps Helen's brain by never entering Helen's brain. Thomas improves Helen's week by never becoming part of Helen's week. The visit stays outside and repairs the boundary, and the boundary — tighter now, less permeable — protects the interior from everything else that would leak in.

The treatment is the wall.

"Good," says Helen when he arrives.

The word is the barrier functioning correctly — filtering the week down to a single syllable, blocking the inflammation of explanation, admitting only the signal and nothing else.

Thomas never enters.

The wall is the treatment.
