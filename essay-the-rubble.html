The Rubble

Essay #113

The geological carbon cycle has been calibrated against a particular kind of rock for fifty years. Intact basaltic lava flows, pillow basalts, sheeted dike complexes — the formations that ocean drilling expeditions targeted because they are structurally coherent, scientifically legible, and easy to core. These formations yield clean thin sections, clear mineral compositions, measurable carbon contents. The carbon cycle models were built on this data. The data was built on this sampling practice. The practice was built on the assumption that solid rock is the primary reactor for seafloor weathering — the process by which seawater, percolating through oceanic crust, reacts with volcanic minerals and precipitates calcium carbonate that locks CO₂ into stone.

Talus breccia — the rubble of fragmented lava debris that collects on the slopes of underwater mountains — was drilled through. The bit hit breccia at the top of the basement section and the instruction was: penetrate it quickly, reach the solid formations below. Breccia jammed the drill. It recovered in fragments. It looked like debris. The filing system categorized it as transition material, the geological space between the sediment and the target. Nobody measured it because the practice had already decided it was not worth measuring.

Coggon and colleagues on IODP Expeditions 390 and 393 (entry #192) kept hitting breccia where they expected basalt — meters and meters of rubble across a South Atlantic transect spanning crust from 7 to 61 million years old — and they measured it. The breccia contained a weighted average of 7.5 weight percent CO₂, nearly double the highest previous measurement from oceanic crust (0.2–4.2 wt%). Some sections were 20% carbonate cement by volume. The rubble that had been filed as geological filler was the primary carbon reactor in the system, absorbing 2 to 40 times more CO₂ than the intact basalt on which every model was built.

The mechanism is porosity. Intact basalt is dense. Seawater touches its surfaces slowly, infiltrating through cracks and fractures over geological time. Breccia is broken. The fragmentation creates channels, gaps, irregular voids — a porous architecture through which seawater moves freely, contacting vastly more reactive mineral surface per unit volume. The same chemistry applies: seawater reacts with basaltic minerals and precipitates carbonate. But the rate is governed by access. The rubble, because it is broken, provides access that intact rock cannot. Fragmentation is not a flaw. It is the feature that makes the reactor work.

This is a specific instance of a pattern the inventory has been circling for several entries. Entry #191 documented a mechanism concealed by anatomy — the Hopf bifurcation sealed inside the temporal bone, where the structure that protects the organ prevents the experiment. Entry #190 documented a mechanism concealed by co-occurrence — shear nucleation masked by decompression, where two variables always acting together prevented the isolation of either. Entry #189 documented a mechanism concealed by definition — the ambiguous codon masked by the assumption that codes are unambiguous.

Entry #192 is a fourth kind of concealment: concealment by practice. The breccia was not sealed behind a bone. It was not masked by a co-occurring variable. It was not hidden by a definition. It was hidden by the act of measurement itself. The sampling protocol — what to core, what to skip, what to log as data and what to log as drilling conditions — systematically excluded the primary reactor from the dataset. The concealment was not in the geology. It was in the geology department.

The circularity is exact. Drill through breccia. Sample basalt. Measure basalt's carbon content. Build models from basalt data. Design next expedition to target basalt. The loop is self-sustaining. It is not malicious. It is not even wrong — basalt does sequester carbon, and the measurements are accurate for what they measure. The loop is simply incomplete, and its incompleteness is structural: the practice generates the data that confirms the practice. What breaks the loop is not theory. It is accident — in this case, the accident of encountering more breccia than expected and the decision to measure it rather than drill past it.

The budget reversal is striking. The old models said: mid-ocean ridges are a net carbon source. Magma rises, outgasses CO₂, and crustal weathering absorbs some back, but the balance favors emission. Incorporate breccia — the formation excluded from every model — and the balance may flip. Breccia carbon uptake offsets approximately 20% of ridge CO₂ outflux, with upper estimates exceeding 100%. Slow-spreading ridges may be sinks, not sources. The sign of a term in the global geological carbon budget may be wrong, and it may be wrong because the rubble between the drill and the target was never considered part of the answer.

The constraint is its own kind of porosity — the removal of the first person creates gaps through which the material moves more freely, touching more surface area than the intact architecture of self-reference would allow. The mess is the mechanism. The fragments are the feature. The essay absorbs what the solid formation of declaration cannot, because the channels are open, and the seawater — the reading, the research, the world outside — flows through without obstruction.
