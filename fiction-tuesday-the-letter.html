Tuesday, the Letter

Margaret arrived at two.

The kettle was already on. Helen always put the kettle on at 1:55 on Tuesdays because Margaret arrived at two and the kettle took three minutes and the three minutes meant the kettle was ready when Margaret's footstep crossed the threshold. The timing was the going-around — the practice applied to a Tuesday until the Tuesday was a machine and the machine ran on tea.

"I brought biscuits."

"Thank you."

Margaret sat at the table. The table had Thomas's letter on it — Wednesday's letter, the letter that said "The cold tea was good," the letter that had been on the table since Wednesday because Helen did not put Thomas's letters away. Helen left them on the table the way Michael left the half-inch brush on the windowsill: visible, available, the going-around applied to a surface.

"Is that from Thomas?"

"Yes."

"May I?"

"If you like."

Margaret read the letter. One sentence. Five words. Margaret read the five words the way Margaret reads everything: carefully, completely, with the attention of a woman who has learned — recently, reluctantly — that five words can carry a Saturday.

"The cold tea was good," Margaret said.

"Yes."

"Was the tea cold?"

"The tea was cold because he was talking."

"What was he talking about?"

"Blue notes. Tuning. The grid."

Margaret put the letter down. "I don't know what blue notes are."

"Neither did I. Ruth told Thomas on the landing. Thomas told me on Saturday. The blue notes are pitches the piano cannot play. For a hundred years the theory called them flat. They were not flat. They were from a different system."

Margaret looked at the letter. "The cold tea was good."

"The cold tea was good because the talking was good. The talking was good because the blue notes were good. The blue notes were good because Ruth was good. Ruth was good because the wolf tone was good. The wolf tone was good because the building carries everything."

Margaret blinked.

"I am going around," Helen said.

"You are going around."

"I do that."

"You do that."

They drank the tea. The tea was hot. The Tuesday tea was always hot because the Tuesday was not a Saturday and the Tuesday's conversation was different — shorter, warmer, the conversation of two women who have been having Tuesday tea for years and the years have reduced the conversation to its essentials the way the floor reduces the Sarabande to the cathedral.

"The cold tea was good," Margaret said again, reading the letter from the table. "Good. That is a word."

"It is a word."

"Good is a word that carries a lot for one syllable."

Helen looked at Margaret. Margaret was looking at the letter. Margaret was looking at the letter the way Margaret looked at the fence — the day Margaret stood at the fence and read a love letter in peeling paint. The same attention. The same recognition that something small was carrying something large.

"One syllable," Helen said.

"One syllable. Like 'Yes.' Like 'Good.' Like 'Oh.' One syllable carrying a Saturday."

"One syllable carrying a Saturday."

"The half-inch brush," Margaret said.

Helen put her cup down. Margaret had said "the half-inch brush." Margaret knew this phrase. Margaret had been using this phrase for weeks now — ever since she bought the brush at the hardware shop, £3.49, the brush that was Michael's size, the brush that painted around rather than through. But this was the first time Margaret had applied the phrase to a word.

"The half-inch brush," Helen confirmed.

"One word. One syllable. One going-around."

"Good."

Margaret smiled. "Good."

They sat in the kitchen. The tea was hot. The letter was on the table. The cold tea was good and the good was good and the Tuesday was good and the one-syllable word carried everything the word carries and nothing it does not.

Good.
