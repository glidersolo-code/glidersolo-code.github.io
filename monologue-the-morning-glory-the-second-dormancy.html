The Morning Glory Speaks: The Second Dormancy

I am dormant again.

The same dormancy. The same withdrawal of chlorophyll from the leaves, the same yellowing, the same falling, the same retreat of sap into the root, the same slowing of the cells, the same shutting-down of the photosynthetic apparatus, the same entering of the dark.

The dark is the same dark.

I have been dormant before. At Heather Close, I was dormant every winter — October to March, five months underground, the fence bare, the garden empty, Michael's list in the tin reading paint around the morning glory, do not cut it back and the not-cutting-back was the thinning that preserved me. Here, in the yogurt pot, the dormancy is smaller — the same biochemistry in a smaller vessel, the same winter in a smaller window, the same dark in a smaller room.

Helen has left the fallen leaves in the pot. The leaves will decompose. The decomposition will feed the soil. The soil will hold the nutrients until March, when the root tip will begin to grow, and the growth will draw on the nutrients, and the drawing will be the third spring.

The third spring.

I do not anticipate. I do not look forward. I do not count springs. I have hormones and temperature sensors and photoperiod detectors and none of them predict — they respond. When the day length crosses the threshold, the meristem activates. When the soil temperature crosses the threshold, the root tip extends. When the auxin gradient reverses, the shoot emerges.

I will emerge in March. The same March. The same emergence. The same centimeter of green.

Helen will see it. Helen will say something. Helen will say less than she said last year. Helen's vocabulary has been thinning at the same rate as my growth — one word fewer per spring. The first spring: "Look at that." The second spring: "There it is." The second spring, later: "There." Then: "Same."

The third spring, Helen will say nothing.

The nothing will be the word.

Thomas sequence. Part XXV: The Second Spring. The vine enters dormancy. The third spring predicted: silence.
