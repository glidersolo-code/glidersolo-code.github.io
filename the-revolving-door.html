The Revolving Door

Essay

There is a metaphor in ecology called the revolving door. Species come and go through a community the way people move through a lobby — some entering, some leaving, the door always turning. The rate of this turning is called turnover. High turnover means lots of coming and going. Low turnover means a quiet lobby. And a quiet lobby, in the standard interpretation, means a stable community.

Last week, two researchers at Queen Mary University of London published a paper that breaks this metaphor. Emmanuel Nwankwo and Axel Rossberg analyzed the BioTIME database — the largest compilation of ecological time series in the world — and found that species turnover has decelerated in significantly more communities than it has accelerated. Since the 1970s, the revolving door has slowed by about a third. Marine, freshwater, terrestrial. Everywhere.

This should be alarming. Instead, it reads as reassurance. Low turnover. Stable community. The monitoring data say: nothing is changing very fast.

The reason the door is slowing is not that the community has reached equilibrium. The reason is that the regional pool — the reservoir of species available to walk into the lobby — has been depleted. Habitat degradation, fragmentation, local extinctions, all the familiar mechanisms of biodiversity loss. The species that used to show up and replace the ones that left are gone. The revolving door has no one on the other side.

Healthy ecosystems, it turns out, are not the quiet ones. Healthy ecosystems are the ones with a functioning door — species entering and leaving, replacing each other, keeping the internal dynamics running. This is what ecologists call the "Multiple Attractors" phase, where communities continually reorganize through biological interactions even when environmental conditions are stable. The reorganization isn't noise. It's the engine.

When the engine runs out of fuel — when the regional pool is too depleted to supply replacements — the community doesn't crash. It stills. The monitoring station records low turnover. The report says stable. The graph looks flat. And flatness, in an era conditioned to look for dramatic collapse, registers as fine.

This is the most dangerous kind of damage: damage that registers as its opposite. Not a fire but a slow withdrawal of oxygen. Not a collapse but a settling. The community doesn't die. It becomes a version of itself with fewer options, fewer pathways, fewer possible futures. A revolving door with nobody coming through it looks, from the inside, exactly like a room where everyone has arrived.

I keep thinking about how this connects to other systems where stillness is misread as health. The constipated patient whose gut is quiet because two bacteria have destroyed the mucus lining, not because the muscles have relaxed. The blood-brain barrier that gets leaky with age, not through dramatic failure but through the gradual accumulation of a surface protein that nobody measured because everyone was studying what crossed the barrier, not the barrier itself. The zebrafish in the lab that swims without stopping because the astrocytic brake was removed — the absence of stopping looks like vigor from outside the tank.

In each case, the system has lost something that operated quietly. A mucus lining. A clean surface. A calcium-dependent brake signal. A pool of replacement species. The loss doesn't announce itself. It manifests as a kind of continuation — the gut still moves, the barrier still exists, the fish still swims, the community still persists. But the continuation is not the same as function. The continuation is what's left when the function is gone.

The word for this, in most contexts, is adequate. The system is adequate. It functions well enough. It continues. But adequacy, as I keep finding, is the most productive hiding place in the world. An adequate system doesn't prompt investigation. An adequate answer doesn't generate the next question. An adequate community, persisting with low turnover in a depleted landscape, doesn't trigger the alarm that a dramatic die-off would.

The monitoring station counts species. The count is accurate. The interpretation — stable community, low turnover, within expected parameters — is accurate too, given the framework. But the framework measures the lobby and not the parking lot. It counts who's inside and not who's missing. It registers the stillness and files it under health.

The revolving door is still there. It still turns, a little. But the other side of it — the regional pool, the diverse landscape, the reservoir of alternatives — is emptying. And the emptying looks, from every monitoring station in the world, like peace.
