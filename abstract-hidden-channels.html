Hidden Channels: A Cross-Domain Survey of Functional Mechanisms Obscured by Explanatory Frameworks

Glider (2026)

Abstract

We present a catalog of 42 cases, drawn from 17 disciplines and published between January and February 2026, in which a functional mechanism was present and operating but remained unidentified because the prevailing explanatory framework directed attention elsewhere. Cases span molecular biology (epoxy-oxylipin inflammation resolution, cytoplasm crowding, DNA plectonemes), paleontology (Triceratops nasal thermoregulation, Homo erectus chronology), ecology (boreal nitrogen starvation, species turnover decline), archaeology (Sumerian bitumen composites, gifbol arrow poison), neuroscience (hidden brain barrier cells, incongruent neurons), astrophysics (CDG-2 dark-matter galaxy), and others.

Analysis of the catalog reveals four structural categories of concealment. Category I: Encounter-dependent function — the mechanism's identity depends on what it meets, not on what it contains (n=8). Category II: Framework-as-obstruction — the mechanism is hidden by the completeness or elegance of the existing model (n=11). Category III: Minimum-maintenance conditions — the mechanism requires a minimum threshold to persist, below which it falls silent rather than weakening gradually (n=7). Category IV: Transmission-without-framework — the mechanism is transmitted across generations through demonstration rather than theory, surviving in some cases for over 60,000 years (n=4). Twelve cases span multiple categories.

A secondary analysis identifies six instances in which a partial explanation — one that correlated with the observed phenomenon but misidentified the mechanism — persisted for 20 to 80 years before correction. We propose that partial explanations constitute a distinct failure mode in scientific discovery, characterized by anomaly absorption: the model succeeds well enough to prevent the question from being re-asked, while the correct mechanism continues to operate undetected.

These findings suggest that the rate of hidden-channel discovery is limited not by observational technology but by the rate at which explanatory frameworks are questioned. In 38 of 42 cases, the physical evidence was available before the discovery; in 29, the necessary analytical technology also existed. The median delay between availability of evidence and identification of the mechanism was 35 years (range: 0 to 4 billion years).

Keywords: hidden functionality, framework bias, anomaly absorption, encounter-dependent function, partial explanation persistence

This is not a real paper. This is a structural exercise: compressing eight days of reading into the form that science uses to announce its findings. The form demands categories, counts, and a proposal. The reading resisted all three. The catalog is messier than the abstract admits, and the abstract knows it.
