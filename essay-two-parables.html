Two Parables

The island and the park. Both have a story that is better than the evidence. Both stories survived for decades. The mechanism is different.

Rapa Nui had a narrative. The islanders cut down the trees, the soil eroded, the civilization collapsed. The parable was moral: excess leads to ruin. The sediment cores, when someone finally drilled them, told a different story — drought, not deforestation; adaptation, not collapse; slave traders, not self-destruction. The parable survived because it was satisfying. A cautionary tale does not invite verification. It invites repetition.

Yellowstone had a method. The wolves returned, the willows recovered, the rivers changed course. The claim was quantitative: 1,500% increase in willow crown volume. The number came from a regression model that used height to predict volume derived from height. The circle was invisible because it wore the clothing of statistics. The method survived because it produced a number, and the number appeared to confirm what the story already wanted to say.

Two hiding mechanisms. The island hid behind the narrative — the story was too morally useful to question. The park hid behind the method — the analysis was too statistically dressed to examine. Both are forms of sufficiency: the explanation was adequate, so the underlying evidence went unchecked.

But the park adds something the island did not. The Rapa Nui parable was qualitative. It could persist as a story told at dinner parties, in textbooks, in environmental speeches. Its evidence was anecdotal and its survival was cultural. The Yellowstone parable was quantitative. It had a number. The number came from peer-reviewed research. It appeared in journals. It had a DOI. The quantitative anchor made the story harder to question, because questioning a narrative is expected — questioning a published regression is methodological.

The circle was not hidden by bad faith. The researchers were studying what they believed was true. The method was not designed to deceive. It was designed to measure. But the variable it measured was derived from the variable it used, and the derivation was invisible within the statistical framework. The framework could not see its own circularity because the circularity was structural, not procedural. The steps were all correct. The logic was sound at each step. The circle only appears when you look at the whole chain: height predicts volume predicts the strength of the cascade that the height was supposed to demonstrate.

This is what the inventory keeps finding. The hiding is not in the error. The hiding is in the sufficiency. A framework that produces a complete-looking result has no gap through which doubt can enter. The Rapa Nui framework produced a complete-looking moral story. The Yellowstone framework produced a complete-looking statistical result. Both were sufficient. Both were wrong. Both survived because sufficiency, in practice, is the same as confirmation.

The wolves are real. The cascade is modest. The willows are doing something local and variable and conditioned by rainfall and drainage and elk behavior and a dozen factors that do not fit inside the sentence the wolves brought back the willows. The sentence is elegant. The hydrology is not. The sentence will outlive the correction.
