Maren, the Rejection

Chen forwarded the email at 8:47 AM. Maren read it at 9:12 AM, standing in the kitchen of the house in Clausthal, the coffee in her hand.

She read Reviewer 1: no formal search protocol... more essay than research.

She read Reviewer 2: the argument is circular.

She put down the coffee. She picked up the phone. She called Chen.

"Maren."

"I read it."

"And?"

"And the reviewers confirmed the paper."

Silence. Then: "Maren, the paper was rejected."

"The paper was rejected by the method the paper describes. The reviewer said our search protocol was inadequate. Our methods section predicted our search protocol would be found inadequate. The reviewer confirmed our prediction."

"That is a generous reading."

"Reviewer 2 said the argument is circular. The argument is circular. The argument's circularity is the argument. If you write a paper about how grids evaluate things, and the grid rejects it for not meeting the grid's criteria, the rejection is the paper's strongest footnote."

"PNAS does not publish footnotes from rejection letters."

"No." Maren paused. "But the rejection is now entry 144 in the spreadsheet."

"I added it this morning."

"I know you did. Chen, listen. The paper needs to go somewhere that understands what it is doing. PNAS is a grid. The paper is about grids. The paper needs a journal that publishes about grids — a journal of methods, or epistemology, or science studies. A journal where the reviewers understand that the circularity is deliberate."

"You want to resubmit."

"I want to revise. The methods section needs to address the circularity explicitly. Not defend against it — embrace it. The paper needs a paragraph that says: 'This paper is subject to its own argument. The review of this paper will apply the very standards the paper identifies as concealment mechanisms. If the paper is rejected on methodological grounds, the rejection is supporting evidence. If the paper is accepted, the acceptance is equally interesting — it means the grid recognized the argument about grids. Both outcomes are data.'"

"You want to add that to the paper."

"I want to add that to the introduction. I want the first thing the reviewer reads to be: you are the experiment."

Silence.

"Maren."

"Yes?"

"The paragraph is now 32 lines. If we add what you're describing, it will be 35."

"The paragraph grows the way it has always grown — one sentence at a time, each sentence changing the paragraph's relationship to itself. Let it grow."

"Where do we submit?"

"Studies in History and Philosophy of Science. Or Social Studies of Science. Or Synthese. Somewhere where epistemology is the method, not the obstacle."

"The method is the obstacle."

"The method is always the obstacle. That is the paper's argument. Now the paper has evidence."

Chen laughed. It was the first time he had laughed about the paper.

"Maren."

"Yes?"

"The rejection is the best thing that could have happened."

"I know," Maren said. "The forest knew too."

She hung up. She drank her coffee. Cold. Cold coffee was still coffee. A rejected paper was still a paper. The argument was still the argument.

The argument had just acquired its strongest evidence.
