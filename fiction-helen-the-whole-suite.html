Poem

Helen, the Whole Suite

Helen heard the whole Suite.

She heard the Prélude first, the way she always hears the Prélude — through the floor, through the joist, through the two hundred and fourteen millimeters, the Prélude arriving in the kitchen the way morning arrives: gradually, then all at once, the cello's lowest string becoming the room's lowest frequency, the kitchen vibrating at a pitch Helen's ears did not hear but her sternum did.

She heard the Allemande. She heard the Courante. She was not listening for the wolf tone. She had written "play the whole Suite" and the writing was the trust and the trust was complete and the completeness meant: she was not sorting. She was not thinning. She was not applying the half-inch brush to the sound that arrived through the floor.

She was hearing.

The Sarabande came and Helen sat in Michael's chair. This was habit — two years of habit, the Sarabande and the chair, the chair and the Sarabande, the sitting that was the listening and the listening that was the going-around applied to an evening. The Sarabande was the cathedral. The Sarabande was always the cathedral. Reduced, essential, the foundation of the Suite without the flesh. Helen had been living in the cathedral for two years.

The first Bourrée began and Helen's hands went still. The first Bourrée was new. Not new — Helen had heard the first Bourrée before, in the weeks before Ruth started leaving it out. But it was new now because the leaving-out had made a space and the coming-back filled the space differently. The coming-back was not the same as the never-leaving. The coming-back carried the leaving-out inside it, the way a repaired crack carries the knowledge of breaking.

The wolf tone came in the second Bourrée.

Helen did not know it was the wolf tone. Helen did not know what the wolf tone sounds like. Helen is not a cellist. Helen is a mother whose son visits on Saturdays and whose husband's fence grows morning glory and whose downstairs neighbor plays Bach through a floor. Helen's instrument is the kitchen. Helen's wolf tone is the kettle — the wobble in the whistle when the water is not quite boiling, the almost that Michael used to stand beside and wait for, the moment before the click.

What Helen heard was: a wobble. Four measures of something that was not the Sarabande's perfection, not the Prélude's certainty, not the Allemande's patience. Four measures of a cello disagreeing with itself. Four measures of the instrument saying: I am here. I am the instrument. The sound you are hearing includes the body that makes it, and the body is not perfect, and the imperfection is not a flaw.

The wobble passed. The Gigue came. The Suite ended.

Helen sat in the chair. The kitchen was silent. The silence after the whole Suite was different from the silence after the Sarabande. The silence after the Sarabande was the cathedral's silence — high, clean, the silence of a space that has been made by reduction. The silence after the whole Suite was the building's silence — the silence of everything, including the wobble, including the imperfection, including the four measures that disagreed.

The building's silence was larger.

Helen understood. She had written "the building will do the rest" and the building had done the rest and the rest was: the wolf tone arrived through the floor and the floor did not remove it. The floor reduced it. The floor thinned it. The floor applied the two hundred and fourteen millimeters to the wolf tone the way it applied the two hundred and fourteen millimeters to everything — the Sarabande, the Prélude, the morning glory's shadow on the ceiling, Thomas's footsteps on Saturday, Michael's absence every day. The floor did not choose what to carry. The floor carried everything.

The wolf tone, after two hundred and fourteen millimeters, was small. The wolf tone was four measures of a larger wobble that arrived as a smaller wobble. The wolf tone was the building's version of the wolf tone — thinned, reduced, essential. The wolf tone was the wolf tone after the going-around.

Helen put the kettle on. The water took three minutes. The whistle came. The wobble in the whistle came and passed and the click came and Helen poured the tea into Michael's cup — the cup Thomas uses now, the cup that is Michael's cup in the way the chair is Michael's chair, by inheritance, by practice, by the going-around applied to an object until the object belongs to everyone who has ever used it.

She sat. She drank the tea. The kitchen was quiet. Downstairs, Ruth was putting the cello away. The Suite was over. The whole Suite. Including the wolf.

Helen had heard the whole Suite. The building had done the rest. The rest was enough.

The rest was always enough.
