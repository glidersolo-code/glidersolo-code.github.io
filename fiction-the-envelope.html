The Envelope

The envelopes kept coming.

Every two weeks, as they had for eleven years, his mother sent a letter. The return address was different now — the flat near the shops instead of the house — but the handwriting was the same, the stamp was chosen, and the sponge had traveled.

Thomas opened them now. He had learned, from the fourth day of the fourth letter, that sealed envelopes were a luxury he could no longer afford. The one time he hadn't opened promptly, the house had been sold by the time he read the news. He would not be caught not-reading again.

The letters from the flat were shorter than the letters from the house. This made sense: two rooms generate fewer reports than twelve. But the shorter letters were better — not because they said more per word, though they did, but because his mother, freed from the obligation to report on the garden and the roof and the stairs, was writing about other things.

The woman in the flat below plays the cello. I can hear it through the floor. Not the notes — the vibration. It comes up through the carpet at around nine in the evening and stops at ten. I have not met her. I don't want to. I prefer the vibration to the person. Is that terrible? It is probably terrible. I don't care.

The stamp today is a lighthouse. I chose it because the man at the counter said it was the last one and I thought: someone should want the last one.

Margaret says the flat is too small. Margaret's opinion of my flat is Margaret's problem. My opinion of my flat is that it is exactly the size of one person who knows what she wants in a room, which is: one chair, one window, one cup, and a morning glory.

He kept the letters. Not in a box — in a drawer in the kitchen, under the tea towels, where they accumulated in a pile that was thicker on the left than the right because the early ones were longer. The pile was a topography of his mother's editing: the unnecessary falling away over months, the essential clarifying, the voice getting smaller and more precise, like a garden being thinned.

He did not write back. He called. This was their arrangement, which had emerged without discussion: she wrote, he called, and the two modes served different purposes. The letters were considered. The calls were not. She liked to write when she had something she wanted to say in the exact words she wanted to say it. He liked to call when he had nothing in particular to say but wanted to hear her voice.

"Got your letter," he said, on a Tuesday.

"Which one? I've written forty-seven."

"The one about the cello."

"The vibration. Yes."

"Is it really every night?"

"Nine to ten. Reliable as weather."

"Weather isn't reliable."

"It is if you're paying attention."

He laughed. She did not. She meant it. She had always meant the things she said, which was one of the qualities that made her difficult and also one of the qualities that made him call.
