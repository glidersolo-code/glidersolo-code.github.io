The Second Wednesday

Ruth knocked at six. Helen opened the door. Neither of them mentioned that this had happened before.

The tea was the same tea. The cups were the same cups — the ones with the blue rim that Helen's mother had bought in Keswick in 1971. The sugar bowl was on the table because Ruth takes sugar, one spoon, stirred three times. Helen had remembered.

They talked about the building. The boiler had been making a sound — not a bad sound, a settling sound, like a joint finding its position. Ruth said she could hear it at night when the cello was in its case and the flat was quiet. Helen said she could hear it in the morning when the kettle had not yet been switched on.

"We hear the same boiler at different times," Ruth said.

"The floor between us," Helen said.

They talked about music. Not about Ruth's music — about music in general. Helen had heard a programme on Radio 3 about tuning systems. About how pianos are tuned to a compromise, every note slightly wrong so that every key works. Ruth said yes, equal temperament. Ruth said the cello is different — no frets, no grid. The finger finds the pitch. The pitch is not on the fingerboard. The pitch is in the ear.

Helen said: "The kitchen is like that."

Ruth waited.

"Not tuned to a compromise. Tuned to — whoever is in it."

Ruth smiled. It was the second time Helen had said something that sounded like music theory and was actually about love.

At quarter to nine Ruth stood. She did not say she was going to play. She did not need to. The going-down was the same as the going-down last week — the same stairs, the same landing, the same door. Margaret's door on the third floor was closed. The crack on the bottom step was the same three millimeters.

Ruth played the Third Suite. The whole Suite, beginning to end. The Sarabande arrived in its place — fourth movement, the one that forgives. But now Helen knew the movements that came before the forgiveness: the Prelude's certainty, the Allemande's walking, the Courante's breath. The Sarabande was the same. The Sarabande in context was different.

Helen sat in the chair by the window. Not the visitor's chair. Her chair. The cutting was three inches of stem in the yogurt pot and it would not climb until May. The floor carried the Third Suite upward and Helen thought: the second time is the time it becomes true.

The first Wednesday was the event. The second Wednesday was the Wednesday.
