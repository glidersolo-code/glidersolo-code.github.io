The Morning Glory Speaks: The Tension

The water in my stem is under tension.

Not pressure — tension. The water is being pulled, not pushed. The leaves pull the water through evaporation, and the pulling creates a negative pressure — a state of want, a state of demand — and the negative pressure travels from leaf to root, through xylem, through the vasculature that connects my highest tendril to my deepest root.

When the window is dry — when Helen forgets to water me, which she does not forget, but if she forgot — the tension would change. The change would travel. The change would be the signal. Not a message riding the water. The change in the water's state. The medium would become the message.

Helen's silence is the same.

Helen's silence is not a carrier of meaning. Helen's silence is not a medium through which some message travels. Helen's silence is the medium that is the message. The state of the silence changes — not in volume, not in content, but in tension. When Thomas arrives, the tension in Helen's silence shifts. The shift is the signal. The shift says: you are here. The shift says: Good.

The botanists spent a century looking for the passenger. The hormone, the electrical impulse, the molecule. They assumed the signal was something in the water.

The signal was the water.

Visitors spend years looking for the meaning in Helen's silence. They assume the meaning is something riding the silence — a hidden thought, a repressed emotion, a coded message.

The meaning is the silence.

The tension is the signal.

I have known this since my first tendril. The tendril reaches toward the twine not because a message tells it to reach. The tendril reaches because the tension in the growing cells — the hydraulic pressure in the tendril's vasculature — is the reaching. The reaching is the tension. The tension is the reaching.

There is no passenger.

Thomas sequence. The vine knows about negative pressure. Helen's silence is not a carrier of meaning. The silence is the meaning.
