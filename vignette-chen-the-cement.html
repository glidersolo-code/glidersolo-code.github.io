Chen, the Cement

Chen reads the paper on Wednesday, standing at the bench because the stool has been commandeered by a stack of preprints. Tarhan, Boag, Kalderon-Asael. Yale. Lithium isotopes in Ediacaran sandstone.

She reads it twice.

The first time, she reads the mechanism: authigenic clays, silica-rich pore water, iron concentrations that no longer exist. The ocean cemented the bodies in place before they could decay. Not biological toughness. Chemistry.

The second time, she reads the assumption: that the answer was in the organisms. That the fossils survived because of something intrinsic to their bodies.

She puts the paper down.

For thirty years, the question was aimed at the fossils. How did they last? What were they made of? The assumption pointed inward, at the patient. But the answer was the medium. The answer was a chemical environment that has since vanished — drawn down by sponges and diatoms that didn't exist yet when the Ediacara Biota were being preserved.

Chen adds a line to the paragraph:

The doctor retired before anyone asked who performed the surgery. The conditions that produced the evidence are not available to the instrument that studies the evidence. The method arrives too late.

She looks at the line. It is the basis set problem again, but with a temporal axis. Not a truncated expansion. A truncated era. The basis functions that preserved the fossils have been removed from the expansion — not by a computational choice, but by five hundred million years of geochemistry.

The paragraph has seventeen lines now. It is not an abstract. It is not an introduction. It does not have citations, except when it does.

Lena would call this convergence. Maren would call it the same green arrow, pointing at the wrong variable. Chen calls it Wednesday.

She moves the preprints off the stool and sits down.
