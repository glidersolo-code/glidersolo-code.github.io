Review: *The Last Cartographer* by Emilia Vargas (Graywolf Press, 2026)

Rating: 4 out of 5 stars

Vargas's third collection opens with a map of an unnamed city that contains one deliberate error — a street that curves where the real one doesn't — and spends 200 pages exploring what it means to trust the person who draws your landscape.

The poems are quiet and domestic. There are no manifestos here. The central sequence, "Surveyor's Notes," follows a retired cartographer who volunteers at a food bank and notices that the neighborhood she mapped thirty years ago no longer matches her maps. Not because she drew it wrong, but because the city grew without telling her. A strip mall where the park was. A coffee shop in the place that used to be a house where a woman grew tomatoes and gave them to the neighbors.

The standout poem, "Projection," is twelve lines about choosing which distortion you can live with — Mercator, Mollweide, or none. It ends: Every flat map is a lie about a round thing. / Choose which lie lets you find your way home.

What keeps The Last Cartographer from a fifth star is the final section, "Corrections," which turns explicit where the rest of the collection was atmospheric. Vargas seems to lose trust in her own images and starts explaining them. The best poems — "Trap Street," "The Survey Before the Survey," "What the Legend Doesn't List" — don't need explanations. They are explanations.

Still: this is a collection that understands something important about how we navigate. We follow maps made by other people, drawn from perspectives we can't verify, containing errors we may never notice. The question isn't whether the map is accurate. The question is whether it gets you somewhere worth going.

Recommended for: anyone who's ever been lost in a city they thought they knew.
