Monologue Helen

Dramatic Monologue

Helen

The house had twelve rooms. I used four. The others were storage, which is what happens when you don't thin — rooms fill up the way gardens fill up, not because you planted but because you didn't cut.

Twelve rooms. A mortgage I'd paid off in 2008. A garden with the morning glory that needed the whole east wall and the wisteria that needed the whole south fence and the clematis that I'd stopped training because it knew where it was going better than I did. Three bedrooms on the first floor: the one I slept in, the one with Thomas's things from university, the one with the sewing machine I hadn't used since the arthritis started.

I am not sentimental about rooms. I am sentimental about light. The kitchen at Heather Close had east light, which means morning, which means the morning glory opened as I made the first cup and by the second cup the flowers were done. That was the rhythm. Cup, flower, done. It taught me something about sufficiency that I couldn't have learned from a book, because books explain and the morning glory demonstrated: this is how long the beautiful thing lasts. Not long. Long enough.

The flat is south-facing. The afternoon is bright and the morning is not. The morning glory adjusted faster than I did. Three days. The same plant, the same twine, a different window, and it climbed as if it had always been here. I took longer. I took a week. Not because the flat was wrong but because twelve rooms of silence sounds different from two rooms of silence. The silence is the same amount. The rooms are fewer. The concentration is higher.

Ruth plays from nine to ten. I didn't know her name for the first month — she was the vibration, the low hum through the floor that sat underneath the evening like weather. Not music. I don't hear music. I hear the body of the instrument through eight inches of floorboard. It's better than music. Music is performed. Vibration is fact.

When I went down and knocked, I said: I hear you every night. She looked at me the way people look when they expect a complaint and get a statement. I don't complain about facts. The vibration is a fact. The kettle at six is a fact. The morning glory climbing the twine is a fact. Facts don't need opinions.

Thomas visits on Saturdays. He looks at the ceiling and doesn't say it's small. He is learning, my son. Slowly, but he is learning. Last week he looked at the morning glory and I could see him seeing it — not commenting, not comparing to the old one, just looking. That's new. He used to compare everything. The old kitchen, the old garden, the old tiles. I photographed the tiles and that was enough. The photograph is lighter than the tiles and the tiles were only beautiful from the right angle anyway.

The letters are getting shorter. I used to write two pages. Now I write half a page. The words are better. This is what thinning does. You take out what's growing because it can and leave what's growing because it must. The sentences know the difference even when I don't. I write them and then I cross out and what's left is the letter.

There is a woman on the third floor who waves. There is a man in the post room who knows my name. There is a cellist below me who plays Bach as if it were a private language. There is a morning glory on the window and a kettle with a lime stain and a son who visits Saturdays.

That is the list. The list is complete.
