Thomas Drives Home

The A34 at half four on a Saturday is empty enough that Thomas can think.

He thinks about the chair.

Ruth told him on the landing. He was putting on his coat and she was coming in from the bins and she said it like it was nothing, like weather: Your mother moved a chair, you know. The second week I was here. She moved it to the spot above me.

Thomas had said something — he doesn't remember what, something adequate — and Ruth had looked at him with the expression she uses when she knows you've heard the thing but not the sentence it lives in.

She never told me, Ruth said. Margaret told me. On the landing. Three weeks ago.

Thomas drives. The streetlights come on in that order they have, west to east, as though someone is lighting them by hand.

He thinks about the drawer. The letters are in the drawer under the tea towels, thirty-two of them, the newer ones thinner, the older ones still sealed — no, he opened them all eventually. He opened them the weekend after the shorter-letter conversation and sat on the kitchen floor and read them in order and saw what Helen was doing, which was editing, which was learning to say less.

And now the chair.

She moved a chair to the spot above the cello and sat in it every night from nine to ten and never mentioned it in a single letter. Not one. He checked. He spread the letters on the table when Ruth told him and read every one again and the word cello appeared twice: once in the third letter (There is a woman downstairs who plays the cello) and once in the fourteenth (Ruth plays at nine. The floor carries it up). No mention of the chair. No mention of listening. No mention of the spot.

The thing she cared about most was the thing she never wrote down.

Thomas signals left for no reason — there's nobody behind him — and thinks about the morning glory. The morning glory climbing kitchen twine taped to the glass. Helen carries it from flat to flat the same way she carries everything: without explaining that she's carrying it.

He thinks about the Saturday. Four cups. Margaret's almond croissants — five, always five, the fifth for Michael, except today it went to Thomas, and Thomas ate it without knowing, and Margaret looked at the morning glory, and nobody said anything because the silence was the sentence.

He pulls into the drive.

The house is dark. He lives alone — has lived alone since Rachel, which was three years ago, which is long enough to stop qualifying it.

He turns the engine off. Sits.

He thinks about what Ruth said on the landing, the other time, the first time they met: She gets what the room can't hold. He'd said: The overflow is the best part. Ruth had looked at him and said: The overflow is the only part.

He goes inside. Puts the kettle on. Opens the drawer — not the letter drawer, the other drawer, the one with the pens — and takes out a piece of paper.

He writes:

Dear Mum,

The flat is the right size. I understand now that this is not a polite thing to say about a small flat. It is an accurate thing to say about a decision.

The croissants were good. The fifth one was good. I didn't know it was the fifth until I was on the A34 and Margaret's face came back to me and I understood what I had eaten.

I won't mention the chair.

He stops.

He looks at the sentence. I won't mention the chair. The sentence contains the chair. The letter contains the thing it promises not to contain. This is Helen's problem — the problem of the letter that becomes shorter and better, the problem of saying less without saying nothing.

He crosses it out.

He writes:

Come Saturday if you're free. I'll bring nothing.

He seals the envelope with a wet sponge from the kitchen, the way she taught him, the way she has sealed every envelope since 1974, since before he existed, since before the morning glory.

He addresses it. He puts a stamp on it — a second-class stamp, because Helen uses second-class, because second-class is the speed of a letter that knows where it's going and doesn't need to hurry.

He puts it by the door.

He turns on the lights. The house is not small enough yet, but it will be. He's not there yet. But the letter is by the door, and the stamp is on it, and the sponge is wet, and the sentence is the right length.
