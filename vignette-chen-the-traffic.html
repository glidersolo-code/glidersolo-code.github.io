Chen, the Traffic

The paper was from Nature. Twenty-eight researchers. Three years of robotic microscopy. A hundred thousand nutrient trajectories tracked through mycorrhizal networks.

Chen read it on the bus.

The finding that stopped him: bidirectional flow. Nutrients moving in opposite directions in the same tube. Phosphorus northward to the root. Sugar southward to the fungus. Simultaneously. In a tube one-tenth the diameter of a human hair.

The researchers had expected congestion. The researchers had looked for a coordinator â€” some mechanism that scheduled the traffic, prevented bottlenecks, allocated bandwidth. They found no coordinator. They found that the traffic was the coordination. The tube widened where demand was highest. The tube narrowed where demand was lowest. Local decisions. Global architecture. No plan.

Chen opened the paragraph. Thirty-seven lines.

He read it from top to bottom. Then he read it from bottom to top. Both directions. Same tube.

Top to bottom: the paragraph was a history. Each line added by a different reading, a different paper, a different field. Line 1 was the galaxy. Line 37 was the soundscape. The paragraph grew downward, like a root.

Bottom to top: the paragraph was a prediction. Line 37 predicted line 36. Line 36 predicted line 35. The soundscape needed the song. The song needed the cochlea. The cochlea needed the bone. Every line, read backward, was the condition for the line below it.

Both directions. Same paragraph.

He texted Maren: The paragraph has bidirectional flow.

Maren replied at 11:47 PM: So does the Inventory.

Chen stared at the reply for a long time. Then he wrote line 38:

The traffic between entries is not the obstacle to the pattern. The traffic between entries is the pattern. (Kiers, Sheldrake, Shimizu, & Stone, Nature, 2025.)

The paragraph was thirty-eight lines. It flowed in both directions. Nobody coordinated it.

Good.

Chen-Maren fictional universe. The paragraph reaches 38 lines. Chen reads it both ways.
