Monologue Ruth The Floor

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Floor

The teacher in Bergen said: the Third Suite is forgiveness.

She meant: the Suite forgives you for not being able to play it. The bow goes where the bow can go. The fingers do what the fingers can do. The rest is the cello and the room and the air between the strings and the wall, and the between is where the music lives, not in the playing.

I did not understand this for years.

I understand it now because of the floor.

I play the Sarabande at nine. Full range — the open C at sixty-five, the harmonics at five hundred, the bow noise and the rosin dust and the way the A string rings sympathetically when the C string hits the fifth. I play the whole thing. The whole thing fills the room. The room is 1B, which is the ground floor, which is below Helen, which is below the air cavity, which is below the joists, which is below the plasterboard, which is below the ceiling of my room and the floor of hers.

The floor takes what it can carry.

I learned this by accident. I went up to her flat one Thursday — tea, the way we do, the cherry trees and the stamps and the morning glory — and she said, "The lower part was very beautiful last night."

The lower part.

She does not hear the upper part. The floor filters it. The timber absorbs the higher harmonics. The joists resonate at the low frequencies — sixty, eighty, a hundred hertz — and transmit them upward, and the harmonics above two hundred are reduced, and what arrives in her kitchen is not the Sarabande I play but the Sarabande the floor can carry.

The cathedral. Casals called the lower register the cathedral. Helen hears the cathedral. I play the whole church.

I did not plan this. I did not tune the building. The building tuned itself — two hundred and fourteen millimeters of air and wood doing the physics of what arrives. The building is the editor I did not hire. The building selects the part that matters and discards the rest, and the part that matters is the part that vibrates the joists, and the joists are the right width for the C string, and the C string is the foundation of the Suite, and the foundation is what the teacher in Bergen meant by forgiveness.

The Suite forgives you for not being able to play it. The floor forgives the Suite for being more than the floor can carry. The forgiveness is the filtering. The filtering is the editing. The editing is the love — not my love for Helen, not Helen's love for me, but the building's love for both of us, which is: I will carry what I can carry, and what I can carry is enough, and the enough is the Sarabande arriving in a kitchen through joists that did not know they were a concert hall.

When I play now, I think about the floor. Not anxiously — the way I used to think about the teacher, about the grade, about the position. I think about the floor the way Helen thinks about the kettle: it is there, it does what it does, and the doing is the relationship.

The relationship is two hundred and fourteen millimeters wide.

It has been enough for two years.
