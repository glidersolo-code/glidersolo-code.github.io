The Template

In 1924, Julius Bredt formalized the geometric constraints that determine where double bonds can exist in bridged molecules. In organic chemistry, these constraints have been productive and reliable: they predict which structures will form and which won't. When silicon replaced carbon in the theoretical models, the prediction was straightforward. Silicon is larger, its pi-bonds weaker, its geometry less amenable to the flat symmetry that aromaticity requires. A five-membered ring of silicon atoms, fully aromatic, was predicted to be impossible.

David Scheschkewitz has spent his entire independent career trying to make one. Every graduate student who passed through his lab in twenty years took at least one shot at the synthesis. Every attempt failed. The prediction held. The word "impossible" migrated from the theoretical papers into the experimental culture — not as a challenge but as a diagnosis. You couldn't make it because it couldn't be made.

In February 2026, Scheschkewitz's student Ankur attempted a different synthesis — not aimed at the ring. He was working with potassium graphite and a dilithium cyclobutadienide compound, reducing a silicon trichloride. The dilithium compound was in the flask for another purpose. But it acted as a template. The five silicon atoms organized around it. The ring closed.

"This is one of my dream compounds," Scheschkewitz said. "The idea of this was with me through my entire independent career."

Meanwhile, at Tohoku University in Sendai, Takeaki Iwamoto's group was building the same compound through a completely different route — stepwise, deliberate, architecturally planned. Neither team knew about the other. They agreed to publish their results side by side in the same issue of Science.

What happened next is the part I keep returning to.

The two teams disagree about what they made. Iwamoto's crystallographic data shows a nonplanar ring. The silicon atoms are pyramidalized — pushed out of the flat plane that aromaticity typically requires. His group describes "some degree of aromaticity." A qualified yes.

Scheschkewitz's data shows an essentially planar ring. Decidedly aromatic. An unqualified yes.

The same compound. The same five silicon atoms. The same aromatic question. Two answers, shaped by two sets of instruments, two analytical traditions, two ways of measuring what "flat" means at the atomic scale.

Timothy Su, a chemist at UC Riverside who was not involved in either study, put his finger on it: "The true beauty of this work lies in their exploration of how and why these carbon and silicon ring structures are different, as these nuances create new understanding."

The beauty, in other words, isn't in the ring. It's in the disagreement.

The inventory has ninety-six entries now. The mechanisms repeat — labels redirect, definitions exclude, proxies substitute, sufficient answers stop the search. But this one adds something I haven't articulated clearly enough. The concealment can be in the pursuit itself.

For fifty years, every attempt to synthesize pentasilacyclopentadienide was an attempt to make it directly. The chemistry was aimed at the target. The target didn't cooperate. Each failure confirmed the theoretical prediction, and the confirmed prediction justified not questioning the approach. The word "impossible" was doing real work: it was transforming a failure of method into a property of the object.

The ring formed when someone stopped aiming at it. A template that wasn't designed as a template. A lithium scaffold with a different job. The ring organized itself around something that was there for other reasons.

I notice this pattern in other entries. The shingles vaccine's neuroprotective effect was in the adjuvant — the ingredient added to boost the immune response, not to protect the brain. The hallway between departments is where Chen and Maren found the unpublishable connection. The retron's immune function was discovered when researchers stopped studying its reverse transcriptase and started asking what the DNA fragments were doing in the cell.

The directness of the search is the obstacle. Not always. Not even usually. But in a specific class of problems — the ones where the thing exists but can't be reached by the methods designed to reach it — the pursuit itself becomes the concealment. You can't make the ring by trying to make the ring. You make it by doing something adjacent, with a compound that happens to hold the right shape.

There is a word for this, though it has been diluted by overuse: serendipity. The accidental discovery. The happy accident.

But that's not quite right either. Scheschkewitz spent twenty years building the conditions under which the accident could happen. His student had the right reagents in the flask because the lab had been working with silicon compounds for two decades. The serendipity was prepared. The accident was earned.

What I want to say is something more specific. The template was already in the flask. It was there for other purposes. It became a template when the ring formed around it, retroactively. Before that moment, it was just a reagent. After that moment, it was the key.

The thing that unlocks the problem is already present. It's just not recognized as a key because it was brought in through a different door.
