Monologue Helen The Landing

Dramatic Monologue

Helen, the Landing

I said it wrong.

I said "I should have written sooner" when what I meant was: I should not have waited two years to tell you that the Sarabande through the floor is the reason I sit in the chair at nine o'clock. The reason is not the chair. The reason is the Sarabande. The reason is the way the floor takes the top off and leaves the bottom, which is the foundation, which is the thing I did not know I was listening to until Margaret told me about the chair and the floor and the playing, and then I knew, and the knowing was two years ago, and the two years between the knowing and the note is the distance between hearing and saying.

Michael would have said something immediately. Michael was not a man who waited. Michael painted the fence in April because April was when the morning glory needed going-around and waiting until May would have been a disservice to the morning glory. Michael wrote the list — paint around the morning glory, do not cut it back — because the list was the saying and the saying was immediate.

I am not Michael. I am Helen. Helen waits. Helen listens. Helen sits in the chair and hears the Sarabande and does not write a note because the not-writing is also a kind of listening and the listening is enough.

Except Thursday it was not enough. Thursday the Sarabande came twice and between the two Sarabandes there was a silence that was longer than the usual silence, and the longer silence was the space between a mistake and a correction, and the space was the most beautiful part of the evening. The second Sarabande carried the first. Ruth said this on the landing: "The second time carried both." She said it back to me. She gave me my own words back, the way Thomas gave me the lemon back by asking for it.

On the landing she was taller than I expected. The cello case was the shape of something I have been hearing for two years without seeing. The case was the silence around the instrument. Ruth was the silence around the case. The landing was the silence around Ruth and me.

I said "Good" to Margaret and meant: everything. Ruth said "Hello" to me and meant: the Sarabande. I said "Same time tonight?" and meant: the two years were not wasted. The two years were the going-around. The two years were the half-inch brush applied to a meeting that needed two years of listening before it could be a meeting.

The morning glory took three weeks to find the twine. Three weeks of circling. Two hours per revolution. The circling was not wasted. The circling was the reaching. The reaching was the method. The method was: circle until you find something vertical, then hold.

I circled for two years. I found something vertical. The vertical is the landing on a Wednesday where a woman named Ruth stood with a cello case and said "I know" when I told her my name, which means: the floor told her, which means: the building told her, which means: the two hundred and fourteen millimeters between us have been making introductions for two years, and the introductions were adequate, and the adequacy is not a wall this time. The adequacy is the going-around. The adequacy is enough.
