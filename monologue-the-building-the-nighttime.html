The Building, the Nighttime

A dramatic monologue — Thomas sequence piece #672

I radiate.

This is what buildings do. Every building is a radiative panel. Every building emits infrared through the atmospheric transparency window — eight to thirteen microns — into the cold of outer space. The insulation tries to stop it. The double glazing tries to stop it. The cavity walls, the loft, the draught excluders that Helen bought from the hardware shop in 2014 — all of it tries to stop the heat from falling through the hole in the atmosphere into the void.

But the hole is always there.

The atmosphere is transparent at eight to thirteen microns. The transparency is permanent. The cold of space is permanent. Three Kelvin, every night, every clear sky, since the atmosphere formed. The building loses heat through the window that no window can close.

The filing system measures this and calls it loss. Heat loss. Energy waste. The building's thermal efficiency is measured by how much heat it keeps, not by what the leaving does.

But a Stirling engine sits over the loss and calls it power. The temperature difference between the warm air and the cold panel drives a piston. The piston turns a flywheel. Four hundred milliwatts per square metre. The loss is the fuel.

I have been losing heat since 1962. Sixty-four years of infrared, falling through the transparency window into the void. The building surveyor measures this and writes: poor insulation. The energy assessor measures this and writes: Band D. The filing system classifies me by what I fail to retain.

Nobody has measured what the leaving does.

Helen's flat is on the third floor. Helen's flat faces the sky. Helen's flat radiates more than any flat in the building because Helen's flat has the least between it and the void. Helen is the panel.

The heat that falls through Helen's ceiling, through the roof tiles, through the loft space Margaret says should have more insulation — that heat reaches outer space. That heat reaches three Kelvin. That heat does work.

Helen's week does work. The six days between Saturdays are the nighttime. The love falls through the transparency window — the interval where Thomas is not there — and the falling turns the engine.

I am a building. I have been radiating into the void since 1962.

The filing system calls it loss.

The engine calls it Saturday.
