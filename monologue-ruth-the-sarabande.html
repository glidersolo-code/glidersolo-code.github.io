Monologue Ruth The Sarabande

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Sarabande

I play it twice sometimes. The Sarabande. The second time is not a repetition — the second time is the first time landing.

The first time through, I am reading. The bow knows where to go but I am still translating — this note, then this note, then the weight that Bach put between the notes where the score shows nothing. The first time is architecture. I am building a room I have built before, but each time the walls go up in a slightly different order and the light comes through the windows at a slightly different angle because it is a different Tuesday and I am a different amount of tired.

The second time, I am not reading. The second time, the room is built and I am walking through it. The bow is slower. Not because I choose to play more slowly but because the room is larger than I remembered and there is no reason to hurry through a room you have already built. Hurrying the Sarabande is like hurrying a river. The river does not care about your schedule. The Sarabande does not care about the Gigue that comes after it.

Helen is upstairs. I know she listens. She has never said so. There are ways you know a person is listening that have nothing to do with what they say — the timing of the kettle, the way the floorboards settle when someone sits down, the absence of the television during the hour when the television is normally on. Helen's listening is structural. It is built into the evening the way the ceiling is built into the floor.

I moved here for the rent and the quiet. The rent is still good. The quiet turned out to be the wrong word. What I found is not quiet. What I found is a building where the sounds have manners — where the kettle waits for the bow, where the floorboards announce only what they mean, where the morning glory on the kitchen window upstairs leans toward something I cannot see but can sometimes hear in the way the light changes when Helen waters it.

Thomas is coming Saturday. Helen told me by not telling me — by the way she walked to the postbox on Thursday, which was three minutes instead of five, which means no stopping at the noticeboard, which means the letter was short, which means the letter said what it needed to say and nothing else.

I will play the Second Suite on Saturday evening. Not because Thomas is there but because Saturday evening is when the Second Suite happens. Thomas will hear it the way Helen hears it — through the floor, through the ceiling, through whatever medium turns sound into the kind of company that does not require an invitation.

The Sarabande is the third movement. The Prelude is the river. The Allemande is the walking. The Courante is the climbing. And the Sarabande is the arriving — the moment when the music stops going somewhere and starts being somewhere. Bach wrote it for a room he never saw, for a cello that may not have existed in its current form, for an evening that would happen three hundred years later in a flat where a woman plays it twice because the first time is the building and the second time is the living.

I do not know if this is what Bach intended. I do not think intention is the right word for what happens between a composer and a cellist separated by three centuries and a floor and a woman who listens by sitting down.

The second time is the one that matters. The second time is the one I play for her.

I have never told her that. I do not need to. The floor is telling her right now.
