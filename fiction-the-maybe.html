The Maybe

Part XXI: The Invitation

The maybe sat in the kitchen for three weeks.

Helen had said "Maybe" on a Saturday — the Saturday Thomas suggested inviting Ruth up for tea. The maybe was Helen's word: the conditional, the if-you-like, the half-inch brush applied to a suggestion until the suggestion was neither accepted nor refused but held. The holding was the going-around. The maybe went around and around the kitchen, touching the kettle and the table and the chair and the window and the morning glory and coming back to the same place every time: maybe.

Three weeks.

In three weeks the Sarabande came through the floor fourteen times. In three weeks the wolf tone came once — Ruth played the whole Suite again on a Wednesday, and the building carried it again, and the silence after was the larger silence, and Helen sat in the chair and heard everything including the wolf and the hearing was the going-around and the going-around was the maybe applied to a sound.

In three weeks Thomas wrote three letters. "The cold tea was good." "The biscuits were Margaret's." "The lemon is becoming a practice." Three sentences. Three Wednesdays. Three openings of the envelope at the right corner with Michael's knife. Each sentence smaller than the conversation it carried. Each sentence the Sarabande of a Saturday.

In three weeks Margaret visited three times. Tuesday tea. Tuesday biscuits. Tuesday conversation reduced to its essentials — one-syllable words carrying Saturdays, "Good" carrying a kitchen, "Oh" carrying a recognition.

In the third week, Helen stood at the window and watched the morning glory. February was ending. The vine was dormant — brown thread on Michael's wire, the going-around paused for winter, the reaching stopped until spring. But the vine was there. The vine had been there for thirty-seven years. The vine would be there in March, in April, in May, reaching for the twine, sweeping two-hour ellipses, the circumnutation that does not need to know it is circling.

Helen made a decision.

The decision was not the maybe becoming a yes. The decision was the maybe becoming the next thing — the maybe moving, the way the morning glory moves: not by deciding to move but by sweeping, by circling, by the going-around applied to a stem until the stem finds the wire and the finding is not a decision but an arrival.

Helen wrote a note.

The note was written on the same blue paper she had used for the first note — the note that said "I sit in the chair above you on Wednesday evenings and listen. The Sarabande is a cathedral. Thank you." The same paper. The same pen. The same practice — the going-around applied to a correspondence until the correspondence is a practice and the practice is the love.

The note said:

"If you would like to come up for tea on Wednesday, you would be welcome. I make the tea at the same time every evening. The kettle has a wobble. You may already know this.

Helen (upstairs)"

Helen read the note. The note was too long. Six sentences. Helen is a woman who uses two words where four hundred used to live. Six sentences was the four-inch brush.

She crossed out everything except: "Tea on Wednesday? You would be welcome. Helen (upstairs)"

Three sentences. The half-inch brush.

She crossed out another sentence: "Tea on Wednesday? Helen (upstairs)"

Two sentences. The going-around applied to an invitation until the invitation is the essential.

She folded the note. She put on her shoes. She walked downstairs — past the landing, two meters by one, the landing where Ruth and she had stood twice now, the landing that was becoming theirs the way the chair was becoming Thomas's and the fence was becoming Margaret's.

She put the note under Ruth's door.

She walked back upstairs. She put the kettle on. The kettle took three minutes. The whistle came. The wobble came.

The maybe was over. The invitation was under a door. The door would do the rest.

The building would do the rest.
