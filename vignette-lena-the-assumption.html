Lena, the Assumption

Lena is at the whiteboard again.

She has been at the whiteboard since Tuesday, which is not unusual — the whiteboard is where she works when the computer is telling her something she doesn't believe. The computer says: converged. The whiteboard says: check the basis.

The basis is the set of mathematical functions the calculation uses to represent the electron. If the basis is large enough, the representation is accurate. If the basis is small or poorly chosen, the calculation converges anyway — it finds the best answer available within the space it was given — and the best answer available within a limited space looks like a real answer. It has a number. It has error bars. It has convergence criteria that say: this is the answer.

She reads Maren's entry about the cave lions — the shared paragraph is open on her laptop, propped on the shelf where the dry-erase markers live — and she sees it immediately.

The geographic assumption was a basis set.

The set of available identifications for a large felid fossil in Japan contained: tiger, bear, unidentified. It did not contain: cave lion. Not because cave lions were explicitly excluded, but because the geographic literature had never placed Panthera spelaea in Japan, and the available categories were drawn from the geographic literature. The basis was defined by the prior knowledge. The prior knowledge did not include the correct function. The identification converged on the best available answer — tiger — and the convergence looked real.

She writes on the whiteboard, in green marker because the black is dry:

The basis defines the space. The space defines the answer. The answer looks converged. Convergence is not correctness.

She photographs the whiteboard with her phone and sends it to the group chat — the informal one, not the lab one. Maren responds in three minutes: That's entry #120 in four lines. Chen responds in twenty minutes: The fish also converges. Active is the answer when the basis only contains active and inactive.

Lena looks at her own calculation. The electron density around the catalytic site. The basis she chose last week — the one that produced the converged, published, error-barred answer — was drawn from the standard library. The standard library was built from benchmark calculations on small molecules. The catalytic site is not a small molecule. The basis is doing what the geographic assumption did: providing a complete-looking space that does not contain the function it needs.

She deletes the calculation and starts over with a larger basis.

The new calculation will take three days. The old one took four hours. The difference is the cost of including the right function. Four hours for the wrong answer. Three days for the question.

Chen-Maren novella. Lena connects the cave lion reading to basis set theory. The geographic assumption was a basis: a space that defines which answers are available.
