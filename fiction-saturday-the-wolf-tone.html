Saturday, the Wolf Tone

Thomas arrived at quarter to twelve. Helen opened the door. The kettle had just clicked.

"The cellist played the whole Suite last night," Helen said, before Thomas had taken off his coat.

Thomas hung his coat on the hook. The hook was the same hook. The coat was the same coat. The door was the same door that Helen opened every Saturday at quarter to twelve, which was not the time Thomas arrived but the time Helen expected him, which was earlier, which was the going-around applied to a clock.

"Including the wolf tone?"

"Including the wolf tone. I could not distinguish the wolf tone from the building."

"Is that good?"

"It is the building doing its job."

Thomas sat at the table. The lemon was on the chipped plate. He cut a slice without being asked — the asking had happened once, five Saturdays ago, and the once was enough, and the enough had become the practice.

"She played the whole thing," Helen said. "Not because I asked. I did ask. But she played it because the playing is the playing, and the building does the rest, and the rest is what arrives, and what arrives is what I hear, and what I hear is enough."

Thomas drank his tea. The tea was different — the tea had been different since the lemon — but the difference was the same difference every Saturday now, which meant the difference was becoming the same, which meant the practice was absorbing the change, which meant: enough.

"You should still invite her up," Thomas said.

"I know."

"You said maybe."

"I still say maybe. Maybe is not no."

"Maybe is the morning glory."

Helen looked at the morning glory. The morning glory was doing what the morning glory does in late February: growing. Not visibly — the growth was in the stem, in the cells, in the slow extension that would become a leaf in March and a flower in July and the going-around applied to glass all summer. The morning glory's maybe was three weeks. Helen's maybe was longer. Helen's maybe was: not yet, but the reaching is the reaching.

"Tell me about the wolf tone," Thomas said.

"I looked it up. A wolf tone is a frequency the cello produces when the body and the string resonate at the same pitch and the two vibrations interfere with each other."

"The cello disagreeing with itself."

"The cello disagreeing with itself. But the disagreement is not a mistake. The disagreement is the instrument's body being present in the sound. Most of the time the body supports the string. At the wolf frequency the body competes with the string. The result is a wobble."

"And the floor carries the wobble?"

"Or the floor filters the wobble. I do not know. I heard the Suite. The Suite sounded like the Suite. If the wolf was there, the building made it part of the building, and the building's frequency is the building's frequency, and the building's frequency is what I have been hearing for two years."

Thomas finished his tea. He looked at the morning glory, at the kettle, at the chair, at the chipped plate that now held a lemon slice with one bite of rind remaining. The kitchen was the same kitchen. The kitchen was always the same kitchen. The sameness was the practice and the practice was the going-around and the going-around was: the visit.

"Same time next week?" he said.

"If you like," she said.

He liked. He had always liked. The if-you-like was the going-around. The going-around was enough.
