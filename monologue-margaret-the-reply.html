Margaret, the Reply

Helen showed me the letter. Not showed — she left it on the table while she made the tea, and leaving is a form of showing when you have known someone for thirty years and the leaving is deliberate.

Four lines. Blue paper. Saturday is fine. The kettle is the same one. Bring nothing. I mean it.

Thomas wrote four lines with a crossed-out sentence. Helen wrote four lines with no crossed-out sentence. The difference between a son learning and a mother who has already learned is the difference between editing and not needing to edit. Helen did not cross anything out because Helen did not write anything she did not mean. This is not a talent. This is a practice. It takes fifty years to learn to write only what you mean, and most people never learn it because most people think the writing is the hard part, when the hard part is the knowing-what-you-mean, and Helen has known what she means since Michael died, and possibly before, and the possibly is Helen's own business.

I picked up the letter. Helen let me. She let me the way the building lets in light — not by opening a door but by having windows, which is structural, not performative. The blue paper was lighter than I expected. I have opinions about paper weight. This paper was too light for a letter of consequence, which made it exactly right for Helen, who would say that a letter of consequence does not need to announce itself with heavy paper, and she would be correct, and I would disagree, and the disagreement would last exactly as long as the tea.

She used the sponge. I could tell by the seal — smoother than a licked seal, more even, the kind of finish that comes from a tool rather than a tongue. Michael licked envelopes. I remember this. I remember a great deal about Michael because Michael was the kind of man who did small things in memorable ways — the crooked stamps, the half-inch brush going around the morning glory, the way he said "the horse doesn't mind" about the envelope glue, which I thought was funny and Helen thought was wrong and neither of us said anything because saying something about Michael's envelope technique was the kind of conversation that lasted longer than the envelopes.

Thomas is coming Saturday. He will bring nothing, because Helen told him to bring nothing, and Thomas is learning that "bring nothing" means bring nothing. This sounds simple. It is not. Most sons hear "bring nothing" and bring something — flowers, wine, a tool she mentioned needing. Thomas heard "bring nothing" and will bring nothing, and the nothing is the correct gift, and the correctness is Helen's doing, and Helen's doing is a lifetime of letters that say what they mean on paper that weighs less than an opinion.

I will not be there Saturday. Saturday is Thomas's. I have my own kitchen, my own windows, my own morning glory cutting that leans toward the fence. My cutting is smaller than Helen's. It will be smaller for a while. It came from a yogurt pot, from a woman who thinned her garden the way she thinned her letters, and the thinning produced a cutting, and the cutting is on my windowsill, and the windowsill is in my kitchen, and the kitchen has three windows and none of them show me what Helen's one window shows her, which is the top of a twine where a plant she carried from another life is growing toward something it cannot see.

I have opinions. I have always had opinions. My opinions about Helen's flat, about Ruth's playing, about the morning glory, about Thomas's letters — these opinions are mine. They are correct and they are irrelevant. Helen taught me the difference, not by telling me but by living in a flat I said was too small and making it exactly the right size, which is a unit of measurement I am still learning, which is Helen-enough, which is enough.

The letter is on the table. The tea is poured. Helen does not ask me what I think. She knows what I think. She does not need to hear it.

The not-needing is its own kind of trust. I am learning to live inside it.
