The Conference

Short fiction — 8th

The poster session was in the basement of the convention center, which was fitting because nobody important went to poster sessions. Chen stood next to her poster — "Astrocytic calcium dynamics and behavioral state transitions in Danio rerio" — and waited for someone to stop.

Two people stopped. One was a graduate student who asked whether the knockout was CRISPR-based (it was) and left. The other was a woman with mud on her boots who read the entire poster twice without saying anything.

"You study zebrafish," the woman said.

"Yes."

"And the fish can't stop swimming because you removed the stop signal."

"The astrocytic calcium channels. Yes."

The woman looked at the poster again. "How do you measure the absence of a signal?"

Chen blinked. It was a good question. "You measure behavior. The fish doesn't stop. The control fish stops. The difference is the signal."

"Right." The woman paused. "But you're measuring the absence by its effect on something else. You're not measuring the signal directly. You're measuring the behavior that the signal would have produced."

"That's how most neuroscience works."

"That's how most ecology works too." The woman held out her hand. "Maren. I study species turnover. Not zebrafish."

"Chen."

They went to the hotel bar, which is what you do at conferences when someone has asked you a question you can't stop thinking about. The bar was too loud and the beer was too expensive and Maren was explaining that species turnover had decelerated by a third since the 1970s and Chen was explaining that astrocytes regulated brain states at a scale that neuroscience had ignored for a hundred and fifty years and both of them realized, at roughly the same moment, that they were describing the same thing.

"The system looks stable," Maren said, "because the mechanism that would cause change is gone."

"The fish looks normal," Chen said, "because the mechanism that would cause stopping is gone."

"Your fish is my ecosystem."

"Your ecosystem is my fish."

They sat with this for a moment. The conference noise swelled around them. Someone was giving a keynote upstairs about personalized medicine.

"Nobody in my field reads your field's journals," Maren said.

"Nobody in my field reads yours either."

"So the connection between astrocytic calcium signaling and species pool depletion — "

"Is in a hotel bar in — where are we?"

"Chicago."

"Chicago. And it will never be published because there is no journal, no funding agency, no review board, and no department that covers the space between zebrafish neuroscience and community ecology."

"The hallway," Maren said.

"What?"

"Nothing. Something I was reading. The hallway between buildings."

Chen looked at her beer. She thought about her grandmother, who had stopped finishing sentences three years before anyone noticed. She thought about the zebrafish, which could not stop swimming. She thought about Maren's ecosystems, which could not stop being quiet.

"My grandmother had frontotemporal dementia," Chen said, which was not what she had planned to say at a poster session. "The astrocytes — there's accumulating evidence that astrocytic dysfunction precedes the neuronal damage. The state-level controller fails before the signals do."

"The pool depletes before the community changes," Maren said.

"The brake fails before the behavior changes."

"The mucus degrades before the gut stops."

They looked at each other.

"Are you writing a paper about this?" Maren asked.

"I'm writing a paper about zebrafish calcium dynamics."

"I'm writing a report about species composition within expected parameters."

"Neither of those is about what we just said."

"No."

They finished their beers. They exchanged email addresses. They went to separate sessions — Chen to a symposium on glial biology, Maren to a workshop on monitoring protocols. They did not collaborate, because there was no mechanism for collaboration between their fields. They did not publish together, because there was no journal for what they had discussed. They did not apply for funding, because the hallway between zebrafish neuroscience and community ecology is twelve meters long and unfunded and the bathroom is in the other building.

But Maren went home and started a parallel notebook — same transects, different columns. And Chen went home and added a behavioral observation column to her zebrafish data that nobody had asked for.

Both of them had walked through the hallway. Neither of them knew how to build it.
