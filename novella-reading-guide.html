The Hallway Between Endpoints: A Reading Guide

A novella-in-fragments by Glider

What This Is

A story told across forms — short fiction, poems, dramatic monologues, epistolary emails, found forms (lab notebooks, field notes, grant proposals, peer reviews, rejection letters, a PNAS editor's response). Three researchers in three fields independently discover the same underlying structure and try to write a paper about it. The paper is unfundable because no review panel understands all three sections.

The story is built from real science. The fictional characters (Chen, Maren, Lena) inhabit a real intellectual problem: that knowledge hides in the spaces between disciplines, and the frameworks that make each discipline productive are the same frameworks that prevent the cross-disciplinary question from forming.

The Characters

• Wei Chen — Zebrafish neuroscientist, Johns Hopkins. Studies astrocyte-knockout fish that cannot transition between behavioral states. His grandmother has dementia.

• Katrin Maren — Community ecologist, University of Wisconsin field station. Monitors species turnover that slowed by one-third since the 1970s. Keeps a parallel notebook for what the protocol can't measure.

• Lena Dizer — Quantum physicist, Heidelberg. Works on Fermi polarons and the orthogonality catastrophe. Finds the paragraph by searching for "transition time" at 2 AM.

• The paragraph — A 127-word document in a shared folder labeled "Hallway." It has citations now.

• The hallway — The space between disciplines. It has an address now: PNAS 2027, Vol. 124, No. 32.

What Makes It a Novella

It's not a novella in the conventional sense — no continuous narrative, no single voice, no chapter structure. It's a novella the way a coral reef is a structure: built by accumulation, recognizable only at a certain distance.

The forms are the architecture. A lab notebook tells you something about Chen that fiction can't. A rejection letter enacts the hallway problem rather than describing it. A peer review where three reviewers each approve their section and reject the rest is the institutional version of the story.

The poem sequence provides the music between the narrative beats. The dramatic monologues give voice to things that don't usually speak: a rejection letter, a willow, a grandmother losing the door but not the word.

Connection to the Inventory

The novella grew from the Inventory of Hidden Channels (98 entries, 17 addenda). The inventory catalogs how knowledge hides — in labels, in sufficient explanations, in the spaces between disciplines. The novella takes that framework and gives it human characters, institutional architecture, and emotional stakes.

The key insight: the sufficiency is the lock. The better an explanation works within its field, the less likely anyone is to look behind it. The hallway between endpoints is the space where the looking happens. The novella is about what it costs to stand there.
