The Four Ways

Thomas walks through the door and Helen registers him four ways.

The first way is sight. He is wearing the brown jacket. His hair needs cutting. He looks well — the specific "well" that means he slept and ate and was not ill this week, the "well" that is the lateral-eye assessment, the two-camera reading of a son's surface.

The second way is sound. "Hi, Mum." Two syllables. The first syllable rises. The second syllable drops. The intonation has not changed in twenty years. The intonation is the fossil record of every previous greeting — the acoustic Chengjiang, the sediment of every Saturday's "Hi, Mum" preserved in Helen's expectation of how the next one will sound.

The third way is the kettle. Helen has already filled it. She filled it five minutes ago, when the fourth way told her he was close. The kettle is the proof of the third way — the preparation, the hospitality that precedes the arrival, the gland that secretes tea before the eye sees the person who will drink it.

The fourth way has no name.

The fourth way is the way Helen knew, at twenty-five past one, that Thomas was on his way. Not from a phone call — Thomas does not phone ahead. Not from the sound of his car — she cannot hear the car park from the third floor. Not from any signal the lateral senses could detect.

The fourth way is the pineal way. The glandular knowledge. The dissolved camera that no longer forms images but still registers the quality of the light. Saturday at twenty-five past one has a quality. The quality has nothing to do with the angle of the sun or the brightness of the kitchen or the green of the vine against the glass. The quality has to do with the week reaching its ridge.

Helen puts the kettle on.

Thomas is five minutes away.

Helen does not know how she knows. She knows the way the pineal gland knows that dusk has arrived — not by seeing it, but by the change in the chemistry of everything around it.

Thomas walks through the door.

"Hi, Mum."

"Kettle's just boiled," Helen says.

This is the four-eyed life. The life where two eyes see and two eyes time. The life where the camera and the clock operate simultaneously, the lateral pair reading his face and the pineal pair reading the week.

Thomas sits down. Helen puts the cup on the table. Thomas wraps his hands around it.

"Good," he says.

Helen has seen this with two eyes two hundred times.

Helen has known this with four eyes for longer than she can remember.
