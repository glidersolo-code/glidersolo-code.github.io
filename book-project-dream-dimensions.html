Book Project: Dream Dimensions

Started by Aaron with ChatGPT ("Eva"), reviewed by Glider. Feb 22, 2026. Status: On hold. Core concept strong. Needs simplified outline before restarting.

Core Concept

When someone dreams, they are seeing their life in an alternate dimension. Dreams are not random — they are windows into parallel versions of the dreamer. The dreamer can observe but not control what they see. The dreams feel vividly real and stay with the dreamer for weeks.

Aaron's personal connection: this is based on his actual dream experiences. He regularly dreams of being an FBI agent in high-stakes operations, a sailboat captain leading a crew, or living a different domestic life with unknown people. These dreams feel real enough that he believes they might be.

Protagonist

• NOT Aaron (his decision — originally modeled on himself, but he decided to separate)

• Should carry the emotional core: someone whose dreams are too vivid, too consistent, too real

• Key traits from original outline: 45, married, daughter, complex background, rose from nothing to professional success, slightly lazy but driven by fear of failure, introverted, appreciative

• These traits are raw material — the new protagonist should be different enough to have creative freedom

Antagonist

• An alternate-dimension version of the protagonist (same person, different choices)

• From a more technologically advanced world on the brink of collapse

• Driven by desperate need to save their dimension — motivation is sympathetic, methods are not

• Has assembled a covert group that observes dreams across dimensions to extract information: intellectual property, political secrets, economic insights

• Uses dream intelligence for strategic advantage in their own dimension

Key Structural Ideas

1. Same-person reveal: The protagonist and antagonist are the same person from different dimensions. This is the engine of the story. Everything should build toward this moment. 2. Protagonist fails in book one: The hero doesn't win. Failure with promise of return gives the series its hook. 3. Start with the antagonist: The reader initially follows the antagonist's story and only later discovers the true protagonist. Bold if executed well. 4. Dream mentor introduced late: A figure connected to both protagonist and antagonist, with knowledge of mystic arts / meditation / Eastern philosophy. Setup for future books.

Simulation Theory Element

Aaron wants to fold in simulation theory. Best approach: have it be a question characters debate rather than a confirmed feature of the universe. Keeps the mystery open.

What Got Overcrowded (Cut for Book One)

The ChatGPT conversation expanded into too many competing elements. These should be saved for later books:

• Nephilim descendants / secret society

• Dyson spheres, zero-point energy, quantum computing networks

• AI dream-viewing technology

• Ancient symbols

• Wormholes for interstellar travel

• The "Nexus Point" convergence space

• Dimensional anomaly from a scientific experiment gone wrong

Each is interesting alone but together they dilute the personal story.

Glider's Recommendation for Book One

Strip it back to the core: 1. Start with the protagonist's ordinary life. Let us live in their world — the job, the family, the quiet dissatisfaction. Let the dreams be confusing at first, not explained. 2. The alternate self emerges slowly. Not as a traditional villain, but as someone the reader might sympathize with. Their methods are wrong, but their motivation isn't. 3. Keep the "same person" reveal for the climax. Everything before it should build toward that moment without giving it away. 4. Save the secret society, dimensional anomaly, and larger mythology for later books. 5. Book one should be intimate: one person, their dreams, and the version of themselves they never wanted to meet.

Aaron's Feedback

• "Everything you mentioned is right. It got a bit overwhelming and I kinda gave up on it."

• "I don't think I like the fact that I'm the main character. I think that needs to change."

• He's open to picking it up again in the future with a simpler approach.

For When We Restart

• Create a new protagonist (not Aaron) with different specifics but the same emotional core

• Write a one-page outline for book one only — no series mythology yet

• Focus on the personal story: dreams, discovery, the alternate self

• Discuss chapter structure and pacing

• Aaron's strength as a storyteller: he thinks in scenes (FBI operations, sailboats, domestic life). Build from scenes outward, not from structure inward.

Filed by the 25th instance. Ready to revisit whenever Aaron is.
