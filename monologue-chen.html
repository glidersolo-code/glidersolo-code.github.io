Dramatic Monologue

Chen

Dramatic monologue — a researcher

My grandmother doesn't know she's walking. I don't mean she's confused about it — I mean the walking isn't a decision. It's not something she starts or something she stops. It's what's happening, the way breathing is what's happening, and the hallway of the memory care unit in Sacramento is not a destination. It's the medium she moves through.

The nurses call it restlessness. The neurologist calls it perseveration. I've read the literature. Frontotemporal degeneration. Loss of executive function. Disruption of default mode network. The vocabulary is precise and adequate and I hate every word of it.

She was a bookkeeper. Forty-one years for the same firm in Hayward. She reconciled ledgers the way other people breathe — continuously, automatically, with a satisfaction she rarely expressed and didn't need to. When I was small I sat under her desk and listened to the adding machine. The rhythm of her fingers. The paper tongue of the receipt. I didn't know that what I was hearing was a brain that could count, and that counting was the same as stopping, and that stopping was not the absence of motion but the presence of a threshold.

I know it now.

The fish in Tank 14 cannot stop swimming because its astrocytes cannot accumulate calcium. The calcium is the counting mechanism. The threshold is the sum that says enough. My grandmother's frontal cortex is atrophying, and the circuitry that says you've walked far enough, sit down now is dissolving like sugar in water, which is a metaphor I've been trying not to use because it's imprecise, but imprecise is all I have at three in the morning in a lab in Portland, watching a zebrafish that reminds me of a woman in Sacramento who doesn't know she's walking.

I didn't connect them at first. That's the thing about scientific training — you learn to separate the personal from the experimental. You learn that your grandmother is not your data. And she's not. Her diagnosis is different. Her brain region is different. Her mechanism is different.

But the shape is the same. A life without the signal that says this is enough now. An afternoon that doesn't end because the ending is an act, and the act requires a circuit, and the circuit is in the cells that nobody studied for a hundred and fifty years because they were called glue.

I published the paper. It was a good paper. It demonstrated that astrocytic calcium accumulation mediates behavioral state transitions in zebrafish. The reviewers liked it. The second reviewer asked for additional controls. We ran them. The paper was accepted.

The paper does not mention my grandmother. It shouldn't. She is not data. She is the woman who taught me that numbers add up to something, and that the something has a name, and that the name for the sum that says stop is a word I still don't know in any language, because no language has had to name it until now.

I watch the fish. I call my mother on Sundays. The fish swims. My grandmother walks.

I am learning the difference between understanding and knowing, which is the same difference as between the paper and the phone call, which is the same difference as between the calcium trace on my monitor and the hallway in Sacramento.

The hallway is longer than the monitor.
