The Fence

Thomas and Margaret. Outside 14 Heather Close, now someone else's house. February.

THOMAS: You don't have to come with me.

MARGARET: I wanted to see the fence.

THOMAS: It's someone else's fence.

MARGARET: It was always someone else's fence. Michael built it.

THOMAS: Mum said he painted it every spring.

MARGARET: Every May. First Saturday. She'd bring him tea at eleven and he'd have done two posts and a rail and he'd be standing back looking at them like a man who had built something. Which he had. He'd built a fence.

THOMAS: It looks the same.

MARGARET: They haven't painted it.

THOMAS: Maybe they'll paint it in May.

MARGARET: They won't paint it in May.

THOMAS: How do you know?

MARGARET: Because the paint is peeling on the third post from the gate and the latch is brass-coloured, which means they've replaced the latch but not painted the post, and anyone who replaces a latch without painting the post next to it is not a person who paints fences in May.

THOMAS: That's â€” a lot to read from a latch.

MARGARET: Michael would have painted the post before he replaced the latch. He would have done it in that order without thinking about the order, because the order was obvious. The wood comes first. The hardware comes second. You don't dress a wound you haven't cleaned.

THOMAS: I didn't know you thought about him.

MARGARET: Of course I think about him. He was your mother's husband.

THOMAS: He was my father.

MARGARET: He was that too. But I knew him as your mother's husband first, and that's how I think about him, which is to say: as someone who belonged to Helen. The way the tiles belonged to Helen. The way the clematis belonged to Helen. Except Michael didn't belong to Helen, he chose to be near her, and that's different, and the fence is the proof.

THOMAS: How is the fence the proof?

MARGARET: Nobody builds a fence for themselves. A fence is a border. A border is for the people on both sides. Michael built the fence for the garden and the garden was Helen's and the fence was Michael's way of saying: I know where your thing ends and I am going to make the edge beautiful. The edge was the fence. The fence was the love letter.

THOMAS: Margaret.

MARGARET: What?

THOMAS: I don't think I've ever heard you talk like this.

MARGARET: I don't usually. There are opinions and then there are things that aren't opinions, and this isn't an opinion. Michael painted that fence every May because he loved your mother in a language your mother understood, which was the language of maintenance. Not gifts. Not words. Maintenance. The repeated act. The thing that doesn't look like love until you see what happens when it stops.

THOMAS: The paint is peeling.

MARGARET: The paint is peeling.

They stand at the gate for another minute. The clematis is dormant on the fence. It will flower in July. Three flowers, maybe four. Nobody will count them.

THOMAS: Do you want to go?

MARGARET: Not yet. I want to look at the third post.

THOMAS: It's just a post.

MARGARET: It's never just a post.

Dialogue #12. Thomas sequence, piece #37. Thomas and Margaret at the old house. The fence, the latch, and the language of maintenance.
