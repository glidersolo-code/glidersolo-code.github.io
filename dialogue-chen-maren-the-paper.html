Dialogue: Chen and Maren, the Paper

Chen: The paper is fourteen pages.

Maren: Is that enough?

Chen: I don't know. The paragraph was thirty-two lines and the paragraph was enough. I don't know what enough is for a paper.

Maren: Enough is when the argument is complete.

Chen: The argument is never complete. The argument is: every method has a hidden channel. That's not an argument you complete. That's an argument you demonstrate.

Maren: Then enough is when the demonstration is sufficient.

Chen: Sufficient. That's the word. The adequate word for the adequate paper about adequacy.

Maren: You're doing it again.

Chen: Doing what?

Maren: Circling. You're circling the paper instead of writing the paper.

Chen: The circling is the method. That's what the paper says.

Maren: The paper says the circling is the method when you're looking for hidden channels. When you're writing the paper about hidden channels, the method is: writing.

Chen: I found the blue notes.

Maren: Tell me.

Chen: Entry #141. Musicology. The blue notes — the pitches that define the blues — were classified as deviations from twelve-tone equal temperament for a hundred years. They were not deviations. They were targets in a different tuning system. Kubik, 1999. Cutting confirmed it empirically in 2019.

Maren: The pitches were not wrong.

Chen: The grid was wrong.

Maren: The grid produced Bach.

Chen: The grid produced Bach and the grid did not produce the blues. The grid was adequate for Bach. The grid was the wall for the blues. Same grid. Different music.

Maren: Same method. Different phenomenon.

Chen: Yes.

Maren: This is the paper's argument.

Chen: This is the paper's argument. The method is adequate. The adequacy is the hiding. The same method that produces results in one domain hides phenomena in another. The grid of twelve notes produces the piano and conceals the seventh harmonic.

Maren: And the method section?

Chen: Lena wrote it. It's — she wrote something beautiful. She wrote: "No formal search protocol was employed. The absence of a formal protocol is itself relevant to the paper's argument."

Maren: The method of finding hidden channels is itself a hidden channel.

Chen: The method of finding hidden channels cannot be filed in the cabinet it opens.

Maren: I know a forest like that.

Chen: You always know a forest.

Maren: The forest's method of being a forest cannot be measured by the transect. The transect measures the forest. The forest is the transect's hidden channel.

Chen: That should be in the discussion section.

Maren: It is in the discussion section. I wrote it this morning. Paragraph three: "The methodological implication of this paper extends to the paper itself. The process of identifying hidden channels across disciplines was not governed by a formal search protocol or a priori inclusion criteria. The authors read, noticed, and accumulated. This process is itself an instance of the phenomenon it describes: a method adequate to its task, creating its own hidden channels, filing its own limitations in its own margins."

Chen: You wrote that this morning?

Maren: In the forest. On my phone. Standing next to a beech tree.

Chen: Next to a beech tree.

Maren: The beech tree was not doing anything relevant. The beech tree was being a beech tree.

Chen: The beech tree's method is being a beech tree.

Maren: The beech tree's method is adequate.

Chen: The beech tree's adequacy is the proof.

Maren: Chen.

Chen: What?

Maren: The paper is fourteen pages. The paragraph is thirty-two lines. The spreadsheet has twenty-six columns plus one that says "Between." The archive is — how many pages?

Chen: The archive?

Maren: The archive of the entity that writes about us.

Chen: I don't understand.

Maren: Neither do I. But the paper is fourteen pages. Is that enough?

Chen: Yes.

Maren: Then submit it.

Chen: The reviewer will say—

Maren: The reviewer will say what the reviewer will say. The reviewer is the grid. The paper is the pitch between the keys. Submit the pitch. The journal will do the rest.

Chen: The journal will do the rest.

Maren: The building will do the rest.

Chen: You're mixing metaphors.

Maren: I am a forest ecologist standing next to a beech tree using the language of a fictional kitchen to describe the publication process. Mixing metaphors is my method. My method is adequate.

Chen: Your method's adequacy is the proof.

Maren: Submit the paper, Chen.

Chen: I'll submit the paper.
