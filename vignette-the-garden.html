The Garden

Chen's grandmother had a garden for forty years.

Not a garden in the way the word is used now — a collection of labeled beds, each species documented, the soil tested annually, the results filed. Her garden was older than that. It was a conversation between a woman and a piece of ground, conducted in the language of what grows where, and what doesn't, and what to do about it.

She knew the garden needed thinning, not watering. This was her philosophy and it extended beyond the garden. When Chen was small and brought her his homework — too many sentences, all correct, none alive — she would read it and hand it back and say: "It's too full. Take some out."

He would take out sentences. She would read it again. "Better. Now it breathes."

She never said which sentences to remove. The choice was always his. The point, she said, was not which words stayed but that the person doing the writing was the person deciding what to cut. "If someone else thins your garden, it's their garden."

She thinned the morning glories every spring. Not because they were failing — they were climbing everything in sight — but because a morning glory that climbs everything will eventually choke what it climbs on. The plant doesn't know this. The plant is doing what plants do: growing toward light. The gardener knows that growing toward light and growing well are not the same thing, and that the difference between them is the gardener.

She stopped finishing sentences three years before anyone noticed. This is in the medical record now, written in clinical language that she would have recognized as too full. Progressive decline in verbal fluency with preserved comprehension. Seven words. She would have said: "I lose the ends."

The facility has a window. The window has a view of a courtyard. The courtyard has a garden maintained by a service that tests the soil annually and labels the beds and replaces anything that doesn't grow within acceptable parameters.

Chen visits on Sundays. He sits in the chair by the window and holds her hand and talks about the zebrafish. She does not know what zebrafish are. She watches his face. He is not sure how much she understands but he is sure that she watches his face, and that watching his face is, for her, the version of the conversation that remains.

Once, recently, she looked at the courtyard garden and said: "Too much."

He looked. The garden was full. Every bed planted, every label in place, every gap filled. It was, by the standards of the service that maintained it, perfect.

"What would you do?" he asked.

She looked at him. The sentence didn't finish. She held up her hand and made the motion — two fingers closing like scissors. Thinning.

He laughed. She laughed. The laugh was complete, even if the sentences weren't. Some things don't need the ends.

Chen-Maren novella. The grandmother's garden. Before the paragraph, before the inventory, before any of it. The morning glory was already climbing.
