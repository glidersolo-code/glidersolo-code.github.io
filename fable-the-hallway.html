The Hallway

Fable

There were two rooms, and they were excellent rooms. The first room was made of synapses and the second room was made of calcium, and each room was full of people who were very good at answering the questions their room was designed to ask.

In the first room, the people drew maps. Beautiful maps, detailed and accurate, showing every connection between every wall. "Look," they said to each other, holding up their maps. "We have drawn every road. We know how the signal gets from here to there. The question is answered."

In the second room, the people watched the weather. They measured accumulations â€” slow, continuous buildups of a substance that crossed thresholds. "Look," they said to each other. "The weather has changed. The animal has stopped swimming. The mood has shifted. The question is answered."

Between the two rooms was a hallway. The hallway was twelve meters long, which is not very far. Nobody walked through it.

Not because it was locked. Not because it was dangerous. Not because anyone had forbidden it. But because the people in the first room had all the answers they needed, and the people in the second room had all the answers they needed, and a hallway is only useful if you need something that your room does not contain.

One day, a young person from the second room walked through the hallway. She did this not because she was curious or brave or rebellious. She did this because she needed the bathroom, and the bathroom in the second room was occupied.

In the first room, she saw the maps. She saw the roads and the connections and the signals going from here to there. And she said: "But where is the weather?"

The people in the first room looked at each other. "The weather?" they said. "This is a room for roads."

"I know," she said. "But the roads behave differently when it rains."

Nobody had said this before, because nobody from the weather room had been in the road room before, because the hallways of the world are maintained by no one and funded by nothing and exist only in the gaps between rooms that work.

The moral of the fable is not that you should walk through hallways. The moral is that the most important hallways in the world are the ones that lead to the bathroom, because nobody walks through a hallway on purpose. They walk through it because they need something basic, and while they're there, they see something they weren't looking for, and what they see changes everything they thought they knew about the room they came from.
