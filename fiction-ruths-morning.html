Ruth's Morning

Ruth wakes at six forty-five, which is ten minutes before the alarm, which is the body's way of saying it has absorbed the schedule and no longer requires instruction.

The flat is cold. The flat is always cold in February, which Ruth has solved not by adjusting the thermostat — the thermostat is Helen's landlord's decision, and the landlord's decision is fourteen degrees — but by sleeping in socks and a cardigan that she bought in Bergen when she was twenty-two and visiting a festival and did not know yet that she would spend the next eighteen years playing cello in cities that were not Bergen but that all had February in them.

She makes tea. Earl Grey, no milk. The kettle is quiet — a modern one, the kind that heats from the bottom and clicks off. Helen's kettle whistles. Ruth has heard it through the ceiling, and the whistle is the sound of another century's technology doing the same thing at a different volume, and the difference in volume is the difference between Helen and Ruth, which is a generation and a philosophy and a kettle.

She practices at nine. She has always practiced at nine. But between six forty-five and nine there is a gap that she fills differently depending on the season: in summer, she walks; in winter, she reads; in February, she sits with the tea and thinks about the Third Suite.

The Third Suite is the one she is always thinking about. The other five suites have things to teach her — the First is architecture, the Second is grief, the Fourth is an argument, the Fifth is a scordatura, the Sixth is a departure — but the Third is the one she returns to because the Third does something the others do not: it forgives. The Sarabande in the Third Suite is the only movement in all six suites that sounds like it has already heard your excuse and decided not to hold it against you.

She has never told Helen this. She has told Helen that the Third is her favourite, and Helen accepted this the way Helen accepts most information: completely, without requiring elaboration, the way a shelf accepts a book. Helen did not ask why. Helen's not asking why is the reason Ruth told her.

Upstairs, the ceiling creaks. Helen is awake. The creak is the floorboard by the kitchen window — the one that moves when Helen waters the morning glory, which means Helen is watering the morning glory, which means it is approximately seven-fifteen, which means Ruth's internal clock and Helen's morning ritual are synchronized in the way that two people who share a building without sharing a wall become synchronized: through the floor.

The floor is the relationship. Not all of it. But the load-bearing part. The floor carries the vibration of the cello upward and the creak of the floorboard downward, and both women receive a reduced version of the other's morning, and both prefer the reduced version, because the reduced version is the one that does not require conversation. The conversation happens on Thursdays. The floor happens every day.

Ruth finishes the tea. Puts the mug in the sink. Picks up the bow.

At nine, she will play the Prelude of the Third Suite. Helen will hear the vibration through the floor. The vibration will be the body of the instrument without the detail of the bowing, which is to say: the essential without the decorative, which is to say: the thing that matters without the thing that explains.

Helen calls it the best part.

Ruth calls it the floor.

They are both right.
