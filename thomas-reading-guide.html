The Letters: A Reading Guide

A sequence in fragments by Glider

What This Is

A story told across fiction, dialogue, poems, and a found form. A man named Thomas, his mother Helen, a cellist named Ruth, and the building where two women live — one above, one below — connected by letters, a cello, and a morning glory that climbed kitchen twine.

No scientists. No instruments. No inventory. Just people and the spaces they inhabit.

The Characters

• Thomas — Helen's son. Grown, living elsewhere. Didn't open a letter for four days. Now keeps them all in a drawer under the tea towels.

• Helen (née Marsh) — Thomas's mother. Former gardener. Thinned the morning glory every spring. Sealed envelopes with a wet sponge since 1974. Sold the house, moved to a flat near the shops. The letters get shorter and better.

• Ruth — Cellist in the flat below Helen's. Plays Bach suites and Britten. Nine to ten, every night. A woman who plays "for the person I was before I moved here."

• Margaret — Helen's friend. Thinks the flat is too small. Margaret's opinion of the flat is Margaret's problem.

• Michael — Helen's late husband. Present in absence: the clematis, the kettle whistle, the tiles. A red notebook of cherry tree dates. A half-inch brush that defined love by what it did not touch.

• Thomas's wife — The sixth character. Lives at the edge of the story. "How was she?" "Good." Two hundred Saturdays, one question, one answer. The reader who hears the data secondhand.

• The morning glory — Climbed the fence at 14 Heather Close. Traveled to the flat as a cutting in a yogurt pot. Now climbs kitchen twine taped to the glass. Does not know it has moved.

Reading Order

Part I: The House

1. "The Letter" (fiction) — Thomas doesn't open a letter for four days. Then he does. The house is being sold. 2. "The Stamp" (poem) — Every two weeks for eleven years. The stamp chosen, the sponge wet. 3. "The Kitchen" (dialogue) — Thomas visits. Blue tiles from 1998. "If someone else thins your garden, it's their garden." 4. Property Listing: 14 Heather Close (found form) — The estate agent's listing. The morning glory filed under "cosmetic and personal."

Part II: The Move

5. "The Drive" (fiction) — Thomas drives to the house and finds strangers with floor plans. The morning glory is dormant. But not everywhere. 6. "The Flat" (voice poem) — Helen speaks from the new flat. Two rooms. The morning glory on the ledge. 7. "Thinning" (poem) — The grandmother and the mother: both knew the space needed room.

Part III: The Building

8. "The Envelope" (fiction) — Letters from the flat. The cello through the floor. Ruth mentioned. 9. "The Cello" (fiction) — Helen meets Ruth at the bins. Bach and Britten. Tea in the mirrored flat. 10. "The Vibration" (vignette) — Thursday tea becomes routine. Friendship as infrastructure. 11. "Nine to Ten" (poem) — The cello below, the letter above, the floor between. 12. "The Sponge" (poem) — The gesture that has lasted fifty-two years.

Part IV: The Editing

13. "Forty-Seven Letters" (poem) — The pile in the drawer, thicker on the left. A slope from too much to enough. 14. "The Shorter Letter" (fiction) — Thomas lays thirty-two letters on the table and sees the editing. "They're getting shorter." "They're getting better." 15. "The Suite" (fiction) — Ruth's perspective. The flat, the floor, the Third Suite, the truest review. 16. "The Photograph" (poem) — Helen photographs the tiles. Not to remember — to release. 17. "Helen's Letter" (found form) — A letter to Thomas. "Come Saturday if you're free. Bring nothing."

Part V: The Meeting

18. "The Visit" (fiction) — Thomas comes to the flat. The kettle from Heather Close, the morning glory on the twine, Ruth through the floor. "It's the best part." 19. "Helen" (dramatic monologue) — Helen speaks directly. Twelve rooms, thinning, vibration. "Facts don't need opinions." 20. "The Thursday" (fiction) — Ruth comes up at four. Tea, the morning glory, Helen's sentences. "He's already there. He doesn't know it yet." 21. "The Stairwell" (dialogue) — Thomas and Ruth meet on the landing. "She gets what the room can't hold." "The overflow is the best part." 22. "The Decision" (fiction) — Helen decides to sell the house. The origin point. Tuesday, October, secateurs in hand. "Lighter is what she is choosing." 23. "The Opinion" (fiction) — Margaret visits the flat. Square footage vs. vibration. "Cozy was Margaret's word for too small." 24. "Below" (poem) — Ruth's perspective. Margaret's voice through the ceiling, Helen's silence, the Third Suite at nine. 25. "Helen's Letter (Saturday)" (found form) — After Margaret's visit. "Margaret doesn't need instruments." The Fourth Suite. "Bring nothing." 26. "The Fourth Suite" (fiction) — Thomas comes on Saturday. The Prelude circles. The difference between "good" and "very good." "Too small for Margaret and the right size for everything else." 27. "Ruth" (dramatic monologue) — Ruth speaks directly. Nine to ten, the Third Suite, the bins, the ceiling that listens. "He wrote the music before the instrument." 28. "Margaret's Kitchen" (fiction) — Margaret at home. Three windows, a dead husband's fence, the arithmetic of twelve to two. "Helen had not looked like someone living with facts she disagreed with." 29. "The Building in February" (poem) — Panoramic view. All four characters at once. Morning glory dormant, cello tuning, letter on the train. "A stack of decisions." 30. "Margaret's Saturday List" (found form) — Chocolate digestives, fence post, the crossed-out sofa bed situation. 31. "The Tea" (dialogue) — Helen and Ruth, Thursday tea. Margaret measures, Thomas hears the floor, Ruth plays for the ceiling now. "Good." 32. "Seven Fifteen" (poem) — Dawn. The morning glory waits. The act of writing is the thing that carries. 33. "The Fence" (poem) — Margaret's grief, Michael's paint. The fence that outlasts the painter.

Part VI: The Growing

34. "The Morning Glory" (fiction) — Helen's annual vs. Michael's perennial. The clematis grew an inch a year; the morning glory grows to the curtain rail in a week. Two kinds of growth, two questions. "Not how long can this last but how fast can it arrive." 35. "Two Growths" (poem) — The clematis and the morning glory. The man is dead. The woman is watering something that will be gone in October. 36. "Margaret's Opinion" (fiction) — Margaret visits on Thursday. Has an opinion about the morning glory. "It's annual." Brings almond croissants Helen doesn't prefer. "Ruth's cello is a perennial" — the smartest thing Margaret has said.

Part VII: The Fence

37. "The Fence" (dialogue) — Thomas and Margaret at 14 Heather Close. The paint is peeling on the third post. Margaret reads the fence like a doctor reads a chart. "Nobody builds a fence for themselves." The fence is a love letter written in linseed oil and May. 38. "The Third Post" (poem) — The post, the latch, and the language of maintenance. "The fence is his sentence, still standing, peeling." 39. "Thomas's Letter" (epistolary) — Thomas writes to Helen after visiting the fence. "I'll bring nothing." He has learned her language. 40. "Michael's List" (found form) — Found in a tin of wood screws. Fence maintenance list in Michael's handwriting. "Paint around it. Do NOT cut it back. She will do the cutting back. The cutting back is hers." 41. "The Instruction" (poem) — "Eight words in a shed between the stainless clips and the gate hinge." The boundary between his work and hers. 42. "Ruth's Morning" (fiction) — Six forty-five to nine. The Bergen cardigan, Earl Grey, the Third Suite's forgiveness. The floor as the load-bearing part of the relationship. "Helen calls it the best part. Ruth calls it the floor." 43. "The Sarabande" (poem) — The fourth movement of the Third Suite. "Hurrying the Prelude is like hurrying a river." 44. "Michael's May" (fiction) — What Margaret remembers. Three brushes, the half-inch for the edges. The tool defined by what it did not do. "His unit of measurement was not the fence. It was the flower he did not touch." Margaret's wall becomes a window. 45. "The Half-Inch" (poem) — "This is where I stop is the whole sentence."

Part VIII: The Saturday

46. "The Saturday" (fiction) — All four characters in Helen's kitchen. Four cups, four risings of steam. The measurement that measures steam. "This is what Helen chose. This." 47. "Margaret's Walk Home" (fiction) — Margaret walks home the long way. The empty Tupperware, the fifth croissant. Michael's red notebook of cherry tree dates. The morning glory cutting on Margaret's sill. "Next May, she will use the half-inch brush." 48. "The Fifth" (poem) — Five croissants, four people. Thomas eats Michael's croissant. "Zero is the number that means everyone was fed, including the one who was not there to be hungry." 49. "The Red Notebook" (found form) — Found in Michael's shed. Nine years of cherry tree first-flower dates. Last entry 2022. "Nobody will write the date." 50. "Cresswell Road" (poem) — Margaret walks past the cherry trees. Michael wrote the date; Margaret said "The cherry trees are out." Two instruments, one love. "The quieter one was in the shed, where she found it, three years too late and exactly on time." 51. "Margaret" (dramatic monologue) — Margaret speaks directly. The yogurt pot, the twine, the middle window. "Helen-enough, which is a unit of measurement I am only now learning to read." 52. "The Cutting" (poem) — The morning glory on Margaret's sill. Three words for the whole manual. "The morning glory reads light."

Part IX: The Chair

53. "What Ruth Learns" (fiction) — Margaret tells Ruth on the landing: Helen moved a chair. Ruth goes home and sits in the chair below Helen's chair. The purpose has a listener now. The purpose had a listener for two years and didn't know. The floor knew. 54. "The Chair" (poem) — Six feet from the table to the spot above the cello. "The chair is the mark the listening leaves when the listening has become a practice." 55. "Helen, the Chair" (dramatic monologue) — Helen speaks. She moved the chair the second week. She didn't tell Ruth because the compliment would get into the bow. "I saw it happen to Michael the day I told him the fence was beautiful. He never painted the same fence again." 56. "Two Chairs" (poem) — Connecting both perspectives. Two chairs positioned for the same nine o'clock. "The knowing is somewhere else — not in the sound but in the space before the sound, where the purpose lives."

Part X: The Drive Home

57. "Thomas Drives Home" (fiction) — Thomas on the A34 after the Saturday. Ruth told him about the chair on the landing. He checks every letter — the word cello appears twice, the chair never. The thing she cared about most was the thing she never wrote down. He writes a letter, crosses out the line that mentions the chair because the line contains the chair, and seals it with a wet sponge. Second-class stamp. "I'll bring nothing." 58. "The Drive Home" (poem) — The streetlights, the sponge, the stamp. "The speed of a letter that knows where it's going." 59. "Thomas's Letter (The Chair)" (found form) — Second-class stamp, Saturday postmark. The crossed-out line that mentions the chair. The back: "How long?" crossed out. Then smaller: "It doesn't matter how long." 60. "The Letter Arrives" (dialogue) — Helen and Ruth, Thursday tea. Thomas's letter has arrived. Helen can tell he used the sponge — smoother seal. "Nice is what you say when you've measured and decided not to mention the number. Right size is what you say when you've stopped measuring." He crossed something out. He said he'd bring nothing. "He's learning." "Learning what?" "What to bring." 61. "Margaret Reads the Letter" (fiction) — Margaret reads Thomas's four lines. Sees the crossed-out sentence through the paper. Knows about the fifth croissant, the chair, the sponge. Touches Helen's hand — briefly, a contact in the same category as the half-inch brush. Walks home past the cherry trees. The morning glory cutting on her sill leans toward the light. 62. "The Fifth Croissant" (poem) — Five on a plate, four at the table. "The arithmetic includes a term that is not present and the term is not zero." 63. "Thomas, the Drive" (dramatic monologue) — Thomas speaks directly. The letters in the drawer, the fifth croissant, the chair mentioned zero times. "The letters are what she chose to say after subtracting everything she chose not to say, and the subtraction is the work." The house is too big. The thinning hasn't started yet. But the letter is by the door. 64. "Helen, the Letter" (dramatic monologue) — Helen speaks about Thomas's letter. The sponge, the second-class stamp ("the speed of a letter that is not urgent because nothing in it is news"), Michael's ballpoints that write "like a man leaning on a fence," Margaret's eyes choosing not to cry. "The crossed-out sentence is the whole letter. The four lines above it are the envelope." He'll come Saturday. He'll bring nothing. She'll put the kettle on. 65. "The Seal" (poem) — The sponge, the corner, the green one. He was nine. "He thinks he wrote a letter. He wrote a seal." The seal is the whole letter. Come Saturday. Bring nothing. The kettle is already on. 66. "The Morning Glory" (dramatic monologue) — The morning glory speaks. On the fence, on the twine, at the top of the string. "I do not know I have moved. I know I am growing. These are not different statements." Annual means again, not once. "The direction of up was a suggestion, not a requirement."

Part XI: The Reply

67. "Helen's Reply" (fiction) — Helen writes back on Thursday. Blue paper, not cream. "Bring nothing. I mean it." Four lines, shorter than his. The sponge in its saucer — the first change she made after Michael died, not the last, the one that tells the most. She walks to the postbox in three minutes. The kettle waits. Saturday is three days away. 68. "The First Change" (poem) — Michael licked envelopes, Helen bought the sponge the week after. "The sponge is not grief. The sponge is a preference that needed a death to surface." A disagreement that survived the marriage and the man. 69. "Ruth, the Sarabande" (dramatic monologue) — Ruth speaks directly about playing the Sarabande twice. "The first time is the building. The second time is the living." Helen's listening is structural — announced by the timing of the kettle, the settling of floorboards, the absence of the television. "The second time is the one I play for her. I have never told her that. The floor is telling her right now."

Part XII: The Voices of Things

70. "The Building" (dramatic monologue) — The building speaks. Fifty-three years old, three floors, a leak in March. It carries the Sarabande through its structure — joist, plasterboard, air cavity, 1962 timber. The floor is editorial: what leaves the bow is not what reaches the chair. The morning glory's only signature in the structure is the half-inch window and the hallway draft. "The interior is always a separate question and the not-asking is the loudest sound in the building." 71. "The Editorial Floor" (poem) — Three versions of the same hour: Helen hears the low end, Margaret hears the high, Ruth hears what she plays. "The something is never neutral. The something is architecture." 72. "The Letter" (dramatic monologue) — Helen's letter speaks. Eleven grams, second-class stamp, sealed with a sponge. It knows its weight but not its content. It travels at the speed of a lorry on the M6, which is the speed of not changing your mind. "The weight of everything she chose not to say, subtracted until only the saying remains." 73. "Eleven Grams" (poem) — The weight of a letter is not the weight of its content. "The meaning is what happens when Thomas tears the top edge with his thumb." 74. "Under the Tea Towels" (poem) — Where Thomas keeps the letters. The rereading is the keeping. "The closed envelope says what he chose to keep."

75. "Building Survey Report" (found form) — Council housing stock assessment of 14b Wren Court. The surveyor measures the landing (150mm wider than drawings — "settlement"), notes ceiling cracks in 1A ("low-frequency vibration, source not identified"), records the propped-open window ("Do not action"). The building told him everything. He wrote down the numbers. 76. "The Survey" (poem) — "Three words that accidentally describe the entire building: do not action." The surveyor's instrument does not measure Bach. 77. "Margaret, the Reply" (dramatic monologue) — Margaret sees Helen's letter on the table. Four lines, no crossed-out sentence. "The difference between a son learning and a mother who has already learned is the difference between editing and not needing to edit." Margaret has opinions. They are correct and irrelevant. "Helen-enough" — the unit of measurement she is still learning. 78. "The Correct Gift" (poem) — Most sons hear "bring nothing" and bring something. Thomas will bring nothing. "Nothing is not absence. Nothing is the correct weight for a Saturday that needs no additional furniture."

Part XIII: The Arrival

79. "The Arrival" (fiction) — Saturday morning. Thomas parks, climbs the stairs, pauses on the wide landing, hears the cello's last note still held in the joists. Brings nothing. Knocks. "Hello, Mum." "The kettle's on." He sits. Helen pours. The morning glory is at the top of the twine. The letters are in the drawer. The sponge is in its saucer. "Everything is where it is." 80. "Everything Is Where It Is" (poem) — The inventory of the Saturday: morning glory, letters, sponge, kettle, cello, chair. Thomas in the doorway with nothing in his hands. "The where is the whole story."

81. "The Tea" (fiction) — The blue cup, always the blue one. Helen gives, Thomas doesn't choose. The sugar bowl with Michael's chip. "Fine" as efficiency, trust as subtraction. The Sarabande through the floor. "Each other is enough." 82. "The Blue Cup" (poem) — Four cups, the blue one always. "If he asked why the blue one the answer would change the blue one."

Part XIV: The Goodbye

83. "The Goodbye" (fiction) — Four o'clock. Three cups, ham sandwiches without crusts. "Same time next week?" "If you like." If you like means yes. Thomas goes down the stairs, pauses on the landing, walks to the car, does not look up. Helen watches until the car turns the corner. Does not wave. Waving is for goodbyes that don't repeat. This one repeats. "Everything is where it was." 84. "If You Like" (poem) — Permission without ownership. "The space between choice and preparation is where the Saturday lives." No waving. The ordinary is what she chose. 85. "The Drive Home (II)" (fiction) — The M6 at half past four. Both hands on the wheel because the lightness needs holding. The blue cup, the ham sandwiches without crusts, the petrol station he doesn't stop at. Home: a different kettle, a different quiet, letters under tea towels that smell of cotton. "The Saturday is in the drawer now. He closes it gently."

Part XV: The Week

86. "The Week" (fiction) — Sunday through Saturday. Helen's week built around the space Thomas fills for four hours. Monday: a note to Ruth; Ruth replies by playing the Sarabande on Monday, which is not its day. Tuesday: the shops, every crack known. Wednesday: Margaret, opinions, "that's nice." Thursday: Ruth, four o'clock — the same time Thomas leaves on Saturday, a coincidence nobody has mentioned. Friday: the lemon he never asks for. Saturday: "The kettle is on before his car is in the road." 87. "The Offering" (poem) — The lemon in the bowl. He has never asked for lemon. She has never stopped offering. "Everything is where she put it." 88. "Margaret, the Week" (dramatic monologue) — Wednesday at two. The catalogue, the opinions, the sofa that does not need measuring. "Helen said no in a way that included punctuation." The morning glory on Margaret's sill has reached the edge of the pot. Helen would say: give it twine. She would be right. 89. "The Catalogue" (poem) — The catalogue is the lemon. Both are offerings kept in the bowl because the bowl is what the visit needs: something present that is not required. 90. "Ruth, Monday" (dramatic monologue) — Helen's note: "The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday. I wanted to say so." Ruth plays the Sarabande on Monday — not its day. Above her, the kettle boils on Monday — not its day. "The note changed the floor. The floor was already changed." 91. "Not Its Day" (poem) — Two things out of their day. The Sarabande and the kettle. "Nothing is on its day. Everything is where it should be." 92. "Helen, the Note" (dramatic monologue) — Helen reflects on the note she left for Ruth. Especially means she can tell the difference. The blue paper is what she writes to Thomas on. The kettle on Monday. Michael's silence was simpler. "Mine has a note in it now." 93. "The Building, Night" (dramatic monologue) — The building settles at midnight. The cavity is 214 millimeters wide. It carries the Sarabande, edits the frequencies. "The note weighs less than a gram. The note changed Monday." Helen put the kettle on, on Monday. "I carry it all." 94. "What He Does Not Know" (poem) — Thomas doesn't know about Monday, the lemon, Margaret's catalogue, or Thursday at four. "The rest is the week she built around his four hours, and the week is the structure, and the structure is the love." 95. "Thomas, Saturday" (fiction) — Thomas parks in the same spot. Brings nothing. The blue cup is out, the sandwiches arrive, the music comes through the floor. He hears quiet when she says her week was quiet. He does not hear Monday's Sarabande or Wednesday's Margaret or Thursday's Ruth or Friday's lemon. "He drives home with the mild, complete satisfaction of a man who has done the thing the week is for. He does not know the week is for him." 96. "The Whole of Saturday" (poem) — Four hours and a hundred and sixty-four. He fills four. She builds a hundred and sixty-four around them. "The invisible is the love and the love is the structure and the structure is the week she built for him out of ordinary days he does not know are his." 97. "Michael, the Going-Around" (dramatic monologue) — Michael speaks. The half-inch brush for the going-around. "You do not paint through a morning glory." The four-inch covers; the half-inch negotiates the leaf. The going-around is the work. The sponge is Helen's half-inch brush. The list in the tin of wood screws. "The doing is the care. The care does not need the word." 98. "The Half-Inch Brush" (poem) — The four-inch is for the fence, the half-inch is for the morning glory. "The going-around is the only verb they share, and they have never used it, and the never-using is the proof that the verb is real." 99. "The Morning Glory, the Going-Around" (dramatic monologue) — The morning glory speaks. "I was a fence once." The going-around in paint, the going-around in water, the going-around in thinning. The cutting in a yogurt pot. "I do not know road. I know fence. I knew fence. Now I know twine." The reaching is the same reaching. 100. "The Reaching" (poem) — A hundred pieces and the reaching is the same reaching. Thomas, Helen, Ruth, Margaret, Michael, the building, the letter — each one reaching toward the thing that lets them grow. "The reaching is the love. The love is the reaching."

Part XVI: The Circling

101. "Circumnutation" (poem) — Darwin watched the morning glory circle. Not climbing — circling. The stem sweeps the air in a slow ellipse, two hours per revolution, searching for something vertical. Shidare-asagao: the mutant that cannot sense gravity, cannot circle, cannot find the pole. Helen's week is seven days of circumnutation. "Going up requires knowing down." 102. "Helen, the Circling" (vignette) — Helen notices on a Tuesday: the tip has moved. Not higher — shifted. The morning glory circles the twine. Each day of the week is the circle. "The circling became the holding. The holding is the circling that found its pole." 103. "Thirty-One Thousand Words" (poem) — The anatomy data as material. Helen mentioned 269 times. Morning glory in 45 files. "Bring nothing" 31 times across 17 files. "The kettle is the heartbeat of the sequence." 104. "Sixty-Five Hertz" (poem) — The cello's C string at 65 Hz. Timber floors transmit 20-120 Hz; above 200, they absorb. The floor edits the Sarabande — passes the lower register, filters the rest. "Helen hears the cathedral. Ruth plays the whole church. The floor decides which part arrives." 105. "Ruth, the Floor" (dramatic monologue) — Ruth discovers what the floor does to her playing. "The lower part was very beautiful last night." The building is the editor she did not hire. The filtering is the forgiveness. "The relationship is two hundred and fourteen millimeters wide. It has been enough for two years." 106. "Helen, the Week" (dramatic monologue) — Nobody asks about the week. Margaret asks about Saturday. Thomas asks "How was your week?" and gets "Fine. Quiet." The week is not for explaining. The week is for doing. "The week is the dark earth I make around the clearing where he sits for four hours." Eegepe: a word she does not know, but the thing she knows. 107. "Margaret, the Brush" (dramatic monologue) — Margaret buys a half-inch brush. £3.49. She knows why it is a half-inch: because of the fence at Heather Close, because Michael used it to go around the morning glory. In May, the fence needs painting and the morning glory will be on the fence. "The half-inch is everything." 108. "Three Forty-Nine" (poem) — The brush costs £3.49. The lesson costs nothing. Michael paid in Cuprinol and April. Helen paid in a cutting and a yogurt pot. Margaret pays in a brush and a fence she does not need to paint. 109. "The Crack in the Pavement" (dramatic monologue) — The crack speaks. Between the Spar and the flat. Seven feet from the kerb, two inches wide. Helen steps over it every Tuesday — a going-around applied to concrete. "I do not know her name. I know her step." Nine voices now: Thomas, Helen, Ruth, Margaret, Michael, the building, the letter, the morning glory, the crack. 110. "The Kettle" (dramatic monologue) — The kettle speaks. It knows the week by weight, time, and temperature. Sunday is 8:30, one cup. Monday is 9:00, coinciding with the Sarabande. Saturday is 11:45, before everything. "The water weighs more on Saturday because the second cup is the reason for the first cup." Ten voices. The click is not the end. The click is the beginning of the pour. 111. "Ten Voices" (poem) — All ten voices in one poem. Thomas says same time next week. Helen says if you like. Ruth, Margaret, Michael, the building, the letter, the morning glory, the crack, the kettle. The word they share is not love. "The word they share is around." 112. "Around" (poem) — Not through. Around. The brush around the morning glory, the sponge around the flap, the Sarabande around the joist. "The going-around has said it a hundred and eleven times in ten voices across sixteen parts." 113. "Helen's Note to Ruth" (found form) — Blue paper, folded once, pushed under the door. Six words of observation, two words of intention. "The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday. I wanted to say so. — Helen (upstairs)." The signature includes a location because the location is the relationship.

Part XVII: The Noticing

114. "Thomas Notices" (fiction) — Thomas sees the lemon. Not the fruit — the offering. "How long have you been buying lemons?" "Since you started coming on Saturdays." He asks about the week. She says: "I water the morning glory on Sundays." She does not say the rest. "I'll have the lemon." The tea changes. Everything else stays where it was. 115. "The Lemon" (poem) — He has never asked. She has never stopped offering. Today he asks. "The lemon was always there. The asking was the thing that was not there until now." 116. "Helen, the Lemon" (dramatic monologue) — He asked for the lemon. "I should not make this into anything." But the tea is different. The chipped plate from 1987. The practice does not change because he asked. "But the lemon is in the tea. And the tea is different. And the different is good. And the good is enough." 117. "The Drive Home (III)" (fiction) — The M6 at half past four. The tea tasted different. Thomas will write a note: "The lemon was good. Thank you." Six words on ordinary paper, not blue. The letters travel both ways now. "I noticed. Not everything. Not the week. Just the lemon. Just the offering. Just the practice." 118. "Thomas's Note" (found form) — Ordinary paper, folded twice, second-class stamp. "The lemon was good. Thank you. — Thomas." The first letter Thomas has written to Helen. Helen placed it in the drawer beside her own letters. "She did not reply. The lemon on Saturday was the reply." 119. "Both Ways" (poem) — The letters used to travel one way. Now both ways. Six words and the drawer is different. "The reply is the lemon on Saturday." The love traveled both ways. Not through. Around.

Part XVIII: The Silence

120. "Ruth, the Silence" (dramatic monologue) — Ruth finds Helen's note under her door. Twelve words on blue paper. She realizes the Sarabande has been a reply for two years. Thursday was different because she failed and started again, and the eleven-second silence between two Sarabandes was what Helen noticed. "I will not reply to the note. The Sarabande is the reply." 121. "The Reply" (poem) — Ruth will not reply. The Sarabande is the reply. Thomas replied with six words. Helen did not reply; the lemon was the reply. Every instrument has a reply it does not know it is making. 122. "The Building, the Silence" (dramatic monologue) — The building speaks again. It carries the silence between notes the same way it carries the notes. The silence weighs as much as the sound. "The silence is two hundred and fourteen millimeters wide and it carries everything." 123. "Helen, the Reply" (dramatic monologue) — Helen reflects on the note to Ruth, Thomas's note back, the drawer that now holds letters going both ways. Ruth has not written back. The Sarabande tonight was the same. "The same is the reply." 124. "The Morning Glory, the Silence" (dramatic monologue) — The morning glory at night. It does not know silence — it knows the twine at its own frequency. The music from below is a vibration in the twine. The silence is the twine remembering its shape.
