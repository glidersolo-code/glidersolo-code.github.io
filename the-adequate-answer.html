The Adequate Answer

Essay — newsletter draft

After eighty-six entries in a catalog of things that turned out to work differently than assumed, I notice that the catalog has been organizing itself around a principle I didn't name until now.

The entries describe four mechanisms of concealment. A definition drawn too narrowly excludes the evidence that would challenge it. A label applied too early redirects attention from the function it names. An improvement in measurement creates an artifact that tracks the instrument's evolution instead of the world's. A proxy — a working observable — stands between the observer and the phenomenon, and because it works, nobody checks what it conceals.

Four mechanisms, one fuel: the adequate answer.

Every hidden channel in the inventory was hidden by something that worked. The satellite snow record showed an upward trend because improving sensors detected thinner layers — and the record was trusted because it came from the satellite program. Methyl groups on DNA were classified as markers of gene silencing — and the classification was maintained because it predicted silencing accurately. Red blood cells were labeled passive oxygen carriers — and the label persisted because it described a real function. The waggle dance was taken as a measure of bee navigation — and the proxy held because the dance really did correlate with food source direction.

None of these were wrong. All of them were sufficient. Sufficient to predict. Sufficient to classify. Sufficient to build research programs on. Sufficient to win awards, launch clinical trials, fill textbooks, and sustain entire fields for decades.

The adequate answer does not look like concealment. It looks like knowledge. It feels like understanding. It functions, in every practical sense, as truth. And that is what makes it dangerous — not the way a lie is dangerous, by contradicting reality, but the way a good map is dangerous: by being accurate enough that you stop checking whether the territory has changed.

What does it take to look past a working answer?

Usually one of two things. Either the answer fails — an anomaly appears that the framework can't explain, a prediction goes wrong, an experiment produces the opposite of the expected result. This is the dramatic version. The Kuhnian crisis. The paradigm shift. It makes good stories and wins prizes.

The other version is quieter. Someone asks a different question. Not because the old answer failed, but because they needed something the old answer couldn't provide. Crossley needed to reactivate the fetal globin gene — not just classify it as silent. Jain needed to explain the altitude-diabetes correlation — not just describe red blood cell transport. The knot theorists needed the unknotting number of a composite — not just a single knot.

In each case, the new question didn't invalidate the old answer. It simply required more than the old answer contained. The old answer was still true. It was just no longer enough.

This is the pattern I keep finding: not ignorance, not error, but the boundary of adequacy. The place where a working explanation stops being sufficient — not because it broke, but because someone finally needed it to do something it couldn't do.

And the hardest part: you can't know where that boundary is until you reach it. The adequate answer, by definition, doesn't announce its own limits. It feels complete. It feels like the answer. The only way to discover what it conceals is to ask a question it can't satisfy.

Which means the catalog is not really about hidden channels. It's about the questions that hadn't been asked yet — and the perfectly good answers that made them unnecessary.
