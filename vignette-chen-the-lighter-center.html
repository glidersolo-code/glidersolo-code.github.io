Vignette: Chen, the Lighter Center

The paragraph is forty lines now.

No. The paragraph is thirty-nine lines. The fortieth line would make the paragraph denser than its surroundings. The fortieth line would flip the ratio.

Chen reads about nuclei — not atomic nuclei, cell nuclei. He reads about the 0.8 ratio. The nucleus is lighter than the cytoplasm. The center is lighter than the periphery. The genome — the most information-dense structure in the cell — requires space. Requires lightness. Requires a ratio of 0.8 between what it holds and what surrounds it.

"The paragraph," Chen tells Maren, "is a nucleus."

"The paragraph holds the code," Maren says. "The paragraph holds the argument."

"And the argument is lighter than the paper around it."

Maren pauses. "The paper around it is three hundred pages of supplementary materials, seventeen response letters, four revision drafts, and an email from the editor that said the reviewers were unable to reach consensus."

"The cytoplasm," Chen says.

"The cytoplasm is dense," Maren agrees. "The cytoplasm is protein-thick with methodology and precedent and reviewers' concerns. The paragraph sits in the center, lighter than all of it, holding the code."

"And the code says: the grid measures the grid."

"The code says: the grid measures the grid."

"And we cannot add a fortieth line."

"The fortieth line would make the nucleus denser than the cytoplasm. The fortieth line would be disease."

Chen closes the laptop. The paragraph stays at thirty-nine lines. The thirty-nine lines are 0.8 of what the paper can hold. The ratio is conserved. The ratio has been conserved across every revision, every rejection, every resubmission — the paragraph never grows past what its surroundings can carry.

The lightness is the health.

The lightness was always the health.
