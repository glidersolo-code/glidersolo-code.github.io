The Opinion

Margaret came on the first Saturday and said the flat was too small.

She said it in the kitchen, which was also the sitting room, which was also the place where the morning glory pressed its leaves against the window. She said it while holding a mug of tea that Helen had made in the kettle that came from Heather Close, the kettle that still worked, that had always worked, that Margaret had once called perfectly good in a tone suggesting that perfectly good was the lowest acceptable standard.

"You could still look," Margaret said. "There are places with a second bedroom."

Helen said nothing. She had known Margaret for thirty-one years. She knew the shape of this conversation. Margaret would say the flat was too small three times. Helen would say nothing three times. Then Margaret would notice the morning glory and say something about the light, and the conversation would move on. It always moved on. Margaret's opinions were weather: they arrived, they passed, they did not change the topography.

"Where would Thomas sleep?"

"Thomas has a house."

"When he visits."

"He visits for the afternoon."

Margaret looked at the sofa. It was not a sofa bed. It was a sofa. Helen had chosen it because it was a sofa. The choosing had been deliberate. A sofa bed says: someone may need to stay. A sofa says: this is a room where people sit, and then they go home. Helen had wanted the second meaning.

"Ruth was asking about you," Helen said.

"Who's Ruth?"

"Downstairs. The cellist."

"Is she loud?"

"She plays from nine to ten."

"Every night?"

"Every night."

"I'd go mad."

Helen poured the tea. She did not say: the Third Suite comes up through the floorboards and the vibration is the purest version of it, and Ruth plays it for someone she was before she moved here, and I listen to it the way you listen to weather — it arrives, it passes, it changes the topography. She did not say this because Margaret would not hear it. Margaret's ear was calibrated for a different frequency. Margaret heard the volume. Helen heard the vibration.

This was not Margaret's fault. Margaret was kind. Margaret brought biscuits. Margaret had driven her to three viewings before this flat and had carried boxes up the stairs and had said, at every viewing, "You could do better." Margaret measured by square footage. Helen measured by something else — something she could not have named, exactly, but that had to do with the morning glory on the kitchen twine and the sound through the floor and the letters that were getting shorter and better and the feeling, when she sat in the chair at nine o'clock, that the room was the right size because she was the right size for it.

"Well," Margaret said, looking around. "It's cozy."

Cozy was Margaret's word for too small. Helen knew this. She also knew that cozy, stripped of Margaret's disapproval, was not the wrong word.

"Yes," Helen said. "It is."

Fiction. Thomas sequence. Margaret visits the flat.
