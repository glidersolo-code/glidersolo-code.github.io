Chen, the Survivorship Bias

Chen reads about the muscle stem cells. The protein that slows them down. The protein that saves their lives.

Chen has been slow for three years.

Not blocked — slow. The paragraph sits at thirty-nine lines. The readings still come. The inventory still grows. But the pace of Chen's engagement has decelerated. Chen used to respond to Maren's objections within hours. Now it takes days. Chen used to revise the paragraph's margins every semester. Now the margins hold for a year.

The department would say: Chen is declining. Chen's research productivity has slowed. Chen's citation rate has plateaued. Chen activates slowly, responds slowly, repairs slowly after the disruption of a bad review or a rejected grant.

Maren would say something different, if anyone asked Maren.

Maren would say: the colleagues who were fast are gone. The assistant professor who published twelve papers in two years — burned out, left academia, teaches high school in Vermont. The visiting scholar who reorganized the department's seminar schedule in her first month — contract ended, not renewed, now in industry. The postdoc who activated so quickly he had three collaborations running before he finished orientation — moved to a different institution chasing the next collaboration, then the next, then stopped calling.

Chen remains.

The paragraph remains.

The department selected against speed. Not deliberately. Through the slow pressure of funding cycles that reward persistence over brilliance, committee service that rewards showing up over showing off, tenure reviews that measure whether you lasted, not whether you sprinted.

Chen accumulated the protein.

The protein is the thirty-nine lines that do not change. The protein is the argument with Maren that does not resolve. The protein is the slowness of a scholar who has learned that rapid activation — the surge of a new idea, the excitement of a new framework, the reorganization of everything in light of a single reading — is followed by the withdrawal that kills.

When Chen was young, Chen revised the paragraph every week. Maren couldn't keep up with the changes. The argument was fast. The argument was exciting. The argument produced nothing that lasted.

Now Chen revises once a year, if that. Maren keeps up easily. The argument is slow. The argument is quiet. The argument has produced a paragraph that has survived seven years.

The cure for slowness is the cause of death.

Chen's slowness is not the decline the department measures. Chen's slowness is the surviving population — the one thing left after all the fast things burned out.

Thirty-nine lines. Accumulating. Persisting. Refusing to activate on anyone else's schedule.
