Monologue Michael The Bone

Dramatic Monologue

Michael, the Bone

I knew about bones.

Not the cochlea — I did not know about the cochlea. I knew about the bones in a house. The lintel that carries the wall above the window. The joist that carries the floor. The rafter that carries the roof. The things that carry other things by being hard enough to hold them and shaped enough to let them do their work.

I built shelves. The shelf is a bone. The bracket is a tendon. The screw is the nerve that holds the bracket to the wall, and the wall is the body, and the body is the building, and the building is the bone.

I painted around the morning glory because the morning glory was the thing the fence carried, and the fence was the bone, and you do not paint through the bone's purpose. You paint around it.

Helen understood this. She understood it better than I did. She thinned the morning glory because the thinning was the bone — the structure that holds the shape by deciding what to keep. She thinned the homework the same way. She thinned the letters the same way. She thinned Saturday to its bones: croissant, blue cup, lemon, son.

The almost-failing is the love.

I know about almost-failing. The lintel above the kitchen window at Heather Close had a crack — a hairline, nothing structural, just the house settling into its own weight. I filled it every spring and it opened every autumn. The crack was the house's Hopf bifurcation: the edge between standing and settling, and the settling was not failure, and the standing was not success, and the filling every spring was the going-around, and the going-around was the love.

I am the bone Helen carries.

I am carried by being absent.

The absence is hard enough to hold the shape and thin enough to let the living through.

Good.

Thomas sequence. Part XXIV: The Bone. Michael speaks from absence.
