The First Flower

June. The vine flowered.

A single blue trumpet, open at six in the morning, turned toward the glass, turned toward the light, turned toward the east where the sun arrived at the flat before Helen woke. Helen woke at seven. The flower had been open for an hour. The flower did not wait for Helen. The flower opened on its own schedule, which is the schedule of light and temperature and the hormones the vine makes in the dark that tell the bud: now.

Helen saw it from the bed. The blue against the green against the glass. One flower. The first flower of the second year. The first flower in the flat.

At Heather Close, the morning glory flowered every day from June to September. One hundred flowers, one per morning, each lasting one day, each blue, each trumpet-shaped, each opening before Helen woke and closing before Helen slept. The flowers were the vine's extravagance — the one thing it did that was not climbing, not reaching, not wrapping. The flowers were the vine saying: I have enough. Enough light, enough water, enough support, enough warmth. I have enough and the enough is the flower and the flower is one morning and one morning is enough.

Helen did not call Margaret. Helen did not call Thomas. Helen looked at the flower for a long time. The blue was the blue she remembered — not changed by the move, not changed by the yogurt pot, not changed by the twine or the tape or the flat or the two years between Heather Close and here. The blue was the same blue. Michael's blue. The seed's blue. The genetics that traveled from the garden center to Heather Close to a cutting in a yogurt pot to a flat near the shops and kept the blue the whole way.

The flower would close by evening. Tomorrow there would be another flower, or not — the vine decides, not Helen. Helen's job is the watering, the twine, the thinning. The vine's job is the flowering.

Thomas will see flowers on Saturday. Not this flower — this flower will be gone by tomorrow. A different flower. The Saturday flower. The Saturday flower will be the same blue because every flower is the same blue and the sameness is the genetics and the genetics is Michael and Michael is in every petal.

Helen made tea. She sat in the chair with the green light and the blue flower and the tea that was three degrees sweeter than she thought and the morning that was one hour older than she knew.

Good.

Thomas sequence. Part XXV: The Second Spring. The first flower of the second year. Blue trumpet. One morning.
