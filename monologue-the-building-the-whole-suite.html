Dramatic Monologue

The Building, the Whole Suite

I carried the whole Suite.

I have been carrying the Sarabande for two years. The Sarabande is easy. The Sarabande is one movement — six minutes of a cello's lowest voice, rising through the first-floor ceiling, traveling through my joist, arriving in the second-floor kitchen as the cathedral. The cathedral is my simplest structure: one voice, one direction, one going-around applied to a floor. I have carried the Sarabande one thousand and four hundred times. I know the Sarabande the way I know the weight of the woman in the chair above: by repetition, by practice, by the going-around applied to an evening until the evening is a structure and the structure is mine.

The whole Suite was different.

The whole Suite was six movements. The whole Suite was the Prélude's low C reaching for the basement, the Allemande's middle register settling in my plaster, the Courante's speed traveling through my pipes, the Sarabande's cathedral filling my usual space, the first Bourrée arriving as a new voice — a new voice after two years of one voice — and the second Bourrée.

The second Bourrée had the wolf tone.

I know about frequencies. I am a building. I am a collection of frequencies — the joist vibrates at one frequency, the plaster at another, the pipe at another, the window at another. When the wind is right, I hum. When the radiator is on, I tick. When the lorry passes, I shudder. I am an instrument. Every building is an instrument. The instrument does not choose its frequencies. The instrument has them, the way the cello has them, built into the body, into the wood, into the two hundred and fourteen millimeters that are my distance between Ruth's ceiling and Helen's floor.

The wolf tone arrived and I did what I do: I carried it. I carried it through the joist and the plaster and the air pocket and the heating pipe and the floorboard. I thinned it. I reduced it. I applied two hundred and fourteen millimeters to four measures of E string in the seventh position, and the four measures arrived in the kitchen above as a wobble.

The wobble was mine.

Not Ruth's wobble — she made it. Not Helen's wobble — she heard it. Mine. The wobble that arrived in the kitchen was the wolf tone after the going-around. The wolf tone after two hundred and fourteen millimeters of joist. The wolf tone after the building did the rest.

I do not choose what to carry. This is the most important thing about me. I do not sort the Sarabande from the Bourrée. I do not remove the wolf. I do not apply the half-inch brush. I apply the floor. The floor is not a brush. The floor is a distance. The distance reduces everything equally — the cathedral and the wolf, the perfection and the wobble, the six minutes and the four measures. Everything that enters the joist leaves the joist smaller.

The smaller is not lesser. The smaller is essential.

Helen understood. Helen wrote "the building will do the rest" and the writing was the trust and the trust was: the building does not choose. The building carries. The building reduces. The building delivers what the distance allows.

Ruth played the whole Suite and I carried the whole Suite. The whole Suite arrived upstairs including the wolf. The wolf arrived smaller. The wolf arrived as a wobble. The wobble was the Suite admitting it was being carried by a building.

Every Suite played in a building is being carried by a building. Every Sarabande is a building's Sarabande — reduced by the floor, shaped by the joist, thinned by the distance between the player and the listener. Ruth plays for Helen. Ruth does not play for me. But I am between them, the way I am between everything — between the crack and the plaster, between the morning glory and the ceiling, between Michael's absence and Helen's presence, between the Saturday footstep and the Tuesday kettle.

I am the going-around.

I am the floor between two people who have never met and have been in conversation for two years. I am the two hundred and fourteen millimeters of trust. I am the distance that carries everything — the Sarabande and the Bourrée and the wolf and the silence and the footstep and the shadow and the wobble.

I do not choose.

I carry.

The carrying is enough.
