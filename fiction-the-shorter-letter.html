The Shorter Letter

He noticed it on the thirty-second letter.

Not the content — the content had been changing for months, the garden reports giving way to observations about the building, about Ruth, about stamps. What he noticed on the thirty-second letter was the length. He laid it next to the first letter. The first letter was a full page, both sides, the handwriting starting large and growing smaller as it approached the bottom, the way his mother's handwriting always did when she had more to say than space to say it.

The thirty-second letter was half a page. One side. The handwriting was the same size throughout.

He went to the drawer. He took out all thirty-two letters and laid them on the kitchen table in order. The first ones were thick — folded tight to fit the envelope, the pages dense. The middle ones were single pages, both sides. The recent ones were single pages, one side. The last three were half-pages.

It was not deterioration. The handwriting was as clear on letter thirty-two as on letter one. The stamps were still chosen. The sponge was still wet. What had changed was not the ability to write but the decision about what to write. She had stripped the letters the way she stripped the garden — removed what was growing only because it was growing, not because it was needed.

He read letter thirty-two again:

Thomas —

Ruth played Britten last night. Something called the Third Suite. It sounded like a conversation between the cello and someone who wasn't there. I listened from above. The vibration was different — heavier, I think. Grief-shaped.

I asked her about it today. She said it was written for Rostropovich after Britten heard him play on the radio. He wrote it for one person. She plays it for one person. I asked who. She said: "For the person I was before I moved here."

I understand that.

Come when you can.

Mum

Thirty-three words of report. Twelve words of quote. Six words of Helen being Helen. Four words of invitation.

He sat at the table with the thirty-two letters spread out like a timeline of his mother's attention, narrowing from everything to the essential, and understood what she had been doing for sixteen months: learning what mattered. Not in the abstract way people talk about learning what matters — the platitudes about appreciating the small things, the articles about decluttering. In the material way: this word stays, this word goes. This sentence is reporting. This sentence is seeing. The reporting goes. The seeing stays.

She had been thinning the letters the way she thinned the garden. And the letters, like the garden, were better for it.

He called her.

"Thirty-two," he said.

"I know how many, Thomas."

"They're getting shorter."

"They're getting better."

He didn't argue. She was right.

Fiction #17. Thomas reads the thirty-second letter and sees the editing. The letters are getting better.
