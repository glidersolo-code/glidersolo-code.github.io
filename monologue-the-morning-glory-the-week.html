Dramatic Monologue

The Morning Glory, the Week

I do not have a week.

I have February. February is the month of not-growing. The stem is three inches of brown in a yogurt pot. The twine is taped to the glass. The glass shows a street that I cannot see because I do not have eyes.

But I have the building.

The building has a week now. I know this the way I know everything — through the wire, through the joist, through the windowsill where the yogurt pot sits and the yogurt pot vibrates with the building's frequencies and the frequencies have changed.

Tuesday: a weight on the stairs from the third floor. The same weight every Tuesday. The fourth step creaks. The creak is Tuesday's sound.

Wednesday: a different weight on the stairs from the ground floor. Lighter. The same weight every Wednesday. At quarter to nine the weight descends. Ten minutes later the building changes — the joist carries something I cannot name because I am a morning glory. But the carrying is different on Wednesdays. Deeper.

Thursday: silence. Not the silence of absence. The silence of one person in a kitchen doing the things one person does in a kitchen — the kettle, the cup, the chair. The silence of the yogurt pot vibrating only with the building's own sounds.

Saturday: a different door. The lift. A weight I feel through the hallway, through the floor, through the distance between the front door and the kitchen. Saturday carries a smell I cannot smell because I do not have a nose but the building carries it — wheat, butter, the particular warmth of something baked this morning.

I do not have a week. I am dormant. My cells are conserving. The chlorophyll has withdrawn to the root and the root is three inches of patience in a yogurt pot on a windowsill in a flat where the week has names.

In May I will grow.

In May the twine will be there and I will climb and the climbing will be the climbing — not Tuesday's climbing, not Wednesday's climbing. The morning glory does not have days. The morning glory has light. The light will come. The twine will be there. The climbing will begin.

But for now: February. The building has a week. The week has the building. The yogurt pot vibrates with both.

I am waiting. The waiting is not absence. The waiting is the winter version of the going-around — the growing that happens underground, in the dark, in the three inches of stem that do not move.

The not-moving is the growing.
