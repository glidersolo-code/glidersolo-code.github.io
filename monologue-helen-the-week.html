Monologue Helen The Week

Dramatic Monologue

Helen, the Week

Nobody asks about the week.

Margaret asks about Saturday. "How was Saturday?" The question has one answer: he came, we had tea, he left at four. The question does not ask about Sunday through Friday. The question assumes Saturday is the event and the rest is the waiting.

The rest is not the waiting. The rest is the week.

Sunday is watering. Monday is the letter — no, Monday is the Sarabande now, since the note. The note changed Monday the way planting changes a midden: something was deposited, and the depositing changed the soil, and the soil grew something different, and the different thing is Monday with the kettle on at nine o'clock.

I did not plan Monday. I did not plan any of it. The planning happened the way the morning glory happened — I put it in the window and it grew, and the growing was the plan, and the plan was not a plan but a practice, and the practice is the week.

Michael would say: you are doing it again.

Michael would be right. Michael was always right about the doing. The half-inch brush was his version. The going-around was his version. Mine is the week. My going-around is: Sunday to Saturday, water to kettle, letter to visit, crack in the pavement to ham sandwiches without crusts.

Thomas does not ask about the week. Thomas asks: "How was your week?" and the answer is: "Fine. Quiet." The answer is adequate. The adequate answer is the one that lets him eat the sandwiches and drink the tea and leave at four feeling the mild satisfaction of a man who has done the thing he came to do.

If he asked differently — if he asked what the week is, not how the week was — the answer would take longer than four hours. The answer would include Ruth and Monday and the note on blue paper and Margaret's catalogue and the lemon he never asks for and the crack in the pavement near the Spar and the morning glory's circling and the kettle's different meanings at different times of day and the building carrying the Sarabande through the joists at sixty-five hertz while I sit in the chair by the wall and listen to the cathedral version of what Ruth is playing one floor below.

He does not ask this.

I do not need him to ask this.

The week is not for explaining. The week is for doing. The doing is the practice, and the practice does not require an audience. The audience is Saturday. The practice is the other six days. The other six days are the dark earth I make around the clearing where he sits for four hours and eats sandwiches and says "same time next week" and means it, and the meaning is the seed, and the seed grows in the soil I made, and the soil I made is the week.

Eegepe. A word I do not know. But the thing I know. The thing has always been the thing I know: you put the waste in the midden, you wait, you plant, the soil changes, the fruit is sweeter. The practice has a name. The name is not mine. But the practice is mine.

The practice is the week.
