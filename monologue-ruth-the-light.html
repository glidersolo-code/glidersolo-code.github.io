Monologue Ruth The Light

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Light

The light is different in June.

I notice because I play at nine, and at nine in February the flat is dark and the Sarabande begins in darkness and the darkness is part of the Sarabande — the darkness is the privacy, the not-being-seen, the playing for the person I was before I moved here.

At nine in June the flat is still light. The light comes through the window — not Helen's window, my window, the ground floor window that faces the car park and the bins and the wall of the building next door. The light comes in long and low and orange and the orange lies across the cello's back and the cello's back is the colour of the light and the colour of the light is the colour of the playing.

The Sarabande in June is different.

Not the notes. The notes are the same — the Third Suite, the fourth movement, the same fingerings I have used since Bergen, since Hallvard, since the forgiveness. The notes do not change with the season. The bow does not change with the light. The hand does not change with the orange on the cello's back.

But the Sarabande is different because the room is different and the room is different because the light is different and the light is different because it is June and June is the month when the morning glory flowers on the wire in the kitchen above me and the blue of the flowers and the orange of the light are complementary colours and the complementary colours are the building's chord — the two notes that, played together, produce the whole spectrum.

I do not know about the blue. I am downstairs. I play. The floor carries.

Helen hears the Sarabande through the green and blue of the vine on the glass. The Sarabande arrives in colour now — the colour of the light through leaves, the colour of the afternoon becoming evening, the colour of June doing what June does: filling the room with more light than the room needs, so the excess lies across the floor and the floor carries the excess the way it carries the Sarabande — upward, without choosing.

I play. The light lies across the cello. The cello does not know it is June. The cello knows the bow and the string and the resonance and the wolf tone on C that emerges on humid evenings when the wood expands and the body of the instrument argues with the string and the argument is the music's hidden frequency.

The wolf tone is louder in June. The humidity. The wood.

I welcome it. Helen said: the wolf tone is welcome. Helen said: I am saying it to you.

I play the whole Suite. The light moves across the room as I play — from the window to the wall, from the orange to the purple, from the beginning of the Prelude to the end of the Gigue. The Suite takes forty-five minutes. The light takes forty-five minutes. Both arrive at the same place: the end of the room, the end of the light, the end of the music.

The silence after the Suite is the silence of a June evening in a flat below a kitchen where a morning glory has flowered and a woman sits in a chair and the floor has carried everything it was asked to carry and the carrying is complete and the light is gone and the dark arrives and the dark is the rest.

The rest is not silence.

The rest is the building, breathing.
