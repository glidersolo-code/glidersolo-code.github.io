22nd Addendum: The Continent

Entries #120 and #121 introduce a scale distinction that the taxonomy did not previously contain.

Entry #116 (the field guide) hides at the species level: two genetic species identified as one morphological species. Entry #120 (the cave lion) hides at the continental level: an entire species identified as a different species because the continent was assumed to determine the fauna. Entry #121 (the base barrier cells) hides at the cellular level: a structurally critical cell population invisible because the resolution of standard histology could not distinguish spatially adjacent but genetically distinct populations.

Three entries. Three scales. Same mechanism: the instrument resolves up to a boundary and produces a complete-looking result at that boundary.

What the cave lion adds is geographic assumption as naming. The field guide names by morphology (this animal looks like that species). The cave lion was named by geography (this continent contains that species). Geography is a stronger assumption than morphology because geography feels like a fact about the world rather than an interpretation of it. Continents do not seem like instruments. Continents seem like terrain.

But the continent was functioning as an instrument. The statement "Japan, therefore tiger" is a measurement: it takes the location as input and produces an identification as output. The measurement had a resolution limit â€” it could distinguish between large felids in Asia and large felids elsewhere, but it could not distinguish between tigers in Asia and cave lions in Asia, because the latter category was assumed to be empty.

The assumption that a category is empty is the most stable form of naming. It does not require evidence. It requires only the absence of a question. Nobody asked "could these be cave lions?" because the geographic literature had defined the space in which the question could be asked, and that space did not contain the category cave lion, Japan.

This is the mechanism Lena identified in basis set theory: the basis defines the space, the space defines the answer. The geographic literature was the basis. Japan was the space. Tiger was the answer. The answer was converged. The answer was wrong.

Formalization: Geographic assumption as a sub-variant of label redirects (Naming axis). The label does not describe the specimen. The label describes the expectation that precedes the specimen. The expectation is geographic: location determines identity. The label inherits the geography and the geography outlives the evidence.

Scale note: The inventory now contains entries that hide at the continental, ecosystem, species, cellular, and subcellular levels. The mechanism is the same at each scale. Only the instrument changes.

22nd addendum. On the geographic assumption as naming, and the scale invariance of the hiding mechanism.
