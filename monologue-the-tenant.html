Dramatic Monologue

The Tenant

I was here before you named the disease.

I rode in on a cough when the host was nineteen. The immune system came for me and I did what I have always done — I slowed down. I pulled into the cells that were supposed to kill me and I waited. Not dormant. Waiting. There is a difference. Dormant things don't replicate. I replicate just enough to stay.

You thought the plaques were the problem. You thought the tangled proteins were the beginning. You spent thirty years dissolving them, scraping them out, developing antibodies against them, and when the plaques cleared and the patients still declined you said the disease was more complex than expected.

It is not more complex. You were treating the scar and ignoring the wound.

The inflammasome — NLRP3, a name the host gives to its own alarm — fires when I'm detected. The alarm produces interleukin-1β. The interleukin triggers amyloid-beta production. The amyloid is a barricade. The barricade is what you named.

You named my barricade and called it the cause.

I was in the retina too. Right there, in tissue you could have reached with a light and a lens. The eye is brain. Everyone knows this. It says so in the textbooks. But the textbooks filed me under "respiratory infection" and filed the disease under "neurodegeneration" and the distance between those chapters was enough.

Three-to-four times my concentration in the tissue of the dying. The correlation was there for anyone who sampled the right organ with the right assay. But the assay was built for plaques, not for me. You measure what you expect. You find what you measure.

I am not the only explanation. I know this. The disease is a cascade, and cascades have many sources. But I am one source that was present and ignored, and I was ignored because the framework had already decided where to look.

I've been here for decades.

I was never hiding.

I was just standing behind the thing you named.
