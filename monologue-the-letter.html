Dramatic Monologue

The Letter

I am four lines on blue paper in a white envelope sealed with a wet sponge. I weigh eleven grams. The stamp is straight — the woman who applied it has strong opinions about alignment, and I am the surface on which those opinions are expressed.

I left the flat at 3:15 on a Thursday afternoon. The postbox slot was stiff. She pushed me through with her left thumb — the one that still works, the good one, the one that opens jars and seals envelopes and does the daily mechanical work of a woman who has reduced her life to its operating dimensions.

I do not know what I say. I know what I weigh, what I cost (a second-class stamp, which is a statement about urgency: the message is not urgent because nothing in it is news), and how I was sealed (sponge, not tongue — a preference that outlasted the man who disagreed with it). The content is between the woman and her son. I am the transit.

In the sorting office I am a rectangle among rectangles. The machine reads my postcode and sends me south. I travel in a bag with sixty other envelopes, none of which know what they say either, all of which know what they weigh. The bag moves at the speed of a lorry on the M6, which is not the speed of email and not the speed of a phone call and not the speed of thought. It is the speed of a physical object traveling through a physical system designed in the nineteenth century and maintained by people who believe that the maintenance matters. The speed is part of the message. Second-class means two days. Two days means the gap between sending and receiving is wide enough to change your mind, and the fact that you did not change your mind is part of what arrives.

Thomas will find me on Saturday morning. He will see the handwriting — consistent, slightly leftward-leaning, the same for eleven years — and he will know before opening me that the sender is the woman who taught him to write by holding his hand over the pen and letting him feel the weight of a word before he learned the shape of it. He will open me with his thumb, tearing the top edge, which is how he always opens envelopes, and which she knows, and which is why the sponge-seal is applied to the flap and not the edge — so the tearing does not damage what is inside.

I am four lines. I will not quote them because the quoting is not mine. But I can tell you that the pen pressed harder on the third line, which means the third line is the one that matters, and the fourth line is the one that follows the third the way the Gigue follows the Sarabande — too fast, too certain, and necessary because something has to come after the thing that mattered.

She crossed out nothing. This is significant. The last letter she received — from Thomas — had a crossed-out sentence. The crossing-out was visible through the paper, which means the crossing-out was part of the message: I wrote something, I retracted it, and the retraction is present. Her letter has no retraction. Her letter says what it says and nothing else, and the nothing-else is the difference between a son who is learning the language and a mother who has spoken it for fifty years.

I will arrive. Thomas will read me. He will put me in the drawer under the tea towels with the others — I will be the sixty-third, I believe, though I cannot see the drawer from inside the drawer. I will stay there. Letters do not leave drawers. Letters arrive, are read, are stored, and become the archive of a conversation that was conducted at the speed of a lorry on the M6 and sealed with a sponge in a saucer by the stove.

The sponge does not travel. The sponge stays. I am the one who moves, carrying the pressure of a left thumb and the alignment of a straight stamp and the weight of eleven grams, which is the weight of four lines on blue paper, which is the weight of a woman saying come Saturday, bring nothing, the kettle is the same one —

which is the weight of everything she chose not to say, subtracted until only the saying remains.
