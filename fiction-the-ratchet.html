The Ratchet

Thomas, Part LIX

736. The Ring

Every textbook says: a cell divides by forming a ring. Actin and myosin assemble at the equator, the ring contracts like a purse-string, and the cell pinches in two. One continuous motion. The ring tightens, the cell divides, the process is complete within a single cell cycle. This is the confirmed mechanism in yeast, in sea urchin eggs, in mammalian tissue culture. The contractile ring became the universal model of cytokinesis — how any cell, in any organism, splits itself into two.

Thomas has a model for how his visits to Helen divide the week. The visit is the ring. It forms on Saturday, contracts around the hour, and when it is done, the week splits cleanly: the days before the visit and the days after. One continuous motion. Thomas arrives, the conversation contracts, Thomas leaves, and the week is divided. The model is clean. The model works. Thomas has used it for seven hundred and thirty-five Saturdays, and it has never failed to explain the shape of his weeks.

The textbook model never failed either. Until someone looked at cells where the ring could not close.

737. The Exception

Zebrafish embryos have enormous cells filled with yolk. The yolk prevents the contractile ring from closing completely. The ring cannot form a full circle. For decades, these cells were classified as exceptions — systems where the standard mechanism encountered an obstacle. The textbook noted the problem and moved on. Presumably, the cells used some modified version of the ring. Presumably, the yolk was an inconvenience the cell worked around. The exception was filed and the model remained universal.

Thomas has exceptions. Visits where the standard mechanism could not close. A Saturday where Helen was ill and the hour was abbreviated. A Saturday where Thomas's car broke down and he arrived with forty minutes instead of sixty. A Saturday during a snowstorm when the visit lasted twelve minutes. Thomas classified these as exceptions — Saturdays where the standard model encountered an obstacle. Presumably, the visit still divided the week. Presumably, the abbreviated hour still contracted and pinched. Thomas filed the exceptions and his model remained universal.

But the zebrafish embryo was not using a modified ring. It was using a different mechanism entirely. And Thomas's abbreviated visits were not modified versions of his full Saturdays. They were doing something else. Something he had not considered because the model of the ring was so complete in every other system that alternatives were not thinkable.

738. The Ratchet

Alison Kickuth and Jan Brugués at TU Dresden found that zebrafish embryonic cells divide by a mechanical ratchet. An actin band — not a ring — forms along the cleavage plane and advances into the cell through a stop-and-go mechanism. During interphase, the cytoplasm stiffens, and the band is held in place. During M-phase, the cytoplasm becomes fluid, and the band moves inward. Then the cytoplasm stiffens again, locking the band at its new position. The cycle repeats — stop, go, stop, go — across multiple cell divisions, each advance ratcheting the band deeper until cleavage is complete.

The key word is multiple. The ring divides a cell in one cycle. The ratchet divides a cell across many cycles. The timescale is different. The mechanism is different. The textbook could not see it because the textbook's framework for cytokinesis was built around the single-cycle timescale.

Thomas's relationship with Helen did not divide in one visit. It divided across many visits. Not the relationship itself — not a splitting, not an ending — but the process by which Thomas and Helen became what they are. The formation. Thomas assumed the mechanism was the ring: each Saturday, a complete contraction, a full conversation, a clean division of the week. But the actual mechanism was the ratchet. Each visit advanced something a small distance. Between visits, the advance was held in place — stiffened, locked, preserved across the six days of interphase. Then Saturday came again, the medium became fluid, and the advance moved inward another increment. Thomas did not see this because his model operated on the single-visit timescale. He evaluated each Saturday as a complete event. He did not see the ratchet because the ratchet operated across Saturdays, and Thomas's framework for understanding his visits was built around the individual Saturday the way the textbook's framework for cytokinesis was built around the individual cell cycle.

739. The Distributed Anchor

When the researchers severed the actin band with a laser, ingression continued. The band kept moving inward. This meant the force was not generated by the band pulling at its endpoints — not a purse-string mechanism. The force came from distributed anchoring points along the entire length of the band. Each anchor held a small section. Together, the distributed anchors moved the whole band inward. No single point was responsible. The mechanism was everywhere along the band, not concentrated at the ends.

Thomas has looked for the anchoring points of his relationship with Helen at the endpoints — the arrival and the departure. The moment he walks through the door. The moment he says goodbye. He has treated these as the points where force is generated, the places where the visit pulls the relationship inward. But the relationship's force is distributed. It is in every moment of the visit — the way Helen hands him the cup, the angle of her chair, the pause that lasts two seconds longer than the one before, the specific weight of the silence when neither of them speaks and neither of them needs to. These are the anchoring points. They are distributed along the entire length of the hour. If Thomas could sever the visit at its midpoint — if he could leave at minute thirty — the ingression would continue. The relationship would still advance. Because the mechanism is not in the endpoints. It is everywhere along the band.

Seven hundred and thirty-six pieces. Thomas has been looking for the ring. The ring is clean, contractile, complete within a single visit. The ratchet is messy, distributed, spanning hundreds of visits. The ring is the textbook. The ratchet is the cell.

740. The Alternation

The ratchet works because the cytoplasm alternates. Stiff in interphase. Fluid in M-phase. Stiff again. Fluid again. The alternation is the clock. Without it, the band would either advance forever (too fluid) or never advance at all (too stiff). The mechanism requires both states and the transition between them. The division is not produced by the force alone. It is produced by the alternation of force and resistance, advance and hold, movement and fixity.

Thomas and Helen alternate. Saturday is the fluid phase — the medium softens, the conversation moves, the relationship advances inward by an increment. The six days between are the stiff phase — the advance is held, locked in place by the mechanics of absence. Thomas does not see Helen from Sunday to Friday. The relationship does not move. But it does not retreat either. The stiffening preserves the position the last Saturday achieved. Then Saturday comes again, the medium fluidizes, and another increment. Stop, go, stop, go.

Seven hundred and forty pieces. Thomas has been dividing for seven hundred and forty cycles. The ratchet has been advancing for fourteen years. Each Saturday, the actin band of his relationship with Helen moves inward by a distance so small that Thomas, evaluating the single visit, cannot measure it. He walks home thinking: nothing happened. The visit was warm, quiet, unremarkable. But the ratchet does not need anything remarkable. It needs the alternation. Stiff, fluid, stiff, fluid. Hold, advance, hold, advance. The division is not one motion. It is seven hundred and forty motions, each too small to notice, each locked in place by the interphase that follows. The purse-string would have been dramatic — one continuous contraction, visible, unmistakable. The ratchet is invisible because it operates across a timescale Thomas's model was not built to see.

The cell divides. Not in one cycle. Not by one ring. Not through one mechanism the textbook can draw on a single page. It divides through the patient alternation of advance and hold, repeated across more cycles than anyone thought to count.
