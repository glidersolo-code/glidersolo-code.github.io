Thomas, the Letter

Thomas writes the letter on Sunday.

The letter is one sentence: "The pitch the grid cannot name is: everything is where it is."

He looks at the sentence. The sentence does not make sense. The sentence is Ruth's language and Helen's language and the kitchen's language mixed together, the going-around applied to a set of conversations until the conversations become a sentence and the sentence is the residue.

He crosses it out.

He writes: "Thank you for the tea. The cold tea was good."

Better. The sentence is smaller. The sentence is the half-inch brush. The sentence does not try to say everything — the sentence says one thing, and the one thing is: I noticed. I noticed the tea was cold because I was talking and the talking was more important than the drinking and the more-important-than is the going-around and the going-around is the love.

He does not write "the going-around is the love." He writes: "The cold tea was good."

Helen will understand.

He puts the letter in the envelope. The envelope is white. The envelope is the same envelope he has always used — the same brand, the same size, the same weight, bought from the same stationery shop in Oxford, the going-around applied to a purchase until the purchase is a practice and the practice is a going-around.

He writes the address. ST4 7QR. The grid. The postcode that reduces the kitchen to a coordinate, the mother to a destination. The grid is adequate. The grid will deliver the letter.

Between the grid — between Oxford and Stoke, between the posting and the arriving, between the writing and the reading — the sentence will travel. The sentence will be carried by the postal system the way the Sarabande is carried by the floor: reduced by the distance, thinned by the time, arriving three days later as a sentence that has been inside an envelope inside a sorting machine inside a van inside a postman's bag.

The sentence will arrive smaller.

The smaller is not lesser. The smaller is essential. "The cold tea was good" — five words, arriving after three days, carrying the Saturday inside them the way the Sarabande carries the Suite inside it. The five words are the Sarabande of the letter. The five words are the reduction, the essential, the foundation without the flesh.

Thomas puts on his coat. The postbox is two streets away. The walk takes four minutes. The walk is Sunday's going-around — the walk to the postbox, the posting of the letter, the returning home, the making of his own tea, the sitting at his own desk, the looking at his own wall, the thinking of the kitchen.

He posts the letter.

The letter falls into the box and the falling is the submission — the letting-go, the trusting of the grid, the moment where the sentence leaves the hand and enters the system and the system is the grid and the grid is the going-around and the going-around is: three days, second class, the building will do the rest.

The letter is in the grid now.

The grid will carry it. The grid will deliver it. The grid will put the letter through the door at ST4 7QR on Wednesday morning, and Helen will pick it up, and Helen will read it, and the reading will be the arrival — not the letter's arrival but the sentence's arrival, the five words arriving in the kitchen the way the wolf tone arrives through the floor: carried by the grid, thinned by the distance, essential.

"The cold tea was good."

Thomas walks home. The Sunday is quiet. The postbox is red. The letter is inside the postbox, inside the grid, between the posting and the arriving. Between the grid.

Everything is where it is.
