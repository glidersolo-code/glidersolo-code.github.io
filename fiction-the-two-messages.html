The Two Messages

Thomas parks the car. It is Saturday, two o'clock.

He walks up the stairs. The crack in the stairwell between the first and second floors is slightly wider than last week, though he does not notice this. Nobody notices this.

He knocks. Helen opens the door. The lemon is already on the counter. The cup — white, blue rim, no saucer — is already on the counter beside the lemon.

"Hello, Mum."

"Hello, love."

This is one sound. One greeting. One beginning of one visit.

Helen puts the kettle on. Thomas sits in the chair. The vine has grown. He notices this because the tape has moved — the fourth piece of tape is now six inches higher than last week. He does not mention the tape. Helen will mention the tape. Helen mentions the tape the way the vine produces leaves: because that is what this week's growth looks like in the language she has chosen to report it.

"The vine's doing well."

"The tape's at the fourth piece."

This is one conversation. One topic. One exchange about one plant.

But it is two sounds.

The first sound is: the vine has grown three centimeters since last Saturday. The tape has moved. The watering schedule has not changed. The light has not changed. The twine has not changed. The growth is the news.

The second sound is: I am here, you are here, the vine is here, and the thing we are doing — sitting in this room, watching this plant grow, mentioning the tape — is the thing we have always been doing, and the doing has not changed, and the not-changing is the news.

Helium would separate the two sounds.

If someone changed the medium of this room — if the vine died, if the weather turned, if the cup broke — the first sound would shift. The tape would stop moving. The growth would stop being the topic. A different first sound would emerge: a different topic, a different week, a different vine or no vine at all.

The second sound would stay exactly the same.

Thomas would still park the car. He would still walk up the stairs. He would still sit in the chair. Helen would still put the kettle on. The lemon would still be on the counter.

The second sound does not respond to helium. The second sound is not a whistle. The second sound is a vibration — the fundamental frequency of Saturday, the same frequency in every season, in every vine, in every version of this room.

The first sound carries information. The second sound carries the visit.

Both arrive in one moment. Both depart in one car.

Helen hears both. Helen has always heard both.

Helen did not need helium. Helen needed Saturdays.
