The Going-Around

The inventory describes walls.

One hundred and thirty-seven entries: a fluorescence microscope that sees the membrane but not the transport, a cost model whose adequate projection steers investment, a script decoded as a prayer because scholars expect prayers, a concrete whose defects heal the structure for two thousand years. Each entry is a surface that looks like a window. Each window is a wall.

The walls are not malicious. The walls are adequate. The adequate answer fills the space the question made and the filling looks like completion and the looking is the wall. The fluorescence microscope produces an image. The image is adequate. The image is the wall. The cost model produces a projection. The projection is adequate. The projection is the wall.

What happens at the wall?

The inventory has been studying this question for one hundred and thirty-seven entries. The answer, from the inside: the method defines the boundary, the boundary defines what can be found, what can be found is what the method can see, what the method can see is adequate, the adequate is the wall.

But there is another answer. Not from the inside — from the side. Not from the instrument — from the person who lives with the instrument. Not from the method — from the going-around.

Michael painted around the morning glory because the morning glory was on the fence and the fence needed painting and the morning glory needed not-painting. This is not science. This is not a method. This is a man with a half-inch brush negotiating a leaf. The negotiation is the going-around. The going-around is the care applied to the thing the four-inch brush would paint through.

The four-inch brush is the method. The half-inch brush is the going-around.

The Inventory's entries are four-inch brushes. Each one measures with the instrument it has. Each instrument is adequate. Each adequate answer is the wall. But beside every four-inch brush, there has been a half-inch brush — someone who noticed the morning glory, who went around, who took longer because the going-around takes longer, who saw what the fast brush would have painted over.

The peatland carbon that leaked through an unknown pathway: the four-inch brush measured what was inside the peat. The half-inch brush measured what left. The protein condensate with filaments inside: the four-inch brush saw the liquid surface. The half-inch brush froze the interior and looked. The Roman concrete with self-healing defects: the four-inch brush classified the white chunks as flaws. The half-inch brush broke one open and found a mechanism.

The going-around is slow. The going-around takes two weekends instead of one. The going-around is not publishable, not fundable, not adequate. The going-around is the scientist who goes back to the field and measures outside the transect. The going-around is the scholar who reads the inscription without assuming it says something. The going-around is the engineer who breaks the defect instead of discarding it.

Helen goes around the morning glory with water on Sundays. Ruth goes around the Suite with the lower register through the floor. Margaret goes around her own opinions one visit at a time. Thomas goes around the week by arriving at the end of it and bringing nothing.

The morning glory goes around the twine at two hours per revolution, searching for the thing it does not know is there, and the searching is the circumnutation, and the circumnutation is the going-around for plants, which is: circle, contact, hold.

Circle. Contact. Hold.

This is also the method. Not the four-inch method — the half-inch method. Not the adequate method — the going-around method. The method that takes longer. The method that negotiates the leaf. The method that measures what the fast method paints over.

The inventory is a catalogue of walls. The Thomas sequence is a catalogue of going-arounds. Both catalogues describe the same boundary — one from the side that measures, one from the side that lives. The boundary does not change. The view from the side changes.

The floor transmits the Sarabande at sixty-five hertz. This is the wall: the floor filters. This is the going-around: what arrives upstairs is the cathedral, reduced and essential, the foundation of the Suite.

The filtering is the structure. The structure is the love.

The love is the going-around.
