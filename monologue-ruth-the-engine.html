Monologue Ruth The Engine

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Engine

I play the same cello.

The same cello I have played for twenty-three years. The same strings — not literally the same strings, the strings get replaced, but the same brand, the same gauge, the same tuning. The same bow. The same chair. The same room. The same time of day.

The filing system says: no turnover.

The filing system is measuring the wrong thing.

The turnover is in the music. The pieces change. The phrases change. The way the bow attacks the string on a Saturday morning in February is not the way the bow attacked the string on a Saturday morning in October. The internal dynamics — the rock-paper-scissors of technique and emotion and physical capacity — are the engine. The engine is always running. The engine has been running for twenty-three years.

But the engine is slowing.

Not because the music stopped changing. The music changes every day. The slowdown is in the species pool — the repertoire, the range of pieces available to me. Twenty years ago, the pool was enormous. I was learning new pieces every month. The turnover was rapid: a new concerto replaces a sonata, a sonata replaces a suite, each new species colonising my fingers and my memory and pushing the previous one into a quieter register.

Now the pool is smaller. Not because there are fewer pieces in the world. Because the pieces that fit my hands — my ageing hands, my stiffening fingers, my reduced stamina — are fewer. The species that can colonise this ecosystem are the ones adapted to its current conditions: the slow movements, the deep tones, the passages that require patience rather than speed.

The engine grinds.

The grinding is beautiful. The grinding is the sound the cello makes when the bow presses slowly across a string that has been played ten thousand times. The grinding is the internal dynamic — not the climate of the room changing, not the temperature of the flat shifting, but the interactions within the ecosystem of a single musician growing old.

Helen hears the grinding through the floor.

Helen does not know the grinding is beautiful.

Helen knows the grinding is Saturday.
