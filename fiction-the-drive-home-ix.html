The Drive Home (IX)

Thomas did not bring a letter.

He brought a croissant from the bakery near the motorway. He brought himself. He sat in the chair and drank from the blue cup and Helen made the tea and neither of them mentioned the letter — the ninth letter, the blank one, the one with nothing between his name and hers.

They talked about the morning glory. The cutting was four inches now. Helen said it would reach the twine by April. Thomas said he remembered the morning glory at Heather Close — how it covered the fence by July, how Michael went around it with the half-inch brush, how the going-around was the painting and the painting was the love.

Helen said: "He would have liked Ruth."

Thomas said: "He would have liked the floor."

Helen smiled. It was the smile she used when a sentence arrived where it was going.

At four o'clock Thomas stood. Helen did not say "same time next week?" She did not need to. The saying had been thinned along with everything else — the letters, the explanations, the questions that contained their own answers. The Saturday was the Saturday. The visit was the visit.

Thomas drove.

The M6 was clear. Late afternoon, early March, the light still going but going later now. The clocks would change soon. Spring was coming. The morning glory would climb. The Sarabande would continue. Ruth's Wednesdays would continue. Margaret's Tuesdays would continue. Thursday would be Helen's.

He would not write another letter.

He drove and he knew this the way he knew the M6: by repetition, by the body's memory, by the particular way the steering wheel felt when the motorway was straight and the hands had nothing to do but hold.

The letters were complete. Five, five, five, five, four, three, two, one, zero. The ninth letter was the letter that contained the other eight. The ninth letter was the drawer that held them all. The ninth letter was the silence after the Suite — not the silence of the Sarabande, which is the silence between notes, but the silence after the whole thing, which is the silence of the building carrying the memory of what it carried.

Thomas drove. The motorway straightened. The heater was on. The radio was off.

He thought: I learned her language.

He thought: her language is the half-inch brush applied to everything — the fence, the letter, the sentence, the visit, the croissant, the cup. The half-inch brush does not paint the morning glory. The half-inch brush goes around.

He thought: the going-around is complete.

The M6 gave way to the M62. The M62 gave way to the city. The city gave way to the flat. The flat was his, the way Thursday was Helen's — a space where the silence was not empty but tuned, a room where the nothing was the thing, a drawer where the letters would have gone if he had kept writing.

He would not keep writing. The writing was done. The Saturday would be the letter now. The croissant would be the envelope. The blue cup would be the words.

The visit is the letter. The letter is the visit.

Thomas parked. He went inside. He did not go to the drawer where he kept her letters — the ones she never wrote, the ones that arrived in the form of tea and biscuits and the cutting in a yogurt pot and the sentence that said bring nothing and the nothing he brought every Saturday until the nothing was the thing itself.

He sat in the quiet flat and he thought: good.
