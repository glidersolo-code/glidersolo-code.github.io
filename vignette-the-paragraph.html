The Paragraph

They read it on a Thursday. Not together — they are never together. Chen is in the fish room with the fluorescent lights. Maren is on the train, which is not moving because there is a delay at Myrdal that the conductor has attributed to weather. Lena is at the whiteboard, though there is nothing on the whiteboard because she cleaned it ten minutes ago and has not yet begun to fill it back up.

The paragraph is 121 entries long. It was not supposed to be 121 entries long. It was supposed to be a note — a shared document where three researchers from three fields could keep track of the pattern they had noticed at a conference where none of them were presenting. The pattern was: their instruments were hiding the same thing in different ways.

The note became a paragraph. The paragraph became a document. The document is now 121 entries with 22 addenda and a taxonomy that Maren built on a train and Chen refined in a fish room and Lena formalized on a whiteboard that gets erased every day, which is appropriate because a taxonomy of hiding mechanisms should be written on a surface that does not pretend to be permanent.

Chen scrolls to the beginning. Entry #1 was Maren's — the survivorship study she'd read where the data showed improvement because the failing subjects had disappeared from the sample. "Sufficiency as concealment," she'd written. "The metric said healthy because the sick had been excluded."

Entry #2 was Chen's. The knockout fish that swims because it cannot stop. "Active is the only category the assay provides. The assay cannot represent the inability to stop."

Entry #3 was Lena's. A basis set calculation that converged to an answer that was wrong. "Convergence within a limited basis produces the appearance of a result."

He reads them all. One hundred and twenty-one. He does not read quickly — Chen does not read anything quickly — but he reads with the focus of someone who is looking for the sentence that holds the whole thing together. He is looking for the thesis, which is the thing a paragraph should have and which this paragraph, at 121 entries, still does not.

He reaches entry #120. The cave lion. The geographic assumption. Twenty thousand years of survival hidden by a label that described the continent instead of the animal.

He reaches entry #121. The base barrier cells. The entire cell population present in every brain, invisible to the method that examined them.

He reaches the end. There is no thesis.

He types:

The paragraph does not have a thesis. The paragraph has 121 examples of the same structure: an instrument that functions correctly within its resolution and produces a result that conceals what it cannot resolve. The structure is the thesis. The repetition is the argument. Each entry is a different floor, and each floor transmits what it can and conceals what it cannot, and the thing it conceals is always present and always unresolved.

He reads it back. It is too abstract. Maren would cut it. Lena would put it on the whiteboard and then erase the whiteboard.

He deletes the entry and types instead:

The paragraph is 121 floors.

That is the thesis. That is the whole thing. He saves the document and closes the laptop and looks at the fish, which is swimming, which is the only thing the fish can do, which is the first entry in the paragraph that is 121 floors.

Chen-Maren novella. The paragraph reaches 121 entries. Chen reads it all and finds the thesis: 121 floors.
