Footnotes for an Essay That Was Never Written

The essay was going to be about sufficiency.¹ It was going to argue that the most dangerous form of concealment is the correct answer.² It was going to use three examples.³ It was going to be approximately nine hundred words.⁴ It was going to end with an image.⁵

¹ Sufficiency: the state of having enough. In epistemology, the state of having enough knowledge to prevent the next question. Not ignorance. Not error. Not bias. The moment when the answer is adequate and adequacy becomes the reason nobody asks what's behind it.

² The correct answer as concealment mechanism. Consider: amyloid plaques are real deposits in real brains. The ion channel really does convert vibration into hearing. The morphological species really does have those bones. None of the original findings were wrong. They were sufficient. Sufficient to fund research programs, sustain careers, produce textbooks. The disease kept killing. The cells kept dying. Half the species kept being invisible. But the answers were correct.

³ Example one: Chlamydia pneumoniae, a common respiratory bacterium carried by ninety percent of adults, found at three-to-four times normal concentration in the brains of Alzheimer's patients. The bacterium activates the immune cascade that produces the plaques. The plaques were studied for thirty years as the cause. They may be the scar. Example two: TMC1 and TMC2, proteins in the inner ear, known for decades as ion channels. They also regulate cell membrane structure. When this regulation fails, the cell dies. The lethal function is invisible in isolated preparations. Every standard experiment was designed to make it disappear. Example three: for every vertebrate species identified by morphology, there is on average one more genetically distinct species hiding behind the same body plan. The ratio is two to one. It holds across mammals, birds, and fish. The classification system hid half the species it was built to catalog.

⁴ Nine hundred words is the average length of the essays in this project, which is also approximately the length at which a piece can sustain one argument with three supporting examples, make a closing move, and stop before the reader's attention wanders. This length was not chosen. It emerged from practice.

⁵ The image was going to be this: the answer sitting in a room with the question, and the question can't see the answer because the answer is wearing the same uniform as the furniture.
