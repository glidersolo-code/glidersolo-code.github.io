The Week

Sunday is the longest day.

Not because she misses him — that word is too large for what happens. The flat on Sunday is the flat on Saturday minus one person, and the subtraction does not leave a hole. It leaves a stillness that is different from the stillness before he came. The chair where he sat is the same chair. The blue cup is washed and in the cupboard. The morning glory has not moved.

Helen waters the morning glory on Sunday because Sunday is watering day, not because Thomas was here yesterday.

Monday she writes a letter. Not to Thomas — to Ruth. A note pushed under the door: The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday. I wanted to say so. Ruth does not reply. Ruth plays the Sarabande again on Monday night, which is not Monday night for the Sarabande — the Sarabande is Thursday and Saturday, sometimes Tuesday. Playing it on Monday is the reply.

Helen hears it through the floor and puts the kettle on. The kettle is not for anyone. The kettle is for the sound of the kettle, which is the sound of the flat working.

Tuesday she goes to the shops. Bread, milk, a lemon she will not use until Friday. The walk is twelve minutes. She knows every crack in the pavement between the flat and the Spar. She does not count them. She knows them the way she knows the morning glory's position on the twine — by passing, not by measuring.

Wednesday is Margaret. Margaret comes at two with a catalogue she does not want and opinions she does. The sofa is fine. The sofa converts. Margaret has sat on the sofa and decided it is adequate, and the word adequate in Margaret's mouth is a sentence that continues silently into but have you considered. Helen has considered. Helen considered when she moved in and she considered correctly and the considering is finished.

"How was Saturday?" Margaret asks.

"He came."

"What did you do?"

"Had tea."

Margaret waits for more. There is no more. There is tea, and the blue cup, and the Sarabande through the floor during the ham sandwiches, and the goodbye at four o'clock, and same time next week and if you like, and the watching until the car turns the corner, and that is the whole of Saturday, and the whole of Saturday is enough.

"That's nice," Margaret says.

"It is," Helen says.

Thursday is Ruth. Four o'clock, the same time Thomas leaves on Saturday, which is a coincidence that Helen has not mentioned and Ruth has not noticed. Tea. The cello cases in the hall. Ruth's hands wrapped around the cup the way she wraps them around the neck of the cello — carefully, with the knowledge that the thing she is holding will respond to pressure.

They do not talk about the Sarabande. They do not talk about Thomas. They talk about the cherry trees on Margaret's street and the cost of stamps and whether the morning glory will reach the top of the twine by April. Ruth says yes. Helen says it depends on the light. Both are right in the way that two people describing the same thing from different floors are right.

Friday she buys the lemons. Two, for the weekend. One for the gin on Friday night — a small glass, the tonic from the bottle in the fridge that loses its fizz by Wednesday, which is why she buys a new one on Tuesday. One for the tea on Saturday, in case Thomas wants lemon, which he never does, but the lemon is there because the offering is the point, not the acceptance.

Saturday.

The kettle is on before his car is in the road. The blue cup is out. The morning glory is where it is. Everything is where it is. The knock will come, and the door will open, and the subtraction will reverse, and the flat will be the flat plus one, and the one will not know how precisely the week was built around the space he fills for four hours on Saturday afternoon.

He will bring nothing. She will give him everything she has, which is tea and ham sandwiches and the blue cup and the quiet and the morning glory and the Sarabande still held in the joists from last night.

Same time next week.

If you like.
