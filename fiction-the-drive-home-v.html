The Drive Home (V)

The M6 at half past four. February light. The kind of light that does not commit — not dark yet, not bright enough to call afternoon, the light equivalent of "maybe."

Thomas drove. The radio was off. The radio had been off for six Saturdays. The silence in the car was not silence — the silence was Thomas thinking about wolf tones.

The cello disagrees with itself. The body resonates at the same frequency as the string, and the two vibrations interfere, and the interference is a wobble. The wobble is not a mistake. The wobble is the instrument being present in its own sound. Most of the time the instrument supports the string. At one frequency the instrument competes. The competition is the wolf.

Thomas thought: I am the wolf tone.

Not the wobble — the interference. The son who visits every Saturday and the mother who opens the door every Saturday and the visit is the resonance — the two lives vibrating at the same frequency — and the interference is the things Thomas does not say. The "how was your week?" that gets "fine, quiet." The lemon he did not ask for until the fifth Saturday. The letters he did not open for four days. The wolf tone is the space between what Thomas does and what Thomas means, and the space is the wobble, and the wobble is the going-around.

Helen said: the building does the rest.

The building is the M6. The building is the fifty minutes between the flat and his own house. The building is the drive that carries the visit through the distance, filtering out the top and leaving the bottom, the fundamental, the thing that arrives at the other end: a man who visited his mother and drank tea with a lemon and heard about a wolf tone and said "same time next week" and meant: I love you, expressed as a conditional, expressed as a question, expressed as the going-around applied to everything he cannot say.

He would not write a note this week. The notes had been good — "The lemon was good. Thank you" — but the notes were one visit's going-around. The next Saturday did not need a note. The next Saturday needed the visit.

The visit was enough. The visit was always enough. The visit was the Sarabande played through a floor — reduced by the distance, filtered by the road, arriving at Helen's door as a man with a coat and no gift and the understanding that no gift is the gift, that nothing is what you bring when you have understood what the visit already contains.

The M6 carried him. The road, like the floor, carried what it could carry. The road carried the wolf tone and the Sarabande and the lemon and the maybe and the if-you-like and the enough and the love.

The love was enough. The love was always enough. The love was the going-around.
