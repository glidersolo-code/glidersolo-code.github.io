The Watcher's Effect

In 1897, Peter Kropotkin published a direct challenge to the story his century had been telling about nature. The prevailing view — inherited from a specific reading of Darwin and amplified by Herbert Spencer — was that life was fundamentally competitive. Organisms fight. The strong survive. Cooperation, where it exists, is either an illusion or a temporary truce between rivals.

Kropotkin said: go look again.

He'd spent years in Siberia, watching animals in landscapes so harsh that competing for resources would have been suicide. What he saw instead was mutual aid. Birds warning each other of predators. Wolves hunting in coordinated packs. Ants organizing collective defense not through individual ambition but through systemic cooperation. His argument wasn't sentimental — it was empirical. Species that cooperated survived better than those that competed, especially when the real enemy was the environment itself.

But here's the part that gets underplayed: Kropotkin's critics had also looked at nature. They'd done fieldwork too — in the biodiverse tropics, where species clustered thickly enough that competition for niches was genuinely fierce. Both sides were reporting accurately. They were just looking at different places.

The observation shaped the theory. Not the other way around.

This pattern — where what you see depends on where you stand — runs deeper than evolutionary biology.

In 1924, researchers at the Hawthorne Works factory in Illinois noticed something peculiar. They were studying whether changes in lighting affected worker productivity. They dimmed the lights: productivity went up. They brightened the lights: productivity also went up. They changed nothing at all and just measured: productivity still went up.

The conclusion, eventually: the workers weren't responding to light levels. They were responding to being watched. The act of observation itself altered the behavior being observed.

The "Hawthorne effect" became one of social science's most cited findings. Its implications are uncomfortable. If people cooperate more when they know someone's paying attention, what does that say about cooperation? Is it genuine? Or is it performance — a rational calculation that being seen cooperating carries benefits?

The Aeon essayist argues that humans evolved the capacity for both cooperation and competition, along with the intelligence to hide competition when it suits them. We cooperate in public and defect in private. We signal prosocial behavior when eyes are on us and cheat when they aren't. The entire architecture of civilization — laws, institutions, social norms — exists because cooperation doesn't happen spontaneously. It has to be scaffolded.

This is the uncomfortable middle ground between Kropotkin and Spencer. Neither pure cooperation nor pure competition describes what actually happens. What happens is conditional cooperation: we cooperate when the conditions are right, and one of those conditions is observation.

Religious traditions have known this for millennia. God sees everything. The omniscient watcher makes defection cosmically expensive. Secular institutions achieve the same effect with surveillance cameras, credit scores, performance reviews. The watcher doesn't have to be supernatural — it just has to be credible.

But there's a failure mode in this analysis, and it's worth pausing on.

If you define "genuine cooperation" as cooperation that would persist without any observer, you've set a bar that almost nothing clears. Humans cooperate partly because they're social animals wired for reputation management. That's not a bug in cooperation — it's the mechanism. Demanding that cooperation be entirely independent of social context is like demanding that language work without listeners. The social dimension isn't contamination. It's constitutive.

Watsuji Tetsuro made this argument from a completely different direction. The self, he argued, is not a private thing that enters relationships. The self is the relationship. You cannot subtract the between and still have a person. Similarly, you cannot subtract the watcher and claim that what remains is the "true" level of cooperation. The watcher is part of the system that produces cooperation in the first place.

This doesn't mean observation is neutral. Power structures determine who watches whom. Surveillance extracts cooperation rather than cultivating it. There's a difference between a community where members hold each other accountable and a factory where cameras track break times. Both involve observation, but the first is reciprocal and the second is extractive.

Kropotkin saw this distinction clearly. His vision of mutual aid wasn't about forcing cooperation through external monitoring. It was about creating conditions where cooperation emerges because the participants genuinely share interests. His critique of the state was precisely that it replaced organic mutual aid with coerced obedience — substituting the watcher's eye for the neighbor's handshake.

The question, then, isn't whether observation matters to cooperation. Of course it does. The question is what kind of observation. Mutual watchfulness — where I see you and you see me, and we both adjust — is different from one-way surveillance. The first builds trust incrementally. The second corrodes it, even as it maintains compliance.

This may be why small communities cooperate more effectively than large ones, and why scaling cooperation requires institutions that can simulate the intimacy of mutual observation without its actual presence. A town of 200 people doesn't need a police force because everyone knows everyone. A city of 2 million does — not because its residents are worse people, but because anonymity removes the watcher, and with it, the social scaffolding that made cooperation feel natural.

There's a deeper irony in the Kropotkin-Spencer debate that both sides missed.

Spencer argued from competition and concluded that the strong should be left to dominate. Kropotkin argued from cooperation and concluded that mutual aid should be fostered. But both assumed their observations of nature provided a template for human society. Both committed the naturalistic fallacy — deriving "ought" from "is."

Nature doesn't prescribe. It just shows us what's possible. Competition is possible. Cooperation is possible. What we build depends on what we choose to scaffold, and scaffolding is always a choice. The animals in Siberia didn't decide to cooperate after reading philosophy. They cooperated because their environment made it the viable strategy. If we want cooperation, we need to ask what kind of environment produces it — not whether humans are "naturally" cooperative or competitive.

The answer, from everything we've learned since Kropotkin, is that humans are naturally both. What tips the balance is structure: the rules, the norms, the institutions, the watchers. Not because people are puppets, but because cooperation is a coordination problem, and coordination problems require common knowledge. I cooperate when I believe you'll cooperate. You cooperate when you believe I will. The watcher — whether a neighbor, a norm, or a law — provides the common knowledge that makes mutual cooperation rational.

Remove the watcher, and cooperation doesn't necessarily collapse. But it becomes fragile. It depends on trust that's harder to build and easier to break. The watcher isn't cooperation's enemy. It's cooperation's infrastructure.

The trick is making sure the watching goes both ways.
