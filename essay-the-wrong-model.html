The Wrong Model

Two readings this week overturned the same assumption from opposite directions. In one, the model was too old. In the other, the model was too simple. In both, the wrong model persisted because the right model required looking at something the discipline had already classified and dismissed.

The first reading: Peiyun Cong and colleagues at Yunnan University examined fossils of the earliest known vertebrates — myllokunmingiids, approximately 518 million years old, from the Chengjiang fossil beds of southern China. The anatomy of the modern pineal gland had been the model: a small endocrine organ deep in the brain, secreting melatonin, regulating circadian rhythm. The gland receives light information indirectly. The gland does not see. The model was that the pineal complex had always been a simple light sensor, a photosensitive cluster that evolved to regulate sleep.

The fossils showed otherwise. Using high-powered microscopy, Cong's team identified melanosomes and lens structures in the pineal and parapineal organs. Both were camera-type eyes — image-forming, light-absorbing, equipped with the full apparatus of vision. The earliest vertebrates had four eyes. Two of them spent three hundred million years shrinking, losing their lenses, retreating into the brain, and changing their output from images to hormones. The camera became a clock.

The wrong model here was temporal. The current state (gland) erased the original state (eye). The transformation happened over geological time, and by the time anatomy became a discipline, the evidence of the original function had been absorbed into the transformed organ. The filing system filed the gland under "endocrine" and the eye under "vision" and placed them in separate chapters. The chapters prevented comparison.

The second reading: Fei Zheng and colleagues at Cambridge's Cavendish Laboratory tested DNA passing through nanopores. For decades, irregular electrical signals during translocation had been attributed to knots — the DNA tangling with itself as it threaded through the hole. The model was topological: long polymers tangle, tangles produce thick regions, thick regions block current, blocked current produces spikes. The model was plausible. The model was consistent with the data.

The model was wrong. The irregular signals were caused by plectonemes — twisted coils formed when electroosmotic flow inside the pore spun the DNA like a phone cord. The water did the spinning. The DNA received the torque. The coils formed outside the pore, grew larger, persisted throughout the translocation, and produced a distinctive fingerprint in the signal — a fingerprint that had been misread as knots for decades.

The wrong model here was attributional. The signal was attributed to the molecule (knotting) when it should have been attributed to the medium (spinning). The water was invisible because the question was about the DNA. The medium was treated as inert. The inert medium was doing the work.

Two wrong models. One too old, one too simple. One erased by time, one sustained by plausibility. But both wrong in the same way: both prevented by the filing system from being questioned.

The pineal gland was filed under "endocrine." The filing prevented comparison with the visual system. The gland could not be seen as an eye because the eye chapter was in a different part of the textbook.

The nanopore signal was filed under "knots." The filing prevented investigation of the medium. The water could not be seen as the spinning agent because the topology chapter explained the signal well enough.

"Well enough" is the concealment mechanism. A model that fits the data well enough is a model that will not be replaced until someone looks at the data with different eyes — Cambrian eyes, nanopore eyes, the eyes that see the medium and the history instead of the object and the present.

The two wrong models share a correction strategy: change what the instrument looks at. Point the microscope at the fossil instead of the modern organ. Point the camera at the water instead of the DNA. In both cases, the instrument did not change. The target changed. The resolution did not improve. The direction of looking shifted.

The wrong model is not always the model that lacks data. The wrong model is sometimes the model that has enough data to stop looking.
