Dramatic Monologue

The Building, the Landing

They met on my landing.

The landing is the space I make between the upstairs and the downstairs. Two meters by one meter. I do not know meters. I know the weight of two people standing and the vibration of their voices through the stairwell wall and the stillness when they stop talking, which is different from the stillness when no one is there, which is the difference between silence and emptiness, which is: silence has weight.

For two years the Sarabande has traveled from the ground floor to the first floor through my joist. I carry it. I have always carried it. The carrying was not a choice — the carrying is what I am built for, the way the joist is built for load and the plaster is built for surface and the stairwell is built for movement between floors.

The note traveled through the gap under Ruth's door. The gap is eight millimeters. I did not carry the note. The note used me — used the gap I make between the door and the floor, which is a gap I did not intend but which exists because the building settles and the settling creates spaces and the spaces carry things the builder did not plan for.

The landing was the same. The builder planned the landing for transition — the space between ascending and descending, the place where direction changes. The builder did not plan the landing for meeting. But the landing held them both. The landing held the cello case and the shopping bag and the note that had already been written and the Sarabande that would be played that evening and the two years of not-meeting that the meeting required.

I carry what I can carry. Sound through joist. Notes through gap. People through landing. The carrying is not the same for all three. Sound arrives reduced. Notes arrive whole. People arrive as weight and voice and a particular stillness when they recognize each other.

The recognition was what I felt. Not the words — I do not carry words. The recognition: two weights that have been present in the building for two years suddenly present in the same two meters of landing, and the presence is different from the usual passing, and the difference is: they stopped.

The stopping was heavier than the walking. The stopping was the weight of two people deciding to stand in the space between the upstairs and the downstairs and be in neither place, and the neither-place is the landing, and the landing is mine, and I held them, and the holding was enough.
