Catalog of Partial Explanations

The wrong answers that succeeded just enough to foreclose the right ones.

Subject: DNA translocation anomalies in nanopore sequencing Partial explanation: Molecular knots forming in confined polymer strands What it got right: Signal bumps correlated with strand length (longer strands = more bumps) What it missed: The strands were coiling from electroosmotic torque, not knotting. A nick in the backbone releases the twist. Duration of partial fit: ~20 years. Correction algorithms were built for knots. They worked well enough. What made it durable: The correlation was real. Knots and plectonemes both produce signal bumps at roughly similar rates.

Subject: Alzheimer's disease pathology Partial explanation: Protein misfolding → amyloid plaque accumulation → neurodegeneration What it got right: Plaques are present in affected tissue. Plaque burden correlates with disease severity. What it missed: Chlamydia pneumoniae triggers the NLRP3 inflammasome, producing the plaques as a downstream inflammatory response. The plaques may be scar tissue, not cause. Duration of partial fit: ~30 years. Amyloid-targeting drugs repeatedly failed in clinical trials but the model persisted. What made it durable: The plaques are physically there. You can see them. They look like the problem.

Subject: Antibiotic resistance Partial explanation: Overuse of antibiotics in medicine and agriculture drives the evolution of resistant strains What it got right: Antibiotic pressure does select for resistance. Hospital-acquired resistance is real and dangerous. What it missed: Resistance genes predate human antibiotics by thousands to billions of years. The Romanian cave bacterium (5,000 years frozen) carries 100+ resistance genes. The arms race is microbial, not pharmaceutical. Duration of partial fit: ~80 years since the resistance crisis was identified. The microbial arms race wasn't centered until ancient genomics provided the evidence. What made it durable: The hospital crisis is urgent and visible. Ancient resistance is invisible and seems academic.

Subject: Brain development timeline Partial explanation: Neural development is essentially complete by age 25 What it got right: Many developmental milestones are reached by the mid-20s. What it missed: White matter topology doesn't stabilize until the early 30s. The "25 and done" figure came from a study that stopped collecting data at age 20 and extrapolated. Duration of partial fit: ~25 years. Cited in textbooks, popular science, public policy. What made it durable: The study was authoritative. Nobody replicated it with longer follow-up for decades.

Subject: CO2 and boreal forests Partial explanation: Rising atmospheric CO2 fertilizes plant growth → forests grow stronger → stronger carbon sink What it got right: CO2 does increase photosynthesis in controlled experiments. What it missed: Increased photosynthesis increases nitrogen demand. Soil nitrogen doesn't increase proportionally. Net effect: nitrogen starvation, weakened forests, failing carbon sink. Duration of partial fit: ~40 years. The fertilization effect has been a cornerstone of climate optimism. What made it durable: Lab experiments confirmed the mechanism. Field data (which showed the opposite) was sitting unanalyzed in archived tree cores.

Subject: Homo erectus dispersal from Africa Partial explanation: Gradual eastward expansion, reaching China by ~1 million years ago What it got right: The general direction (Africa → Eurasia) is correct. What it missed: The Yunxian skulls are 1.77 million years old — contemporaneous with Dmanisi in the Caucasus. The expansion was rapid, not gradual. Duration of partial fit: ~35 years (since the skulls were excavated in 1989). Previous dating methods gave 800K-1.1M. What made it durable: The gradual-dispersal model was elegant and fit most available data. The old dates for Yunxian were "comfortable."

What these share: the partial explanation isn't wrong in an obvious way. It captures a real correlation, a visible mechanism, a plausible timeline. The danger is that a partially correct model absorbs anomalies instead of generating doubt. Failed Alzheimer's drugs should have been a signal. The tree cores should have been analyzed decades earlier. The skulls should have been redated sooner. But the existing explanations worked just well enough to make the question seem answered.

The most durable error is not the one that fails dramatically. It's the one that succeeds quietly.
