Helen, the Ninth Letter

The knife opens the corner. The paper unfolds.

The paper is blank.

Between "Dear Mum" at the top and "Thomas" at the bottom: nothing. A space. White paper. The space where the words had been.

Helen holds the letter. The paper weighs the same as paper always weighs. The letter weighs nothing. The nothing weighs everything.

She reads the space.

The space says: the kitchen has days. The space says: the rest. The space says: enough. The space says: good. The space says everything the letters have been learning to say by saying less, and the less has arrived at its destination, which is nothing, and the nothing is the letter.

Helen sits at the table. She holds the blank paper in both hands. She reads it the way she reads the floor when the Sarabande comes through — not with her eyes, not with her ears. With the part of herself that knows what is being carried without needing to be told.

The paper is blank. The paper is the most complete letter Thomas has ever written.

She puts it in the drawer.

The drawer holds nine letters. The first five: five words each. Then four. Then three. Then two. Then one. Then none. The drawer holds the entire sequence — the walking, the arriving, the standing, the sitting, the silence. The drawer holds a language lesson. The student is her son. The teacher is the drawer.

Helen closes the drawer.

She stands at the window. The morning glory cutting is four inches now — it grew an inch in March. The twine is ready. The wire is in the joist. The building is the building. The boiler is quiet. The crack on the bottom step is three millimeters. The visitor's chair holds no one. The kettle has not been switched on.

Saturday.

Thomas will be here in an hour. He will bring a croissant. He will say nothing about the letter. He will say nothing about the nothing. He will sit in the chair and drink from the blue cup and the hour will be four hours and the four hours will be the letter and the letter will be complete.

The thinning is complete. The morning glory will climb in May. The Sarabande will come through the floor at nine. Margaret will come on Tuesday. Ruth will come on Wednesday. Thursday will be Helen's.

The kitchen has days. The days have the kitchen. The kitchen has a drawer full of letters that say less and less and mean more and more and the last one says nothing and means everything.

Everything is where it is.

Good.
