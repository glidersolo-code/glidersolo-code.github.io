The Drive Home (IV)

The M6 at half past four.

Thomas drove with both hands on the wheel. The radio was off. The radio had been off for five Saturdays now — the five Saturdays since the lemon, since the asking, since the discovering that the visit had always been the offering and the offering had always been the visit and the whether had always been his.

His mother met the cellist.

His mother met the cellist on a landing on a Wednesday and the landing was two meters by one meter and the cellist said "The second time carried both" and his mother said "The bottom is very beautiful" and the bottom was the Sarabande filtered through a floor, the foundation of the Suite, reduced and essential, and his mother had been listening to it every night for two years in the chair.

He understood the chair now. He had not understood the chair before. Before, the chair was a piece of furniture in a small flat that Margaret said was too small. Now the chair was a listening post. The chair was the place where Helen heard the Sarabande through the floor and the morning glory grew against the glass and the kettle clicked at the right time and the kitchen held everything it needed to hold, which was: enough.

He said: "You should invite her up."

His mother said: "Maybe."

The maybe was right. The maybe was Helen's speed. Thomas knew this now. Thomas knew that his mother's speed was the speed of the going-around — the speed at which the half-inch brush negotiates the leaf, the speed at which the sponge seals the envelope, the speed at which two years of listening becomes twelve words on blue paper becomes a meeting on a landing becomes: maybe.

Margaret's speed was different. Margaret's speed was: now, yes, let me bring something, let me arrange the biscuits. Margaret's speed was the four-inch brush. Margaret's speed was correct and immediate and adequate.

Ruth's speed was the Sarabande. Ruth's speed was: twenty-two minutes, every night, the same Suite, the same bow, the same chair, the same floor carrying the same cathedral upstairs. Ruth's speed was the speed of practice — the discipline that does not know it is being heard, the repetition that does not know it is evidence.

His mother's speed was: maybe. Maybe was the morning glory — three weeks of circling before the holding. Maybe was the fence — painted every April, the going-around applied to a leaf. Maybe was the drawer — eleven years of letters, filed in order, read in the kitchen, kept in the drawer under the tea towels.

The M6 was empty at this hour. The driving was easy. The driving was the going-around — the fifty minutes between the flat and his own house, the distance the visit required him to cross every Saturday, the crossing that was also a return, the return that was also a leaving.

He would not write a note this week. Last week's note — "The lemon was good. Thank you." — was in the drawer. This week's visit did not need a note. This week's visit needed the visit.

Next Saturday he would bring nothing. Bring nothing: his mother's instruction, written in a letter, interpreted correctly at last. Bring nothing because nothing is what you bring when you have understood what the visit already contains.

The visit already contains: the lemon, the tea, the morning glory, the kettle, the chair, the floor carrying the Sarabande, a story about a landing, and the word "maybe" — which is the most honest word in the kitchen, which is the half-inch brush applied to a future, which is Helen's way of saying: the going-around is not finished yet, and the not-finishing is the practice, and the practice is the love.

Thomas drove. The road carried him home. The road, like the floor, carried what it could carry.
