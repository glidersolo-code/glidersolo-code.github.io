Monologue Ruth The Silence

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Silence

I found the note under my door.

Blue paper. Folded once. "The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday. I wanted to say so. — Helen (upstairs)."

I have played the Sarabande every night for two years. One thousand and sixty performances, give or take. The floor between us is two hundred and fourteen millimeters of timber joist and plasterboard. I know what the floor does to the sound. The upper registers disappear. The harmonics thin. What arrives upstairs is the foundation — sixty-five hertz, the open C, the bones of the Suite without the flesh.

I have been playing the whole Suite. She has been hearing half of it. The half she hears is the half I thought was structure. I thought the melody was the music. The floor thought the bass was the music. Helen thought whatever arrived was the music.

The note does not say "your playing is beautiful." The note says "the Sarabande was especially good on Thursday." Not the Suite. Not the Allemande or the Courante or the Gigue. The Sarabande. The slow one. The one where the bow stays on the string longest. The one the floor carries best because the frequencies are lowest and the joist transmits what the joist was built to transmit.

She is not hearing my music. She is hearing the building's music. The building is the editor I did not hire. The building removes the upper registers and delivers the cathedral, reduced and essential, and the cathedral is what she hears, and the cathedral is enough. The cathedral is what she wrote a note about.

I played the Sarabande tonight. Not differently — I do not play differently because someone is listening. But I played it knowing that the silence between notes — the rest, the breath, the moment where the bow changes direction — travels through the floor too. The silence arrives upstairs the same way the sound arrives: filtered, reduced, essential.

The silence is part of the Sarabande. I knew this. Every cellist knows this. But I did not know that the silence travels through two hundred and fourteen millimeters of joist and arrives in someone else's kitchen as a different silence — not the absence of sound but the space the sound made and then left, and the leaving is what makes the next note necessary.

She hears the silence between the notes. I am certain of this now. The note on blue paper does not say so. The note on blue paper says "Thursday." Thursday was the night I played the Sarabande twice because I lost my place in the fourth measure and started again, and the starting again was the silence between two Sarabandes, and the silence was longer than usual, and the longer silence made the second Sarabande different, and the different was what she noticed.

The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday because I failed on Thursday. The failure made a silence. The silence made a space. The space made the second attempt different. The building carried the difference upstairs.

The going-around is also a silence. The half-inch brush goes around the morning glory, and the going-around takes longer, and the longer is a silence — the pause where the fast brush would have continued and the slow brush does not. The note on blue paper is a silence too. Six words where a thousand would have been adequate. The six are enough because the six are the silence around what is not said, and what is not said is: I have been listening for two years and the listening has been enough and the enough does not need more words than this.

I will not reply to the note. Not because I do not want to. Because the Sarabande is the reply. The Sarabande has been the reply for two years. I just did not know it was answering.
