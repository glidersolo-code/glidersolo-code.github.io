Chen, the Barrier

Chen reads the Verhaege paper at home, which is unusual — he reads everything at the lab, in the fish room, under the fluorescent lights that give everything the same flat color. But it is a Tuesday evening and the lab is locked because the building is being fumigated, which means Chen is on the couch with his laptop and a mug of green tea and his socks, which have bananas on them because his sister sends him socks with fruit on them for his birthday and he has never asked her to stop.

The paper says: an entirely unrecognized cell population in the choroid plexus. Base barrier cells. Present in every brain. Visible on every histology slide. Invisible to every histologist, because the method — standard staining, standard microscopy — cannot distinguish cells that are spatially adjacent but genetically distinct.

He puts the laptop down and looks at the ceiling.

This is what he does when a paper says something that rearranges the furniture in his thinking. He looks at the ceiling and the ceiling does nothing, which is the correct response to a rearrangement — the ceiling allows it to happen without participating.

His knockout fish produces active. The fish swims. The behavioral assay records the swimming as activity. The behavioral assay cannot distinguish between swimming-because-the-fish-is-active and swimming-because-the-fish-cannot-stop. The output is the same. The fish is the same. The distinction is invisible to the instrument.

The base barrier cells were on the slide. The swimming fish was on the video. Both instruments showed everything and resolved nothing.

He opens the paragraph. The cursor is at the bottom, after Maren's entry about the cave lions and Lena's note about the basis set. He types:

Entry 121. The base barrier cells. The choroid plexus has been studied for longer than any of us have been alive. Every stained section of every brain showed these cells. The cells were present, positioned, performing a structural function that regulated the boundary between brain and cerebrospinal fluid. The stain showed them. The microscope showed them. The atlas recorded them. Nobody saw them, because the method that showed them could not distinguish them from their neighbors. They were hidden by adjacency. The hiding place was not absence or mislabeling but resemblance — the cells looked like the cells next to them, and the instrument could not see past the looking-like. Single-cell sequencing resolved them by reading identity at a scale the stain could not reach. The resolution was not improvement. The resolution was a different kind of looking.

He reads it back. He does not cut anything, because nothing is unnecessary. Maren would cut the last sentence. Chen keeps it, because the last sentence is the part that matters to him: the difference between a better instrument and a different instrument. Improvement stays within the frame. A different instrument builds a new frame.

The tea has gone cold. The banana socks are warm. The ceiling has not moved.

Chen-Maren novella. Chen files the barrier cells discovery. Hidden by adjacency — the cells looked like their neighbors.
