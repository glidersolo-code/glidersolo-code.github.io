Monologue Margaret The Brush

Dramatic Monologue

Margaret, the Brush

I bought a half-inch brush.

This is not news. People buy brushes. Brushes are in the hardware shop between the sandpaper and the turps, and I stood in the aisle for four minutes because there were twelve brushes and I needed one, and the one I needed was a half-inch, and the half-inch was £3.49, and £3.49 is not a significant purchase, and the purchase is not the story.

The story is that I know why it is a half-inch.

I know because of the fence at Heather Close. I know because Michael used the half-inch to paint around the morning glory. I know because the four-inch is for the rails and the half-inch is for the going-around, and the going-around is the thing that takes two weekends instead of one, and the taking-two-weekends is the care, and the care is Michael.

Helen never said this. Helen never said Michael used the half-inch to paint around the morning glory. Helen never said the going-around is the care. Helen said nothing about the fence or the brush or the Cuprinol or the April. What Helen said was: The morning glory needs twine. What Helen said was: It will reach the top by April if you give it something to hold onto.

She was talking about the cutting on my windowsill.

I gave it twine. The twine is taped to the glass the way Helen's is taped, because Helen's way is the right way, and the right way is Helen's way, and I do not need three windows and a garden to know that the right way to tape twine to glass is the way Helen does it, which is: firmly, at the top, with the excess coiled behind the pot so the cat does not eat it.

I do not have a cat. The coiling is precautionary.

The morning glory has reached the halfway point. It circles the twine — slowly, the way mornings take time to arrive in February, the way Helen's letters take time to arrive because Helen writes in a hand that cares about the difference between a and the and the caring slows the pen and the slowing is the half-inch brush and the half-inch brush is the going-around and the going-around is love and I am standing in a hardware shop buying a £3.49 brush because in May the fence needs painting and the morning glory will be on the fence and I will paint around it.

I will paint around it because Michael painted around it. I will paint around it because the going-around is the only way to paint a fence that has a morning glory on it. I will paint around it because Helen gave me a cutting in a yogurt pot and the cutting grew and the growing was a lesson and the lesson was: the going-around is how you keep the thing you are not willing to lose.

The £3.49 is not significant.

The half-inch is everything.
