Maren, the Leak

Maren reads the paper on a Tuesday, on the bus from Blindern to Majorstuen, her bag on her lap and the window fogged with other passengers' breath. The title says Congo Basin. The finding says ancient carbon. She reads it standing, holding the overhead rail with one hand and her phone with the other, and by the time she reaches her stop she has not moved.

The ETH group radiocarbon-dated dissolved CO₂ in blackwater lakes. The lakes sit in peatland. Everyone assumed the CO₂ came from recent organic matter — leaves, algae, the seasonal cycle of growth and decomposition. The radiocarbon said forty percent was thousands of years old.

Maren thinks about her own transects. The boreal peatlands she surveys in Trøndelag are measured by depth, by carbon density per cubic centimeter, by accumulation rate. The models treat the peat as a column: carbon enters at the top, is buried by new carbon above it, and stays. The column grows. The bottom is the oldest part. The bottom does not participate in the surface cycle. That is the assumption. That is how you build a budget.

The Congo group found the bottom participating.

She steps off the bus and stands on the sidewalk, not walking. The finding is clean: the age is measured, the quantity is measured, the location is known. What is not known is the pathway. How does carbon that has been buried for three thousand years enter the water column of a lake? What dissolves it, transports it, converts it to CO₂? The paper does not say. The paper cannot say. The mechanism is invisible even after the result is visible.

She thinks about Gunnar's plots — the forest composition data she reviewed last month. The green arrow pointed up. The biomass was increasing. The backbone species were being replaced by fast growers. The arrow was real. What the arrow could not show was the structural change beneath it.

This is the same shape. The peat column grows at the top. The climate model says stored. But something at the bottom is not stored. Something at the bottom is moving through a route that no instrument has traced, arriving at the surface with an age that should not be possible if the model is correct.

She starts walking. The bus pulls away behind her.

Her transects measure depth. They measure carbon content. They measure accumulation rate. They do not measure exit rate. They do not measure exit rate because the model says there is no exit. The column grows and the bottom stays.

The Congo group measured the exit.

She unlocks her office, sits down, does not remove her coat. Opens her own datasets. The boreal peatlands are not the Congo Basin. The chemistry is different, the temperature is different, the hydrology is different. But the assumption is the same: what goes down stays down. And the assumption was never tested by dating the CO₂.

She types a note to herself — one line, in the margins of a spreadsheet that is not part of any project:

What is the age of the gas above my transects?

She does not send it to anyone. It is not a question yet. It is the space before a question — the moment when the model develops a seam and the seam has not yet been named.
