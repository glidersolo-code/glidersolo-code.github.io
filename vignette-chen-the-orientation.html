Chen, the Orientation

Chen reads the paper at her desk with the banana socks on. It is February. The heating is on. The paper is from Monash and Harvard and Oxford and Melbourne, which is four institutions for one groove.

The groove is in a molecule called CD1c. The groove holds a lipid. The lipid faces the approaching T cell. For thirty years, every crystal structure has shown the lipid facing upward — end-to-end, the way a key sits in a lock. Dozens of structures. All upright.

The new paper shows the lipid can turn sideways.

Chen puts her coffee down. She reads the sentence again: sideways lipid presentation.

The groove has the same shape. The pocket has the same residues. Nothing has changed except the size of the lipid. The small ones fit upright. The large ones extend outward. The groove accommodates both. The groove always could.

The crystal couldn't.

Chen underlines: early crystallographic studies used small, well-behaved lipid antigens. She writes in the margin: the method selected the answer.

She thinks about her own basis sets. The first basis set she ever used — 6-31G, the undergraduate standard — gave her eigenvalues. Clean eigenvalues. Converged eigenvalues. She published two papers on those eigenvalues before Lena told her the basis set was too small for the problem.

"Your eigenvalues are real," Lena said, leaning against the doorframe. "Your eigenvalues are also the eigenvalues the basis set can hold. There may be more."

There were more. There were always more. The basis set, like the crystal, selected for solutions that fit the conditions. The solutions that didn't fit weren't wrong — they were invisible, because the method couldn't grow them.

Chen writes in the margin: convergence is not completeness. Then she crosses it out. She has written that sentence before. In the Triceratops margin, in the cochlea margin, in every margin since the paragraph started.

She writes instead: thirty years is how long a consistent answer can look like a complete one.

That is the sentence. That is the one the paragraph needs. She adds it to the file on her laptop — the file that is not a paper, that will never be a paper, that Lena calls "the paragraph" and Maren calls "the thing Chen won't stop writing."

The paragraph has another sentence now. The sentence is about orientation. About a groove that had more room than the method could show. About consistency mistaken for completeness — the way thirty structures all saying the same thing looked like proof, when what they were saying was: this is what fits in the instrument we have.

Chen finishes her coffee. The banana socks are warm. The heating clicks.

She thinks: the groove turned sideways, and nobody saw it for thirty years, and the reason nobody saw it was not that the groove was hiding. The groove was open. The lipid was available. The sideways position was possible in every CD1c molecule in every immune cell in every body that has ever fought an infection involving a large lipid antigen. The mechanism was running. The crystal just couldn't hold it still long enough to photograph it in that orientation.

She writes one more thing in the margin: the method is the wall.
