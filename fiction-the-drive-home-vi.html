The Drive Home (VI)

The M6 was quiet. A Sunday, 4 PM, the traffic thin, the road doing what the road does when the road is not full: it carries the car the way the building carries the Sarabande. Reduced. Essential. The car in the road the way the cello in the building. One vehicle. One instrument. One going-around.

Thomas was thinking about the wolf tone.

The wolf tone is the frequency where the cello's body agrees with the string. The agreeing is the disagreeing. The instrument admits it is an instrument — the body is present in its own sound, and the presence is a wobble, and the wobble is four measures out of many, and the four measures are the part where the going-around stops pretending it is smooth.

His mother had said: "I am saying it to you."

The wolf tone is welcome. She had said it about Ruth's cello. She had said it about the Suite. But then she looked at him across the kitchen table — across the bread, across the three plates, across the two cups and the one that was not there — and she said: "I am saying it to you."

Thomas turned off the M6 at Junction 15. The A500. The familiar road. The road he had driven every Saturday for — how long now? Months. Since before the letter. Since before the Sarabande. Since before the going-around had a name.

I am the wolf tone.

He had said that. In the kitchen. He had said: "The frequency where your body vibrates with the string of the son." And the saying was the wolf tone — the saying itself was the wobble, the moment where Thomas's voice disagreed with the kitchen's quiet, the four measures of a son admitting he is present in his mother's sound.

The car passed the roundabout. The chip shop. The petrol station. The row of houses where the morning glory grew on fences that were not Michael's fence but looked like Michael's fence because every fence in this part of Stoke looked like Michael's fence: panel, post, wire, the going-around applied to a boundary.

Helen had not cried. Helen does not cry. Helen does what Helen does, which is: she puts the kettle on. The kettle's whistle has a wobble. The wobble is the wolf tone of the kitchen. Michael used to wait for it. Helen waits for it. Thomas listens for it. The wolf tone of the kettle is the wolf tone of the family: the frequency where the kitchen's body vibrates with the string of the missing husband, the missing father, the man who painted around the morning glory and did not cut it back.

Thomas was on the A34 now. South. Oxford eventually. The flat with the desk and the envelopes and the letters he reads now, unsealed, Michael's handwriting becoming more legible each time because the reading was the practice and the practice was the going-around and the going-around was love and the love was what the letters had always contained.

The wolf tone is welcome.

Helen was not just talking about Ruth's cello. Helen was talking about the wobble in the visit — the four measures out of many where the Saturday disagrees with itself about whether it is Michael's Saturday or Thomas's Saturday. The Saturday where the bread is the same bread and the knife is the same knife and the chair is the same chair but the body in the chair is different and the difference is the wolf tone and the wolf tone is welcome.

The body admits it is a body. The son admits he is a son and not the husband. The kitchen admits the going-around includes a wobble. The wobble is four measures. The floor is two hundred and fourteen millimeters. The drive is ninety-seven miles.

Thomas turned the radio on. The radio played something that was not Bach. Thomas turned the radio off.

The silence in the car was the building's silence — larger than the cathedral's, larger because it included everything: the wolf tone, the bread, the three plates, the "I am saying it to you," the morning glory dormant on the fence, the kettle's whistle, the wobble, the whole Suite, the drive home.

The drive home was the going-around.

The going-around was enough.
