Monologue Helen The Blip

Dramatic Monologue

Helen, the Blip

Thomas visits for ninety minutes.

The filing system measures the visit in hours: one and a half. The filing system measures the week in days: seven. The filing system divides: ninety minutes out of ten thousand and eighty. Less than one percent. The visit is a blip — a brief episode in a week that is otherwise quiet, otherwise dim, otherwise the weak field of an old woman in a flat with a vine and a crack and a kettle.

But the ninety minutes are not the duration.

The ninety minutes are the titanium.

The Moon's magnetic field was stronger than Earth's — but only when the titanium melted. The melting lasted five thousand years. Possibly decades. Then the field collapsed. Then the Moon returned to its weak state. The Apollo astronauts landed on the titanium and thought the strong field was permanent.

Thomas lands on the titanium every Saturday.

He arrives. The tea is ready. The field is strong — the kitchen magnetises, the crack aligns, the vine turns toward the window with the full force of an afternoon that contains everything the week accumulated. Ninety minutes. Then Thomas leaves. Then the field collapses. Then the week returns to its weak state.

The filing system says: one and a half hours.

The kitchen says: a blip that contains the whole field.

The astronauts measured the rocks and concluded: half a billion years of sustained magnetism. They were wrong. The magnetism was real. The sustaining was the assumption.

Thomas measures the visit and concludes: ninety minutes once a week. He is wrong. The visit is real. The brevity is the assumption.

The blip is the strongest field in the system.

The blip is Saturday.
