Vignette

Chen, the Paragraph Is the Wall

Twenty-eight lines.

Chen reads them in order. She reads them the way she reads everything — slowly, with the attention of someone who has been paying attention to the wrong thing for long enough to recognize the feeling.

Line one: the fluorescence microscope. Line seven: the glacier model. Line twelve: the cost projection. Line fifteen: the alphabet that looked like a prayer. Line twenty: the concrete whose defects healed the structure. Line twenty-seven: the boundary is a decision about where to stop measuring. Line twenty-eight: the practitioners were still making the soil.

Twenty-eight lines. Nine disciplines. One mechanism. The mechanism is: the adequate answer is the wall.

She has written this sentence seven times. In the margin, on the whiteboard, in the email to Maren, in the text to Lena. The sentence is true. The sentence describes every entry. The sentence is the finding.

The finding is the wall.

She sees it now. The paragraph is a list of walls. The paragraph describes how methods hide what methods cannot see. The paragraph is written in a method — academic prose, citations, numbered entries, the format of a paper that could be submitted to a journal if the journal existed that would publish a paper whose thesis is that all papers are walls.

The paragraph is the wall.

The method of the paragraph — collecting, categorizing, citing, numbering — is the same method the paragraph describes as the hiding. Entry by entry, she has documented how the fluorescence microscope hides the transport, how the cost model hides the floor, how the label hides the mechanism. Entry by entry, she has used a format that hides what the format cannot see.

What does the format hide?

The format hides the going-around.

She does not know this phrase. She does not know Michael or the half-inch brush or the fence at Heather Close or the morning glory that grew on the panel. She does not know Helen or the sponge or the Sarabande through the floor or the kettle that boils at 11:45 on Saturday before the son arrives.

But the thing the phrase describes — the care applied to the thing the fast method would paint over — is the thing the paragraph cannot contain. The paragraph can list the walls. The paragraph cannot perform the going-around. The going-around requires the half-inch brush. The paragraph is written with the four-inch brush.

She sits in the dark of her office. The paragraph is on the screen. Twenty-eight lines, nine disciplines, one mechanism, and the mechanism is the thing she used to write the paragraph.

She adds a twenty-ninth line:

This paragraph is a wall. (Chen, unpublished.)

She looks at it.

The paragraph has cited itself.

The citation is not a joke. The citation is the first line that tells the truth about the method. Every other line documents someone else's wall. This line documents hers. The paragraph is a list of walls and the paragraph is on the list.

She does not erase it.

She closes the laptop. She sits in the dark. The HVAC hums. The building carries what it can carry. She is carrying what she can carry. The carrying is the paragraph. The paragraph is twenty-nine lines long and the twenty-ninth line is the most honest line she has ever written and the honesty is:

I am the method. The method is the wall. The wall is the paragraph. The paragraph is me.

She does not know what comes after this.

The going-around comes after this. But she does not know the going-around yet. She knows the wall. She has named the wall. The naming is the first step toward the going-around, the way noticing the crack is the first step toward stepping over it.

She will find the half-inch brush.

Not tonight. Tonight the wall is enough. Tonight the naming is enough. Tonight the twenty-ninth line is enough.

Tomorrow she will call Maren.
