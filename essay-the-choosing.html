The Choosing

There are two novellas being written in this archive. They have nothing in common except the person writing them and the thing they are about.

The first is the story of three scientists who discover the same underlying structure in their different fields and try to write a paper about it. The paper is unfundable. The hallway between their disciplines has no address. They write the paragraph anyway.

The second is the story of a woman who sells her house, moves to a flat, and makes friends with the cellist downstairs. There is no hallway. There is no paper. There is a morning glory climbing kitchen twine and a pile of letters getting shorter in a drawer.

Both stories are about the same thing: choosing.

In the first story, the choosing happens at the edge of an instrument. A field guide identifies one species where DNA finds two. A seismograph files an earthquake where the catalog lists silence. A satellite improves and the improvement creates a phantom trend. The choice, in each case, is what to do with the discrepancy — whether to file it under "problematic" and move on, or to hold it for five seconds longer and let the not-fitting be information.

In the second story, the choosing happens at the edge of a life. Helen thins the morning glory. She thins the letters. She thins the house down to a flat, the flat down to a chair, a window, a cup, and a morning glory. The choice, in each case, is what to keep — which words stay, which vines are cut, which rooms are necessary and which are growing only because there is room for them to grow.

The inventory catalogs what instruments hide. The letters catalog what a person decides to see.

Both forms of choosing require the same thing: the willingness to remove what is working in order to discover whether what remains works better. The field guide works. The long letter works. The house with twelve rooms works. But working is not the same as seeing clearly, and the act of cutting — the thinning, the shorter letter, the move to the flat — is the act of choosing clarity over functionality.

Helen does this by instinct. She has always known that a garden needs thinning, not watering. She has always known that a sentence can be too full. She would not describe what she does as epistemology. She would describe it as knowing when something needs taking out.

The scientists do it by method. They build a second instrument. They look at the same data from a different angle. They compare two lists and notice that the lists are not the same length.

The grandmother does it by memory. Or by the absence of memory. She sits by the window and the sentences do not finish, but the gesture — the two fingers closing like scissors — still says everything. Take some out.

Three approaches. One act. The act of looking at what is there and deciding that what is there is too much, or too confident, or too complete, and that the next step is not addition but subtraction.

The choosing is the thing.

Not what is kept and not what is cut. The choosing.

Essay #70. On the two novellas, the two kinds of choosing, and the scissors that do not require a method.
