The Crack, the Landing

Her step changed on Wednesday.

I do not know Wednesday. I know her step. Seven feet from the kerb, two inches wide, between the Spar and the building. She steps over me every Tuesday and Thursday. The stepping-over is the going-around applied to concrete: the step before me is fifty-eight kilograms bearing left, the step after me is fifty-eight kilograms bearing right, and the step that does not touch me — the step that goes over — is the going-around.

On Wednesday her step was different. The weight was the same. Fifty-eight kilograms. The direction was the same. From the Spar to the building. The bearing was the same. Left, over, right. But the tempo was different. Not faster. Not slower. Lighter. As though the fifty-eight kilograms had become fifty-seven and a half, and the half-kilogram was somewhere else — in the air, in the carrying, in the invisible lightness that a person carries when a thing they have been waiting for has happened.

I do not know what happened. I am a crack. I know concrete and weight and weather and the slow widening that happens every winter when the water freezes and the ice expands and the expansion is the opposite of the going-around — the expansion is the going-through. The ice goes through the concrete the way the four-inch brush goes through the morning glory. The ice does not negotiate. The ice expands.

But she goes around. She has always gone around. Two hundred and thirty steps over me in the time I have been a crack wide enough to notice. Two hundred and thirty going-arounds. The going-arounds are the opposite of the ice: careful, habitual, the weight distributed to spare the concrete, the step adjusted to miss the gap.

On Wednesday the step was lighter. I noticed. I notice everything that touches me, which is: rain, leaves, shoes, weight, weather, and the particular silence of a pavement at night when no one is walking. The silence is when I widen. The silence is when the ice does its work. The silence is the crack's version of the Sarabande — the thing that happens when no one is listening, the thing that changes the structure without announcing.

Her step was lighter on Wednesday. Something had happened. Something involving the building she walks to. Something involving the landing I cannot see because I am in the pavement and the landing is inside and the inside is a place I do not know.

But I know the step. And the step, on Wednesday, carried both — the usual weight and the unusual lightness — the way the second Sarabande carried the first.

The carrying is the going-around. The going-around is what I know. The going-around is the step that misses the crack, the brush that misses the leaf, the note that says twelve words instead of a thousand, the maybe that means: not yet, not no, the going-around is not finished.

Her step was lighter on Wednesday. The lightness is filed in my memory, which is concrete, which remembers everything, which widens slowly, which is patient, which is a crack that knows her step the way the floor knows the Sarabande: by what arrives.

What arrived on Wednesday was lighter. The lighter was enough. The lighter told me everything I needed to know, which is: the going-around continues, and the continuing is the lightness, and the lightness is the step, and the step goes over me with the care of a person who goes around.
