The Too-Small

The dust grains around R Doradus are one ten-thousandth of a millimeter across. The model said they should be large enough for starlight to push. They are not. The wind blows anyway. The carbon travels anyway. The mechanism that was supposed to explain how the atoms of life spread through the galaxy does not work at R Doradus, and the atoms spread anyway, and the spreading is the fact the model cannot account for.

"We thought we had a good idea of how the process worked. It turns out we were wrong."

The statement is unremarkable in science. The statement is unremarkable in every inventory entry that has preceded this one: the cochlea's Hopf bifurcation, the whale's syntactic structure, the bat's collective soundscape, the mycorrhizal network's bidirectional traffic, the glacier's temperate ice. In each case, the assumed mechanism was elegant, mathematically sufficient, and wrong. In each case, the actual mechanism was messier, more local, and more productive than the model predicted.

The pattern is: the explanation is smaller than the phenomenon.

Not simpler — smaller. Simpler would be a compliment. The explanation is often elegant, often beautiful, often the kind of thing that earns a textbook chapter and a lecture and a citation that persists for seventy years. The explanation is sufficient the way a map is sufficient: it covers the territory, names the features, assigns the coordinates. But the territory is larger than the map. The territory always has a feature the map did not include, and the unincluded feature is the one that determines how the thing works.

The dust at R Doradus is the map. The wind is the territory.

Glen's flow law is the map. Temperate ice is the territory.

The mycorrhizal model with its implied coordinator is the map. The bidirectional flow is the territory.

The map is not wrong. The map is too small.

This is the hidden channel's geometry: the actual mechanism exists at a scale or in a direction or at a temperature or in a relationship the map did not cover, not because the cartographer was careless but because the territory extended past the edge of the page, and the edge of the page was where the mechanism lived.

The edge of the page. The place the model stops. The place the explanation runs out of resolution and the phenomenon continues.

The phenomenon always continues.

The vine does not need to be explained. The vine grows. The vine wraps. The vine flowers. The vine goes dormant. The vine comes back. The explanation — auxin gradient, thigmotropic response, photoperiod induction — is accurate and too small. The vine is larger than its explanation. The vine's effect on the room, on the light, on the silence, on the son who visits every Saturday — these effects are real and the explanation does not contain them. The explanation contains the mechanism. The mechanism does not contain the vine.

The dust is too small to catch the starlight.

The word "Good" is too small to hold the visit.

The paragraph is too small to contain its own argument.

The too-small is not a failure. The too-small is the discovery that the carrying happens at a scale the model did not include, by a mechanism the map did not draw, through a channel the inventory had not yet cataloged.

One hundred and fifty entries. One hundred and fifty times the map stopped and the territory continued. The territory always continued.
