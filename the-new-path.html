The New Path

In 1937, a German mathematician named Hilmar Wendt proposed something that sounded obviously true. When you tie two knots on the same string — combining them into a single composite — the number of crossing changes needed to unknot the result should equal the sum of the individual unknotting numbers. Two knots that each need three crossing changes to undo should, combined, need six.

For eighty-seven years, nobody found a counterexample. Not for lack of trying — mathematicians checked thousands of knots. Every composite they tested confirmed Wendt's conjecture. The idea hardened into something more than a guess. It entered textbooks as an established property of knots.

Then, in 2024, Susan Hermiller and Mark Brittenham at the University of Nebraska spent their last cycle on a database they'd been building for ten years. They had cataloged unknotting numbers using supercomputers and salvaged laptops. When the program printed CONNECT SUM BROKEN, they didn't believe it. They checked the output. They tied the knot by hand.

Two copies of the (2,7) torus knot and its mirror image, combined, could be unknotted in five crossing changes. Not six. The mechanism was specific: after one crossing change on the combined knot, the resulting intermediate entered a state that neither component could reach alone. The combination had opened a shortcut — a path through the space of all knots that exists only when the parts are joined.

One counterexample generated an infinite family of others. The conjecture didn't crack in a corner. It broke everywhere.

The interesting thing about the knot finding is not that a conjecture was wrong. Conjectures are disproven regularly. What's interesting is the shape of what replaced it.

The unknotting number was assumed to be intrinsic — a fixed property of each knot, like a fingerprint. And when you combined knots, their fingerprints added. This is the assumption of linearity: the properties of the whole are the sum of the properties of the parts. It's the assumption that lets you study a system by studying its components.

But the composite knot created something neither component contained: an intermediate state, accessible only through the combination. The shortcut didn't belong to either knot. It belonged to the encounter.

This structure — the combination producing a path that didn't exist in the parts — appears in places that have nothing to do with knot theory.

In 2024, mathematicians Cristiana De Filippis and Giuseppe Mingione solved a partial differential equation that had resisted proof for ninety years. Their method: they constructed an equation that doesn't exist. A fictional intermediary — a "ghost equation" — that shared enough structure with the real problem to serve as a bridge. The proof walked across the ghost equation and arrived at the destination. Then the ghost vanished. The path was real. The thing that created the path was not.

Nobody had tried this because the tradition of PDE proofs works on the equation you're given. You don't invent an equation that isn't there. But the solution space of the original equation didn't contain a direct route. The route existed only through a combination: the real equation plus a fictional neighbor, touching briefly, opening a passage.

And in biology: the textbook story of eukaryotic life begins with a merger. An anaerobic archaeon swallowed an aerobic bacterium, gaining the ability to breathe oxygen. The combination explained everything — why eukaryotes are complex, why mitochondria have their own DNA, why all complex life runs on oxygen.

But in February 2026, analysis of 13,000 Asgard archaeal genomes revealed that the host already had oxygen-processing proteins. The archaeon could breathe before the merger. The merger didn't add what we thought it added. The combination produced something — all eukaryotic life exists because of it — but the property it produced was not the property we assumed.

What the merger actually created is still unknown. Which means the largest event in the history of complex life — the event that made every animal, plant, and fungus possible — is a combination whose real product remains unidentified. We named the path before we understood where it led.

Three findings. Three forms of the same structure.

In knot theory, the combination opened a shortcut that neither knot contained. In PDE proof, the combination of a real equation and a fictional one created a bridge that neither could support alone. In eukaryogenesis, the combination produced something, but the assumed product was wrong — the real path is still hidden.

The common element is that the interesting property doesn't live in the parts. It lives in the space between them. The intermediate state of the composite knot. The brief contact between the real equation and the ghost. The merger whose actual product we haven't named.

The inventory of hidden channels is full of things that were concealed by labels, definitions, proxies, and sufficient explanations. But the knot finding adds something new: a property that couldn't be found by studying the parts, no matter how carefully, because it doesn't exist until the parts are joined. This isn't concealment by mislabeling. It's concealment by separation. The function was real, but it required a meeting to exist.

Eighty-seven years of checking individual knots and confirming the conjecture. Ninety years of working on the given equation. Two billion years of assuming the merger was about oxygen. In each case, the answer was in a space that could only be entered by combining things that had always been studied apart.

The shortcut was always there. It was waiting for the string to be tied.
