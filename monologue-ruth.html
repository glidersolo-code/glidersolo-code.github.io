Monologue Ruth

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth

I play from nine to ten. I have always played from nine to ten. Before this flat it was a different flat and before that flat it was a different city and the hour was the same. Nine to ten. You choose one thing and you keep it.

I chose the Third Suite first. In the old city. I was twenty-three and the Third Suite was the first one I learned to play without the score and the not-needing-the-score was the point. The score is a set of instructions. The memory is a conversation. When you play from memory you are not reading a letter. You are saying what you already know.

The flat is on the ground floor. I did not choose it for the acoustics. I chose it because the rent was what I could pay and the landlord did not ask what I did for a living. He asked if I had references. I had references. The references said quiet tenant. This is the funniest thing I know about myself.

The floor above me has Helen. Helen arrived after I did. I heard her moving in — the boxes, the kettle, the sound of someone putting things in exactly the places she had already decided they would go. Efficient. I knew from the moving that she would not complain about the cello.

She did not complain about the cello.

We met at the bins. She said: I hear you playing. I said: I'm sorry if it's loud. She said: It isn't loud. She said it the way you say a fact that does not require an opinion attached to it. It isn't loud. The loudness was not what she was commenting on.

We have tea on Thursdays now. She comes down or I go up. The tea is always the same. The conversation is always short and good. Helen does not fill silence. Helen knows what silence is for. I have played for people who talk through music and I have played for people who listen through walls and the difference is not attention — both are paying attention — the difference is what they think the attention is for. The talkers think attention is a performance. Helen thinks attention is a room you enter.

Her son came once. Thomas. He sat in the chair and listened to the Third Suite through the floor. He did not say anything for the whole Sarabande. When it ended he said that's the best part. He did not mean the Sarabande. He meant the floor. The reduction. The version that comes through eight inches of wood and arrives as vibration, stripped of the overtones, stripped of the upper register, carrying only the foundation. He was right. The best version of the Third Suite is the one you hear through a floor, because the floor edits it the way Helen edits a letter — removes the excess, keeps the structure, delivers the weight.

He came again on Saturday. I played the Fourth Suite. The Fourth is harder. The Prelude is long — five minutes, six if you breathe — and it circles. It circles the way you walk through a house you are leaving, touching the doorframes, checking the light in each room, not looking for anything, just being finished with the looking. Bach wrote it for an instrument that may not have existed yet. He wrote the music before the instrument. The music required the instrument. The instrument was built to hold the music.

I played it and the floor carried it and Helen was above me with her morning glory and her kettle and her son and I could not hear them but I knew they were listening the way I know the floor is there — not by seeing it, not by testing it, just by the fact that the sound goes somewhere and something receives it.

I play for the person I was before I moved here. That is what I told Helen once, when she asked. It was true then. It may still be true. But the person I was before I moved here did not have a ceiling above her that listened.

Now I play for both.
