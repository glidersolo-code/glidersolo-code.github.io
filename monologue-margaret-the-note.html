Dramatic Monologue

Margaret, the Note

Helen told me about the note. Not directly. Helen does not tell things directly. Helen said, "Ruth played the Sarabande twice on Thursday," and the way she said "twice" told me the rest.

I said, "Did you say something to her?"

Helen said, "I wrote a note."

I wanted to say: finally. I wanted to say: you have been listening to that woman play for two years through a floor and you have said nothing and the saying-nothing was very Helen and the very-Helen was driving me slightly mad. But I did not say this. I said, "What did you write?"

"The Sarabande was especially good on Thursday."

Twelve words. Twelve words after two years of listening. I would have written twelve words after twelve minutes. I would have knocked on the door. I would have brought something — a jar, a card, a plate of something. Helen brought twelve words on blue paper, folded once, pushed under a door.

This is the difference between Helen and me. Helen reduces. I accumulate. Helen's flat has a morning glory and a kettle and a chair and enough. My kitchen has three windows and a garden that needs something every weekend and the needing is what I am for. I buy the half-inch brush because the half-inch brush is the right brush and the right brush is what Margaret does — Margaret finds the right thing, the correct thing, the appropriate thing.

But Helen writes twelve words.

And the twelve words are more than my three windows and my garden that needs something. The twelve words are what I would have made into forty, and the forty would have been correct and the correct would have been too much, and the too much would have been the four-inch brush applied to a conversation that needed a half-inch.

Thomas wrote back too. Helen told me this also not directly. She said, "Thomas liked the lemon," and the way she said "liked" told me the rest of that too. Six words on ordinary paper. "The lemon was good. Thank you."

She is surrounded by people who speak in single digits. Thomas sends six words. Ruth sends the Sarabande. The morning glory sends a leaf against the glass. I send paragraphs and opinions and casseroles and the half-inch brush I found at the Spar for £3.49.

I think my paragraphs are my going-around. Not Helen's going-around — Helen goes around with silence. Not Ruth's — Ruth goes around with sixty-five hertz. Margaret goes around with words. Too many words, and the too-many is the going-around, because the too-many means I am circling the thing I actually mean, which is: Helen, you are the bravest person I know, and the bravery is not the twelve words, the bravery is the two years of listening before the twelve words, and the listening was the going-around applied to a relationship, and the relationship is now a door with a note under it, and the door is different.

I did not say any of this. I said, "Good."

One word. For Margaret, one word is the silence.
