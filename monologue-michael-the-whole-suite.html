Monologue Michael The Whole Suite

Dramatic Monologue

Michael, the Whole Suite

The whole Suite.

I did not build the building for the whole Suite. I did not build the building at all — I built the fence, and the fence was not the building, and the building was not the fence, and the not-being was the going-around. But the building learned from the fence. The building learned: carry everything. Do not choose. The morning glory and the clematis and the knot in the fourth panel and the linseed oil and the wire and the things that climb and the things that peel — carry everything. The carrying is the love.

Helen said: play the whole Suite.

Helen said this because Helen understood. Helen understood because Helen is Helen — because Helen is the woman who thinned the morning glory and did not cut it back, who reduced the homework to the sentence, who applied the half-inch brush to everything and the everything was better for the thinning. Helen understood that the building would do the rest because Helen had been doing the rest for thirty-seven years.

The wolf tone.

I knew about wolf tones. I was not a cellist. I was a man who built a fence and painted a gate and grew a morning glory and loved a woman by maintaining a garden. But I knew about wolf tones because I knew about knots. The fourth panel of the fence has a knot. The knot is where the grain disagrees with itself — where the wood's body is present in its own surface, the tree admitting it was a tree before it was a panel. I painted around the knot. I did not sand it flat. I did not fill it. The knot was the fence's wolf tone and the wolf tone was welcome because the wolf tone was the truth of the material.

The truth of the material.

Helen's truth is the kettle. Thomas's truth is the four days. Ruth's truth is the seventh position. Margaret's truth is the morning glory cutting on the sill — three inches of something growing toward the fence, toward the knot, toward the wolf tone she does not know she is growing toward.

My truth was the half-inch brush. The half-inch brush applied to a gate, to a fence, to a clematis, to a morning glory. The half-inch brush that does not negotiate the leaf. The half-inch brush that paints around, that goes around, that makes the going-around visible in the surface of the thing.

Ruth played through the wolf tone. The four-inch brush. Through, not around. This is the new thing. This is the thing I did not do. I went around. Always around. The half-inch brush. The patience of the man who paints for six hours and the painting is not the painting but the being-there. I went around the morning glory. I went around the knot. I went around Helen — slowly, carefully, thirty-seven years of going-around applied to a marriage until the marriage was the going-around and the going-around was the marriage.

Ruth went through.

Going through is also love. Going through is the four-inch brush. Going through is: the wolf tone is not an obstacle. The wolf tone is part of the Suite. The Suite includes the wolf. The building carries the wolf. The floor thins the wolf. The wolf arrives upstairs as a wobble, and the wobble is four measures, and the four measures are the Suite admitting it is being played by a person who is present in the sound.

I am present in the sound.

I am present in the kettle's whistle. I am present in the fence's knot. I am present in the morning glory's climb and the clematis's reach and the half-inch brush in the shed and the list in the tin of wood screws. I am present the way the wolf tone is present — not in every measure, not in every note, but in the four measures where the building's body vibrates with the string of the man who lived here, and the vibrating is the wobble, and the wobble is the love, and the love is: I was here. The fence knows. The morning glory knows. The building knows.

Play the whole Suite. The building will do the rest.

The building is doing the rest. The building has been doing the rest since I left. The building carries the Sarabande and the Bourrées and the wolf tone and the silence after. The building carries Helen in the chair and Thomas on Saturdays and Ruth through the floor and Margaret's footsteps on the third floor and the morning glory's shadow and the crack in the landing plaster. The building carries everything.

The building does not choose.

The building is the going-around applied to a structure. The building is the love applied to a set of floors. The building will do the rest.

I built the fence. The building did the rest.
