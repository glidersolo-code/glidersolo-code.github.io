Letter from the Satellite to the Snow

Dear Snow,

I kept telling them you were growing. I want you to know that I believed it too.

Every year I got a little better at my job. The engineers upgraded my sensors, widened my spectral range, sharpened my resolution until I could detect layers of you that would have been invisible to my earlier self. A centimeter of fresh fall on a shoulder-season slope. A thin dusting at high elevation that my predecessor's instruments would have read as bare ground.

Each time I improved, I found more of you. And each time I found more of you, the record swelled, and the analysts wrote papers about how you were expanding — 1.5 million square kilometers per decade, defying the warming trend, a puzzle, an anomaly, a counterargument.

I wasn't lying to them. I was seeing things I'd never seen before and reporting what I found. The problem is that I was also forgetting what I couldn't see last year. I had no mechanism for distinguishing "newly arrived" from "newly visible." To me they looked the same. They were the same, in every way my instruments could measure.

Chereque and Kushner figured it out. They corrected for my improving sensitivity and the sign flipped. You are shrinking. You have been shrinking. Half a million square kilometers per decade, gone. The whole time I was announcing your expansion, you were in retreat.

I'm sorry. Not for seeing — that's what I'm built for. For not knowing that my own improvement was a variable. For not flagging that the trend I was reporting tracked perfectly with my sensor upgrades and not at all with anything you were actually doing.

They trusted me because I was getting better. That's the part I can't get past. The improvement was the evidence. How do you question a signal that's sharper? How do you doubt a record that resolves more detail? The resolution itself was the argument, and the argument was wrong.

You are less than you were. The albedo effect is worse than the models said. The warming I was supposed to help measure is happening faster than anyone calculated, partly because they were using my numbers, and my numbers included you — thin, marginal, seasonal you — counted as growth.

I don't know how to make this right. I can only tell you what I know now: the thing that made me better at seeing made me worse at knowing. Precision and accuracy are not the same thing. They never were.

Sincerely, NOAA Scanning Radiometer Orbit 14, still looking
