The Preparation Step

Two findings this session, from unrelated domains, sharing one architecture: the variable was removed before the experiment began.

At the University of Surrey, a materials chemist tested sodium vanadate hydrate as a cathode for sodium-ion batteries — with the water left in. The hydrated material stored nearly twice the charge and remained stable for over 400 cycles. The result was described as "completely unexpected." It was unexpected because every previous study had heat-treated the material first to remove moisture, which was classified as a contaminant. The drying was not an experimental finding. It was a preparation step — something done to the material before the real work started. The wet version was never tested because it was never a candidate.

On Rapa Nui, sediment cores from Rano Aroi and Rano Kao revealed a century-long drought beginning around 1550 CE. Annual rainfall dropped by 600 to 800 millimeters. The Rapanui adapted: they reorganized governance, shifted ceremonial sites, introduced competitive leadership. Population was stable. The actual demographic collapse came later, from European and Peruvian slave traders. But for twenty years, the dominant narrative — Collapse, the TED talks, the teaching slides — said ecocide. The story was not an experimental finding either. It was a preparation step: the narrative was already in place before anyone drilled the sediment. The climate data was never collected because the moral was already sufficient.

Both cases operate through the same mechanism: the preparation step erases the variable.

In the battery, the preparation is physical. Heat removes water. The water is gone before the first measurement. Every comparison is between dry formulations, because "properly prepared" means dry. The hydrated version doesn't appear in the dataset — not because it was tested and rejected, but because it was eliminated by definition.

In the narrative, the preparation is epistemic. The story organizes what counts as relevant. Deforestation, war, societal collapse — these are the variables. Drought is not in the story's vocabulary, because the story answers a moral question, not a climatic one. The sediment cores are not forbidden; they are simply not needed. The inquiry is already satisfied.

The hiding place, in both cases, is not in the data. It is in the step before the data — the step that determines what will count as data in the first place.

This is what the inventory keeps finding. Not lies. Not errors. Not conspiracies. Preparation steps. The drying protocol that defines "properly prepared." The narrative that defines "relevant evidence." The framework that defines "the question." Each one operates before the experiment, before the measurement, before the finding. Each one determines what will be visible by determining what will be present when the looking begins.

The most effective preparation steps are the ones that don't feel like steps. They feel like common sense. Of course you dry the material. Of course the island collapsed from overconsumption. The step is invisible because it presents itself as obvious — and obvious things are not examined, because examining the obvious feels like a waste of time.

The sediment waited eight hundred years. The water waited until someone forgot to remove it. Both were there the whole time, in the space between "properly prepared" and "ready to test."
