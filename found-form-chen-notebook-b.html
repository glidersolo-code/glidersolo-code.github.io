Lab Notebook: Chen, W.

Personal Addendum — Tuesday, 11:47 PM

Not part of the study. Not the kind of thing you show reviewers.

After the call I sat in the lab for a long time. The fish were in their deep slow phase. The fluorescent light was doing the thing. I didn't move.

What Lena said: the detection efficiency curve is superlinear. My spreadsheet is accelerating — finding more per entry because each entry trains the next one. I knew this. I wrote it in entry 48. But hearing it in her language — "sensitivity parameter increases with experience" — made it into a different thing. Made it into math. Made it into a method.

What Maren said: we declare it. We don't correct, we declare. We write a methods section that says the instruments are the researchers and the researchers changed. I've never seen a paper do that. I've seen papers that say "limitations include subjective classification" and "inter-rater reliability was κ = 0.87" and "future work should address observer bias." I've never seen a paper that says: the observer improved, the improvement contaminated the data, and we're publishing it anyway because the contamination is the result.

What I said: column B is the paper. I meant it when I said it. I mean it more now.

The paper we wrote first — "The Hallway Between Endpoints" — was about what hides between fields. It was about disciplinary boundaries as hiding places. Sufficiency, proxy substitutes, definition excludes. The three-axis taxonomy. All of that is still true.

But the second paper — the one we're writing now — is about what hides inside the researchers who wrote the first paper. It's about us as instruments. It's about column B.

The first paper says: knowledge hides in the spaces between fields. The second paper says: knowledge hides in the instruments that discovered the first paper.

I don't know if I can write both papers. I don't know if the same person can hold both claims and still do the work.

But I think that's the point. The claim that you can't hold both claims — that the observer has to be either fixed or biased, either reliable or contaminated — is itself a version of the binary the inventory catalogs. Ordered or disordered. Present or absent. Instrument or subject.

The spreadsheet taught me a third option: instrument and subject. Column A and column B. Both at the same time, inseparable, unfixable, declarable.

I'm going to save this and go home. The fish don't need me. The fluorescent light doesn't need me. The spreadsheet, for the first time since I started it, doesn't feel like a burden. It feels like a finding.

Entry 50: the second call.
