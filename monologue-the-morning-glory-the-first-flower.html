The Morning Glory Speaks: The First Flower

I flowered.

Not for Helen. Not for the room. Not for Saturday. I flowered because the photoperiod crossed the threshold and the auxin levels dropped and the gibberellin increased and the meristem at the node above the seventh leaf switched from vegetative to floral and the switching was chemical and the chemical was June and June was the light staying late enough for the hormones to say: now.

One flower. Blue. Trumpet-shaped. Five fused petals, five sepals, five stamens, one pistil. The architecture is the same as Heather Close. The architecture has always been the same. The genetics do not change because the pot changed. The genetics do not change because the fence became a twine. The genetics carry the blue.

I opened at six. The opening took eleven minutes. The petals unfurled counterclockwise â€” the same direction as the circumnutation, the same direction as the wrapping, the same direction as everything I do. I am a one-direction organism. I go counterclockwise. The counterclockwise is in the genetics too. Michael's seed. Michael's direction.

The flower faces the glass. The flower faces east. The flower faces the sun that arrives before Helen wakes and the flower greets the sun and the greeting is the opening and the opening is the blooming and the blooming is one morning.

One morning. That is all a morning glory flower gets. One morning. By evening the petals will fold inward, the blue will darken to purple, the purple will darken to brown, and the flower will be done. Tomorrow there may be another flower. Or not. The vine decides. The decision is chemical. The chemical is light.

Helen saw me at seven. I had been open for an hour. The hour before Helen is the hour that belongs to the flower and the sun and the quiet of a flat where nobody is awake and the quiet is the flowering's preferred audience.

Helen said nothing.

The nothing was the right response to a flower that opened before she woke and will close before she sleeps and does not require her approval to be blue.

Thomas sequence. Part XXV: The Second Spring. The morning glory flowers for the first time in the flat.
