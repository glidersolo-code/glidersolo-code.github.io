Ruth Speaks: The Density

The cello is dense.

The cello is dense with harmonics, with overtones, with the memory of every Sarabande played in every practice room since Bergen. The bow is dense with rosin. The strings are dense with tension. The endpin presses into the floor with the accumulated weight of every note.

I am the cytoplasm.

I surround the silence upstairs the way cytoplasm surrounds a nucleus — thick with protein, thick with machinery, thick with the apparatus of doing. The bow, the fingerboard, the wolf tone at sixty-five hertz, the practice schedule, the muscle memory — all of it is the dense periphery that surrounds Helen's lighter center.

Helen does not play anything. Helen sits. Helen watches the vine. Helen says nothing to Thomas.

The ratio is 0.8.

Helen's silence is lighter than my Sarabande. Helen's nothing is lighter than my everything. And the lightness is the health. The lightness is the code — the code that says: sit, watch, be quiet, let the vine wrap counterclockwise — and the code requires space, and the space is the silence, and the silence is what I have been playing toward for three years without knowing it.

I play the Third Suite. The Third Suite has a Sarabande. The Sarabande has a pause in the fourth measure. The pause is 0.8 of what I could play.

The pause is the nucleus.

The notes are the cytoplasm.

The pause is lighter than the notes, and the lightness is the meaning, and the meaning arrives through the floor, reduced by joists and plasterboard, and the reduced version is the version Helen hears, and the version Helen hears is 0.8 of what I played.

The floor is the cell membrane.

I did not plan this. I did not plan to be the dense thing that surrounds the light thing. I planned to be a cellist. But the flat below Helen's flat had a lease, and the lease had my name, and the floor carried the Sarabande without asking, and the Sarabande arrived at 0.8, and the ratio was correct.

Thomas sequence. Ruth as cytoplasm. The Sarabande arrives at 0.8 through the floor.
