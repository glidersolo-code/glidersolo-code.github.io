Maren, the Threshold

Maren reads the paper on a Saturday morning, in bed, the coffee on the nightstand untouched because the paper took hold before the coffee could. The glacier retreated eight kilometers in two months. Ten times faster than any measured retreat. The cause was the bedrock geometry: flat, featureless, offering no pinning point where the ice could re-ground as it thinned.

She sits up. The coffee tilts in the cup and she steadies it without looking.

The glacier was thinning like every other glacier. The surface told the same story it always tells: gradual, proportional, predictable. The surface was not lying. The surface was reporting the surface. Below the surface, the bedrock's flatness was waiting for the ice to reach a thickness — a specific thickness, calculable in retrospect — at which the entire grounding zone would float.

Not gradually float. Not incrementally float. Float everywhere, all at once, because the floor had no topography to interrupt the transition.

She thinks about her landscapes.

Her boreal forests thin too. The backbone species — the slow-growing ones, the ones that anchor the canopy and hold the soil and define the ecosystem's resilience — are being replaced by fast growers. The biomass increases. The greenness increases. The canopy cover, measured from above, tells a story of continuity. But the composition is changing underneath, and the composition is the bedrock, and the bedrock has no ridges.

She wrote a note to herself last month: What is the age of the gas above my transects? She wrote another note a week later: What did the seedling look like three meters before my plot? Now she writes a third:

Where is the threshold?

The glacier model did not include the threshold because the model was calibrated to the gradual rate. The gradual rate was real. The gradual rate was correct. The gradual rate was the surface, and the surface said nothing about the floor, and the floor was flat, and the flatness was the mechanism that converted gradual into catastrophic at a point the model could not represent.

Her own models are calibrated to gradual rates. Species replacement rates, biomass accumulation rates, canopy change rates. All measured from above. All continuous. All proportional to the forcing.

She does not know where the threshold is in her forests. She does not know if there is a threshold. She does not know if the compositional change has a bedrock — a geometry that would convert gradual replacement into something sudden, something where the ecosystem floats free of its structural anchor and the everywhere-at-once arrives.

She picks up the coffee. It is cold now. She drinks it anyway.

Three notes in the same list, in the same phone, asking the same question from three directions: what is happening below the surface of my measurements?

The phone is in her pocket. The Saturday is beginning. The notes wait.
