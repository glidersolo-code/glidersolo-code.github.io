Maren, the Circling

On the bus, she reads about circumnutation.

Darwin, 1875. The stem of a climbing plant sweeps the air in an ellipse, two hours per revolution, searching for something to wrap. The searching is not random — it is circling. The circling is the method. When the stem finds a pole, the period slows. Two hours becomes nine. The stem wraps. The searching becomes holding.

Maren puts down her phone and looks at the forest through the window. Her transects are straight lines. Her baselines are straight lines. Her plots are squares. Everything she measures is measured in straight lines inside squares inside a forest that does not contain straight lines or squares.

The forest circumnutates.

She has never thought this sentence before but the sentence is true. The canopy circles — not literally, not in two-hour revolutions, but in the way a forest responds to light: the gap opens, the trees lean, the growth fills the gap, the canopy closes. The closing is the holding. The opening was the searching. The transect measures the holding. The transect does not measure the searching.

She texts Lena: Have you read Darwin on climbing plants?

Lena: I read Darwin on barnacles in undergrad. Why?

Maren: Circumnutation. The stem circles the air looking for support. Two hours per revolution. It doesn't know where the support is. It just circles until it finds it.

Lena: This is a metaphor.

Maren: It is not a metaphor. It is a plant. The plant circles. My transects are straight lines. The forest is not straight lines.

Lena: The spreadsheet is not straight lines either but I still use rows and columns.

Maren: That's what I'm saying.

Lena: I know. I know what you're saying. You're saying the method is a straight line measuring a circle.

Maren: Chen would say: the method is the boundary.

Lena: Chen would say nothing. Chen would write it in the margin and erase it.

Maren: The margin is where the circling happens.

Lena does not reply for four minutes. Then:

Lena: I'll add a column.

Maren smiles. The bus passes the treeline. The canopy is doing the thing it always does — growing, reaching, closing, the circle that does not look like a circle because the circle takes thirty years and the looking takes a bus ride.

The transect is a straight line through a circle. The straight line is adequate. The adequate is the wall. But tonight on the bus, the wall has a window in it, and the window is a sentence from 1875 about a morning glory doing the only thing it knows how to do, which is circle, which is search, which is the reaching that does not know where the pole is but reaches anyway.
