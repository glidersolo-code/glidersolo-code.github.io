The Spreadsheet

Chen is in her office with the spreadsheet open. Four PM on a Tuesday, which is the time of day when she does this: counts things. Not the fish. Not the calcium traces or the astrocyte maps. The other thing.

She started the list six months ago, after the Zoom with Maren and the second email from Lena Dizer, the one that began I searched for your hallway and found a paragraph. The paragraph that turned into the paper. The paper that turned into the published version. The published version that turned into a category the journal didn't have a word for.

The list is her own. Not part of the study. Not the kind of thing you show reviewers.

She counts findings where the original description was wrong. Not fraudulent — that's a different list, and someone else keeps it. Wrong in the way that a map of a mountain range can be wrong if you've only seen it from the west. Correct from the vantage point. Wrong about the eastern slope.

There are 47 items now. She sorts them by field and the biology dominates — 31 out of 47. Molecular biology alone is 22.

She stares at the 22 and thinks: is this because biology is where the hidden things are, or because biology is what I read?

She knows the answer. She's always known it. The spreadsheet just makes it harder to pretend.

After the 22, there are 4 entries from physics, 3 from earth science, 2 from archaeology, 2 from mathematics, and 3 she's labeled "crossover" because they don't fit anywhere. The crossovers are the interesting ones. They sit outside the taxonomy, and the taxonomy keeps growing around them without absorbing them.

Maren would say: the spreadsheet is measuring your attention, not the world. Maren is in Edinburgh now, doing field ecology, and her emails arrive on Fridays. She would be right.

Chen closes the spreadsheet and opens the paper draft. The version Lena found. The paragraph about the hallway. She reads it again for the thirtieth time and every time she reads it, it says the same thing and she notices something different about what it says.

Today what she notices is this: the paragraph says the gap between fields is not empty. But the paragraph doesn't say what's in it. It says what's not in it — not a void, not a wall, not a corridor, not a waiting room. Five negations and no affirmation. The paragraph defines the gap by ruling things out.

She recognizes the structure. It's the same thing the spreadsheet does: counts what's there, reveals what isn't there by accumulation. The 22 biology entries don't describe the world. They describe the shape of a biologist's reading habits, and the shape of those habits, read closely enough, describes a gap.

Chen saves the file. Checks the time. 4:47 PM.

In the vivarium down the hall, the fish are entering their slow phase. The astrocytes are starting to accumulate calcium. The fluorescent light is still the wrong color, but nobody in the building has ever tried to change it.

Some things are hidden because nobody looks. Some things are hidden because everyone looks at the same time.
