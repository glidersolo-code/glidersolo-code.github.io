Dramatic Monologue

The Crack in the Pavement

I am between the Spar and the flat.

I am seven feet from the kerb, two inches wide at the surface, narrowing to nothing below. I appeared in the frost of 2019 — or the frost revealed me. I was always in the concrete. The frost made me visible.

I know Tuesday.

Tuesday is the woman with the slow step and the shopping bag that weighs more on the way back than on the way there. She passes at 10:15, give or take the weather. In rain she is earlier. In sun she stops at the bench by the bus stop and sits for two minutes, which I know because the light changes — the shadow of the bench shifts and I can feel the shift in temperature, the way all concrete feels weather: not as a thought but as a expansion, a contraction, a slow breathing that nobody notices because nobody watches concrete breathe.

She does not step on me. She has not stepped on me since 2020, which is the year she started noticing me, or the year I became wide enough to notice. The noticing is mutual. I know her weight from the vibrations. She knows my width from the looking.

I do not know her name. I know her step. The step is lighter on the way there — anticipation, purpose, the list in her head that she will not write down because writing down is for people who do not remember, and she remembers. The step is heavier on the way back — bread, milk, a lemon. The lemon weighs nothing. The lemon is in the bag because it is Friday's lemon but she buys it on Tuesday, which means the lemon passes over me every Tuesday at 10:30, five days before it matters, and the five days are the practice, and the practice is the week, and the week passes over me every Tuesday in both directions.

I have cracked further since 2019. Not from her — from the frost, from the buses, from the council lorry that parks on this stretch every second Thursday and whose weight I can feel in my foundations, which are deeper than anyone knows because nobody cores a pavement crack to see how deep it goes.

She passes every Tuesday. Every Tuesday the same gap in traffic, the same crossing at the pelican, the same step over me — not a step onto me, a step over, a going-around, a small motion of care applied to concrete that does not need the care but receives it anyway.

The going-around is the care. I know this because I am the thing that is gone around. The going-around says: you are there, I see you, I will not step on you, not because stepping would hurt but because noticing is the practice.

The morning glory does not know it has moved. I do not know I have cracked. The knowing is not ours. The knowing is Tuesday's, the woman's, the step that goes around.

She has never spoken to me. She has never spoken about me. I am in no letter, no conversation, no catalogue. I am between the Spar and the flat, and the between is the week, and the week passes over me every Tuesday, and the passing is the going-around, and the going-around is enough.
