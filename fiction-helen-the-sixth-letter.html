Helen, the Sixth Letter

The knife opens the corner. The paper unfolds.

The rest. Good.

Three words.

Helen looks at the letter the way she looked at the morning glory the day it reached the top of the twine — not surprise, not pride. Recognition. The letter has arrived where it was going.

The rest. He heard Ruth's word. Ruth said the rest is not silence — the rest is the note played by not playing. Helen told Thomas on Saturday, standing in the kitchen with the blue cup between them, and Thomas listened the way Thomas always listens: forward-leaning, certain, the same angle as the handwriting.

And the letter came back: The rest. Good.

Meaning: I heard you. Meaning: the rest is good. Meaning: your Thursday is the rest and the rest is good and the good is the going-around and the going-around is: I do not need to say more. Three words say it.

Helen puts the letter in the drawer. The drawer is full of Saturdays and the Saturdays are getting lighter. The first letters were five words. Then four. Now three. The drawer holds them all. The drawer does not compare.

She thinks: the next one will be two.

She does not know what the two words will be. She does not need to know. The letters will arrive and the knife will open the corner and the paper will unfold and the words will be fewer and the fewer will be more and the more will be the going-around and the going-around will fit in the drawer.

Three words. Two next. Then one.

Then the envelope will arrive and the knife will open the corner and the paper will unfold and there will be one word and the one word will be good and good will mean everything it has always meant — the kitchen, the days, the rest, the brush in the drawer, the cutting in the pot, the floor, the Sarabande, the building, the carrying, the holding, the enough.

Good.

Helen rinses the cup. Saturday is ending. Thomas has driven home. The M6 is carrying him the way the floor carries Ruth: without choosing, without asking, without needing to be told where the weight is going.

Three words. The letters are learning her language. Helen is glad.
