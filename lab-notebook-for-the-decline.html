Lab Notebook for the Decline

A found form — laboratory notebook entries

Day 1. Measured NDRG1 in 200 aged satellite cells. Average 3.47x elevation over young controls. Consistent with previous literature. Filed under "age-related accumulation."

Day 14. Confirmed mTOR suppression via Western blot. The pathway is dampened. Less activation. Less proliferation. Less repair. The cells are slower. This is what aging looks like at the molecular level.

Day 30. NDRG1-knockdown cohort showing remarkable recovery. Activation kinetics nearly match young controls. PI is excited. "We've reversed the aging phenotype." Started drafting the abstract.

Day 45. Second injury in knockdown mice. Recovery still fast. Writing the paper. The story is clean: remove the brake, restore the function.

Day 60. Third injury. Knockdown cohort healing well but — cell counts are lower than expected. Not alarming. Noted in the supplementary.

Day 90. Fourth injury cycle. Knockdown mice healing noticeably slower. Not because the cells are slow — because there are fewer cells. The fast ones didn't last.

Day 90, evening. Went back to the control group data. The unmodified aged cells — the slow ones, the ones with the brake — still there. Every one of them. Slower, yes. But present.

Day 91. The PI asked what happened to the knockdown mice. I said: they healed beautifully and then they ran out of cells.

Day 91, margin note. The decline was the cells that didn't decline dying first. What survived was what we were trying to fix.

Day 120. Rewrote the paper. Changed the title from "Reversing Age-Related Stem Cell Decline" to the one we published. It took three months to see what the cells had known for seventy years.
