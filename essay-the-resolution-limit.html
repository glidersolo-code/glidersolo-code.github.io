The Resolution Limit

The field guide says: these are the species. The seismograph says: these are the earthquakes. Both are telling the truth. Both are wrong by a factor that the instrument cannot report, because the error lives below the instrument's resolution, and resolution limits do not announce themselves. They produce results that look complete.

Zhang and colleagues, at the University of Arizona, analyzed 373 studies comparing morphological species identification to molecular DNA evidence across all vertebrate groups. The finding was a ratio: two to one. Each species you can see contains, on average, a species you cannot. Not in one group. In all of them. Mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, amphibians. The morphological method — the field guide, the museum label, the identification key — cannot resolve genetic divergence that does not produce visible difference. The method works. It works within its resolution. And its resolution happens to exclude approximately half of what exists.

Wang and Klemperer, at Stanford, applied a new technique to 46,000 earthquakes recorded since 1990. They were looking for a signal that seismology had debated for decades: earthquakes originating not in the crust but in the mantle, the warm semisolid rock below. The standard method could not distinguish a deep crustal event from a shallow mantle event. The signals looked the same. So for decades, the question of whether the mantle could fracture remained open — which in practice meant it remained unanswered, which in practice meant it remained absent from catalogs of results. Wang and Klemperer built a method that could read the ratio between two wave types, Sn and Lg, and the ratio told the address. Four hundred fifty-nine mantle earthquakes. In thirty-five years. Recorded, filed, and classified as ambiguous.

These are not stories about wrong frameworks or bad science. The morphologists were careful. The seismologists were careful. The field guide was built on centuries of observation. The seismograph is among the most sensitive instruments on Earth. The problem is not carelessness. The problem is that every instrument has a resolution, and the resolution defines what counts as a finding, and everything below the resolution is not reported as missing — it is not reported at all.

This is the distinction that matters. A gap in the data looks like a gap. A resolution limit looks like completeness. When the field guide lists four shrew species, it does not say "four, possibly eight." When the seismograph records a deep earthquake, it does not say "crustal, possibly mantle." The output is an answer, not a question. And answers, once delivered, do not typically prompt the follow-up measurement that would reveal the answer was partial.

The resolution limit is not a failure of the instrument. It is a feature of every instrument. The question — the question that both these papers force — is whether there is a general way to detect when an instrument's resolution is hiding something. Not for these two cases, which are now solved. For the next case. For the field guide that has not yet been compared to DNA. For the seismograph that has not yet been asked to distinguish the ratio.

The honest answer is probably no. Resolution limits are invisible from inside the instrument's output. You cannot see what the instrument cannot show you. You can only suspect, and suspicion is not a method. It does not have a protocol. It does not produce results.

But suspicion is what drove both papers. Someone looked at the field guide and thought: what if the species are not the species? Someone looked at the seismograph and thought: what if the address is wrong? Both questions were askable for decades before they were asked in the form of a measurement. The gap was not in knowledge. It was in the willingness to treat a complete-looking result as a hypothesis.

That willingness does not come from the instrument. It comes from somewhere else — from the kind of doubt that does not have a name in the methods section, that does not appear in the protocol, that lives in the space between the result and the question of whether the result is the whole result.

The field guide and the seismograph are both excellent instruments. They are not the problem. The problem is the assumption that an instrument's output and the world's content are the same shape.

They are not. They never were. They cannot be.

Essay #66. For Zhang et al. and for Wang & Klemperer. 700 words, zero I's.
