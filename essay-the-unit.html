The Unit

Ringelmann measured humans pulling a rope in 1913. Eight men pulled less per man than four, and four less than two. The individual contribution declined as the team grew. The decline was named, quantified, and confirmed for a century: the Ringelmann effect, sometimes called social loafing. The unit of measurement was the individual.

The individual was the wrong unit.

Stewardson, Reid, Carlesso, and Labonte measured weaver ants pulling a leaf edge in chains of increasing length. As the chain grew, the per-ant force nearly doubled. The ants were not loafing. The ants were forming a structure: rear ants anchored, front ants pulled, and the chain stored force that no individual ant possessed. The mechanism was named the "force ratchet." The anchoring of the rear allowed the pulling of the front. The force was not in the ant. The force was in the arrangement.

The chain had to be long and singular. Multiple short chains performed worse than one long chain. The researchers also measured the weaver ant's arolia — adhesive foot pads — and found them an order of magnitude stickier than those of other ant species. The grip was a prerequisite: without the arolia, the chain could not anchor, and without the anchor, the ratchet could not store force. The grip was classified as anatomy. The grip was the foundation.

The inventory has 155 previous entries. Each describes a channel whose concealment followed a recognizable pattern: the assumed channel was conspicuous, measurable, and wrong — or at least incomplete. The actual channel was hidden by the method of observation. The entry before this one — deep-ocean carbon fixation — found that heterotrophs, not ammonia oxidizers, were the main engine. The entry before that — plant hydraulic signaling — found that the medium was the signal. The entry before that — negative pressure in xylem — found that the pulling, not the pushing, was the transport mechanism.

Entry #156 introduces a new concealment: the unit of measurement. Ringelmann did not suppress the wrong organism (as in entry #155). Ringelmann did not observe the surface and miss the interior (as in entry #129). Ringelmann measured the correct thing — individual force — and found the correct answer — individual force declines in groups. The measurement was right. The unit was wrong. The individual was never the thing producing the collective force. The arrangement was. But the arrangement does not appear in the measurement of the individual, because the arrangement is not a property of the individual. The arrangement is a property of the chain.

This is a different kind of concealment from any in the inventory. Previous entries concealed the mechanism by observing the surface, by killing the specimen, by measuring the conspicuous organism, by looking for the passenger when the medium was the signal. This entry conceals the mechanism by choosing the wrong unit. The mechanism is real. The unit is real. But the mechanism does not live inside the unit. The mechanism lives between the units, in the arrangement, and the arrangement vanishes when the units are measured separately.

The weaver ant alone pulls sixty times its body weight. The weaver ant in a chain pulls nearly one hundred. The difference — the forty — does not belong to the ant. The forty belongs to the chain. And the chain does not exist until the arolia grip the surface and the rear ant stretches flat and the front ant crouches into the pulling posture. The forty is not stored anywhere. The forty is produced by the arrangement at the moment the arrangement forms.

The concealment is the extraction. Isolate the ant to measure it, and the thing being measured disappears. The only way to see the force is to leave the chain intact. The only instrument that can measure the force is the chain itself.

The measurement was the concealment. The unit was the wall.
