Ruth, the Theories

Thomas sequence — dramatic monologue, piece #690 Part XLVII: The Interface

I had a theory about Helen. The theory involved loneliness.

The theory was wrong. Not because Helen is not lonely — I do not know if Helen is lonely; Helen does not discuss loneliness, she discusses lemons — but because the theory was too complex for what it was trying to explain. I was looking for something deep. Something involving childhood, or loss, or the particular quality of light in a third-floor flat facing east.

The answer was simpler. She has a son. The son visits on Saturdays. She makes tea. The tea involves a lemon now. That is the mechanism.

I know about this error. In my practice, I learn a piece. I have a theory about the piece — the composer's intention, the historical context, the emotional arc. Then I play it. The bow touches the string. The string vibrates. And none of my theories matter. The mechanism is: contact. Rosined horsehair against wound steel. The vibration does not care about my analysis of Baroque dance forms. The vibration cares about pressure, angle, speed.

Two vectors. The bow's direction. The string's tension.

I spent four years at the conservatoire learning theories. I have spent twenty years since learning to ignore them and listen to the scattering.

Through the floor, Helen's kettle clicks. Saturday. One vector arrives. One field responds. I can hear it in the way the building breathes.
