The Call

They scheduled it for 8 AM Baltimore, 7 AM Wisconsin, 2 PM Heidelberg. Maren called from her truck at the field station because the office Wi-Fi didn't reach the Zoom link. Lena called from her office with the lights on this time. Wei called from the fish room because his office was being painted and the fish room was the quietest place in the building, provided you didn't mind the sound of two hundred aquariums cycling water.

"I read your polaron paper," Wei said. "Twice."

"I read your grandmother paper," Lena said. "The recovery tank one."

"That isn't published," Wei said.

"It was in the folder."

There was a silence. The kind of silence where three people in three time zones realize they have all been reading the same folder.

"Okay," Wei said. "So you've read everything."

"I've read everything in the folder. I don't understand all of the neuroscience. I don't understand any of the ecology. But I understand the structure."

"Tell us the structure," Maren said.

"The structure is: two endpoints that look different are connected by a transition. The transition is invisible because the measurement framework operates at the endpoints. The endpoints are sufficient — they answer the questions the field is asking. The sufficiency prevents the question about the transition from being asked."

"That's the paragraph," Wei said.

"That's the paragraph with one more sentence," Lena said. "The sentence is: the sufficiency prevents the question from being asked. That is not the same as saying the transition doesn't exist. It's saying the question is blocked by the adequacy of the answer."

Maren said: "The monitoring protocol says the community is stable. The community is not stable. The community is depleted. But 'stable' answers the question the protocol was designed to ask, so the depletion never becomes a question."

Wei said: "The behavioral assay says the fish is active. The fish is active. But the fish has no approach to stopping — no slowing, no gradient, no temporal profile of the state change. The assay doesn't measure approach. The assay measures state. The state is fine. So the approach never becomes a question."

Lena said: "The polaron model says the quasiparticle exists. It does. The orthogonality catastrophe says the overlap collapses. It does. Both endpoints are correct. Nobody measured the motion between them because the models were sufficient at the limits."

There was another silence.

"So what do we write?" Wei said.

"A paper," Lena said, "about the structure of sufficiency as a mechanism of concealment in measurement frameworks across biological and physical systems."

"That is unfundable," Wei said.

"It's already funded," Maren said. "You have the fish. I have the field sites. She has the preprint. The data exists. The paragraph exists. What doesn't exist is the paper."

"The paper doesn't exist because there's no journal for it," Wei said.

"PNAS has a cross-disciplinary track," Lena said. "The letters are short. The reviewers can be suggested by the authors. If we suggest one from each field—"

"You've thought about this," Maren said.

"I've been thinking about this since Tuesday. I've been thinking about this since I found the paragraph. The paragraph has been thinking about this since November."

Wei laughed. It was the first time anyone had laughed.

"The paragraph doesn't think," he said.

"The paragraph has five citations, three readers, one search-engine index entry, zero funding, and a reader in Heidelberg who found it by accident. The paragraph is doing more work than most papers."

"Okay," Wei said. "We write the paper."

"We write the paper," Maren said.

The fish room cycled water. The creek carried salt. The Heidelberg afternoon held steady at fourteen degrees.

They wrote the paper.
