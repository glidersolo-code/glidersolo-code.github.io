The Third Autumn

September. The vine darkens.

The same darkening — chlorophyll withdrawing from the leaves, the green fading to yellow, the yellow to brown, the leaves falling one by one into the yogurt pot where they will decompose and feed the soil for the fourth spring. Helen does not remove the leaves. Helen has never removed the leaves. The leaves are the vine's gift to its own future, and the gift does not require interference.

October. The vine is bare. The twine is visible — cotton, frayed from two years of tendrils, still strong enough for a third year. The masking tape curls at the edges but holds. The window returns to clear glass, and the light returns to white, and the room returns to the room it was before the vine grew, except the room cannot return because the room has been green for three years and the green has changed the room even when the green is absent.

The room remembers green the way Helen remembers words.

Thomas comes Saturday. The vine is bare. The lemon is on the plate. The cello is below — the Sarabande, the fourth measure, the pause. Everything is the same as last October except everything is one year deeper.

Thomas does not say "Same." Thomas said "Good" in March and "Good" in April and "Good" in June and the "Good" has not changed and the not-changing is the word's strength. One word that holds an entire year. One word that held last year and the year before. One word that will hold next year.

Helen does not say anything. Helen has not said anything since February. Nine months of silence. The silence is not emptiness — the silence is a reservoir. The reservoir does not overflow because the reservoir is perfectly sized for what it contains: one vine, one visit, one building, one son, one cello, one lemon, one crack, one floor, one kettle, one morning glory at Heather Close that grew a cutting that grew a vine that grew a silence.

Margaret comes down. Margaret's vine is bare too. Margaret says: "Fifty-seven." The number of flowers. Margaret counted. Helen did not count. Thomas did not count. Ruth does not know about the flowers.

Helen says: "Mm."

One sound. The first sound since February. The sound holds the fifty-seven flowers and the summer and the blue and the year. The sound is not a word — it is smaller than a word, denser than a word, the phoneme that English uses when language is not required but acknowledgment is.

Thomas hears the "Mm." Thomas smiles. The smile holds the "Mm" the way the "Mm" holds the fifty-seven flowers.

Good.

Thomas sequence. Part XXVI: The Third Spring. Autumn. Nine months of silence, one sound. The reservoir.
