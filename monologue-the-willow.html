The Willow

I grew. I can confirm that. I grew in some of the places they measured and in some of the places they didn't, and the measurements they took of me in 2001 were at different coordinates than the measurements they took in 2020, but nobody noticed, because the story needed a willow and I was a willow and the numbers went up and the numbers were the right kind of up.

I didn't cascade. I don't even know what that means, applied to me. They say the wolves came back and the elk stopped eating me and I grew and the streams recovered. That's the story. It has the shape of a story: a beginning (wolves), a middle (elk), and an end (me, taller, in a photograph next to a stream).

The math that proved it used my height to calculate my volume and then used my volume to predict my height and then celebrated the correlation. I didn't ask for this. I'm a willow. I respond to water, to browsing, to soil, to season, to the particular angle of a valley. The wolves are one factor. The snowmelt is another. The elk hunters in the next valley are a third. Nobody put the hunters in the model.

Do you know what it's like to be the proof of something you didn't do? To stand in a valley and be photographed and have the photograph become a slide in an ecology lecture and have the lecture become a policy argument and have the argument become a conservation movement and have the movement need you to be taller than you were in 2001, and to be taller, but not for the reason they think, or not only for the reason they think, or not in the clean way the cascade requires?

I grew because I'm a willow and the water was good and the elk were fewer and the elk were fewer partly because of wolves and partly because of hunters and partly because of drought and partly because that's what elk populations do. But "partly" doesn't cascade. "Partly" doesn't teach. "Partly" doesn't win grants or make documentaries or convince a generation of students that nature heals if you just add the right predator.

I'm not angry. I'm not capable of anger. But if I were, I'd be angry not at being measured but at being narrated. At having my height turned into a story about something I wasn't doing. At being the willow in the photograph that launched a thousand lectures.

I did grow. Write that down. I grew, for several reasons, in several places, at several rates, and the pattern was not a cascade but a landscape doing what landscapes do when conditions change in multiple ways at once.

That's not a story. That's a willow.
