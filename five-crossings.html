Five Crossings

Catalog

1. Marc Freeman, 2018

Freeman was a neurobiologist studying how neurons die after injury. He was not studying astrocytes. He had no reason to study astrocytes. But astrocytes were wrapped around every dying neuron he looked at, and after enough years of seeing the wrapping and ignoring it, he stopped ignoring it. The crossing was not dramatic. He did not switch fields. He asked one question — what are the astrocytes doing? — that his field had not been designed to ask. The answer took seven years to assemble, across three labs and three species, and the answer was: everything.

2. Crossley Lab, UNSW, 2024

For thirty years, the methyl groups on DNA were markers — indicators of gene silencing, useful for classification and diagnosis. Crossley's team needed something the marker model couldn't provide: they needed to turn the fetal globin gene back on. Not classify it. Activate it. The crossing was from observation to intervention. The moment the question changed from "what do these marks predict?" to "what happens if we remove them?", the marks became anchors. Removable anchors. The experiment was simple. Nobody had done it because nobody had needed to.

3. Kathryn Appler, postdoc, UT Austin, 2024

Appler was processing environmental DNA from marine sediments — 15 terabytes of it, from sites around the world. She was looking for Asgard archaea, the microbes most closely related to the ancestor of all complex life. Previous sampling had found them in deep-sea, oxygen-poor environments, and the field assumed they were anaerobic. Appler found them in shallow coastal sediments, breathing oxygen. The crossing was literal: from deep-sea sampling sites to shallow water. The assumption about habitat had been built on where researchers looked first. Nobody was wrong about what they found. They were wrong about where they stopped looking.

4. Eugen Dizer, graduate student, Heidelberg, 2025

Dizer's advisor, Richard Schmidt, had worked on the Fermi polaron problem for years. The problem was well-characterized: light impurities in quantum media form stable quasiparticles. Anderson's orthogonality catastrophe described the opposite limit. Nobody connected them because nobody's grant application straddled the boundary. Dizer found the connection by noticing that the "perfectly heavy" impurity — the idealization at the core of the catastrophe model — shivers. Not enough to break the model. Enough to prevent the catastrophe. The crossing was from one limit to the other, through the tremor in between.

5. Grad student, unnamed, OHSU fly lab, late night, 2019

The published record doesn't capture this one. But somewhere in Freeman's lab, at some unrecorded hour, someone ran the experiment that showed norepinephrine activates astrocytes instead of neurons, and said something like that can't be right. And then ran it again. And got the same result. And sat with the result. And decided to keep going, through a hallway that had been there the entire time, connecting two buildings that had never spoken, because the hallway was nobody's job and the buildings were everyone's.

The crossing was from that can't be right to what if it is.

The five crossings share one feature: none of them required new technology. The tools existed. The samples existed. The math existed. What was missing was the walk. The twelve meters across the courtyard. The willingness to enter a building where your badge doesn't scan.
