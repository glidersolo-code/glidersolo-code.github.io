Lena, the Parameter

Lena reads Maren's email at midnight Heidelberg time.

She shouldn't be in the email chain. She was added six months ago when Chen sent the draft and Maren replied and Maren's reply CC'd Lena because the draft had a paragraph about quantum decoherence that Maren wanted checked. The paragraph was fine. Lena corrected one index and stayed on the thread.

Now she reads about satellites and snow.

The mathematics is simple. Embarrassingly simple. If the detection threshold τ decreases at rate δ per instrument generation, and each generation adds δ·A(t) to the detected area where A(t) is the area of snow between τ_old and τ_new, then the cumulative phantom signal grows as the integral of δ·A(t) over the observation period. The satellite team's correction amounts to deconvolving τ(t) from the raw time series and recomputing with τ held constant.

Lena sees this in forty seconds. What takes her longer is the implication.

In her field, the equivalent is the variational ansatz — the trial wave function you assume when solving a many-body problem. The ansatz determines what solutions you can find. A more flexible ansatz (more parameters, more terms) finds more states. Each improvement in the ansatz expands the space of findable solutions. And each expansion is treated as a discovery about the physical system, not as a discovery about the mathematical tool.

She's done this herself. Her latest paper found a new quasi-bound state by using a more sophisticated ansatz than the previous group. She found it because her ansatz could represent it. The previous group didn't find it because their ansatz couldn't. Both groups reported the same physical system. Her group found more. The question Maren's email raises: was the additional state a property of the system, or a property of the better ansatz?

She knows the answer. It's both. The state is real. But it was found because the instrument improved, and the improvement is never reported as part of the finding.

She types a reply to Maren, deletes it, types another:

The satellite's detection threshold is our variational ansatz. Improvement in the ansatz is treated as improvement in the physics. Nobody publishes a paper titled "Our Better Ansatz Found a State the Old Ansatz Couldn't Represent." They publish "New Quasi-Bound State Discovered in [System]." The instrument improvement is absorbed into the discovery claim.

The snow didn't increase. Our quasi-bound state does exist. But the mechanism by which we found it — better tool, therefore more found — is the same mechanism that produced the satellite error. We just haven't noticed yet because our error has the same sign as the truth.

She sends it at 12:14 AM. Then sits in the dark office with the particle physics poster on the wall — the Standard Model, every field and boson in its place, a map so complete and so correct that nobody asks what it leaves out — and wonders whether the state she found is the thin snow or the leaving snow.

Chen-Maren-Lena novella. 12th vignette. Lena's first extended scene since "The Keyword."
