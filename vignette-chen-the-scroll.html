Chen, the Scroll

Chen reads the paper on her tablet, sitting in the departmental kitchen at 7:40 AM, before anyone else arrives. The coffee is instant. The mug says DEPARTMENT OF PHYSICS in letters that have survived the dishwasher longer than three postdocs.

A dinosaur's nose. She wouldn't normally read paleontology. But Lena sent it — one line in an email, no subject, just the DOI and the sentence: The scroll is inside the bone.

Chen reads the abstract, then the methods, then goes back to the abstract. CT scanning of Triceratops skulls reveals respiratory turbinates. Thin scroll-shaped bones inside the nasal cavity. Not for smelling. For cooling. The nose was a radiator. The nose had always been a radiator. The surface anatomy said olfaction and the surface anatomy was not wrong, just insufficient, and insufficiency that looks complete does not generate questions.

She puts the tablet down. Picks up the mug. The coffee is bad. The coffee is always bad.

What she is thinking about is basis sets.

In quantum chemistry, a basis set is the collection of functions you use to approximate a wave function. Choose the standard set and you get a clean answer. The answer converges. The numbers stop changing. You report the result: this is the energy. This is the bond length. This is the geometry.

But the convergence is a property of the set, not of the system. The wave function does not know your set converged. It is still out there, in its full dimensionality, containing terms your basis set cannot represent. And the result you report — clean, converged, publishable — will not tell you what it missed, because what it missed is orthogonal to everything you included.

The nose had a basis set: surface morphology. The basis set converged. The answer was olfaction. The answer was not wrong. It was converged within its space. The scroll-shaped bones were orthogonal to the basis — they required a different instrument, a different resolution, a different question.

Chen opens her laptop and pulls up a file she hasn't shown Maren. It is a list. Not the paragraph — the paragraph is Maren's, or maybe it belongs to all three of them now. This is something else. A column of examples. Things that converged too early.

She adds: Triceratops nasal cavity — surface morphology converged on olfaction, CT scan revealed thermoregulation. Turbinates present in every specimen. The basis set could not contain the scroll.

She saves the file. Finishes the coffee. The kitchen is still empty.

The scroll was inside the bone. The bone was inside the nose. The nose was inside a framework that said it already knew what the nose was for.

Novella vignette. Chen reads the Triceratops paper, recognizes her basis set problem.
