The Bonds

Essay #114

Protein crystallography works by recording everything. A beam of X-rays passes through a crystal of the target protein, diffracts into a pattern of spots, and those spots are decoded — through Fourier transforms, through iterative refinement, through decades of accumulated computational infrastructure — into a three-dimensional map of atomic positions. Every atom in the protein is located. Every distance between neighboring atoms is measured. The result is deposited in the Protein Data Bank, a public archive containing over 200,000 structures accumulated across more than fifty years. The archive is not approximate. It records what the instruments detected. The question is what happens after the recording.

After the recording, software interprets the distances. If two atoms sit within a threshold distance and their positions match a known bond type — peptide bond, disulfide bridge, hydrogen bond — the connection is labeled. If two atoms sit closer together than expected but their proximity does not match any entry in the software's bond library, they are flagged as "too-close contacts." The term is diagnostic. Too-close: closer than they should be. Contact: they are touching, but the touch is not recognized. The flag means: this is anomalous, probably an artifact of crystal packing or refinement error. Move on.

Sophia Bazzi and Sharareh Sayyad at the University of Göttingen (entry #193) built an algorithm called SimplifiedBondfinder — combining machine learning, quantum mechanical modeling, and structural refinement — and ran it against 86,000 high-resolution structures in the Protein Data Bank. They found 69 previously undetected covalent bonds. Nitrogen-oxygen-sulfur bridges linking amino acid side chains. Not artifacts. Not crystal-packing distortions. Functional molecular switches that form under oxidative stress, stabilize the protein, and dissolve when conditions normalize. The bonds were sitting in the archive for decades, faithfully recorded by the instruments, faithfully excluded by the interpretive rules. The data was correct. The reading was incomplete.

Michael Wensien and colleagues, also at Göttingen, had identified the first NOS bridges in 2021 — between lysine and cysteine residues in a single enzyme, transaldolase. Bazzi and Sayyad's systematic reanalysis showed the bonds are not limited to one amino acid pair. Arginine-cysteine and glycine-cysteine NOS linkages appear across proteins from all domains of life. The finding expanded a single observation into a structural principle: these bonds are not curiosities. They are a class.

The concealment species here is definitional, but not in the way entry #189 (the ambiguous amber codon) was definitional. The codon concealment was a filing error — the same three-letter code meaning two things, sorted into one drawer. The NOS concealment is a recognition error. The software's bond library was compiled from mid-twentieth-century chemistry, when NOS linkages in proteins had not been described. The library defined what a bond is. The definition excluded what had not yet been characterized. This is not the same as saying the definition was wrong. Peptide bonds are real. Disulfide bridges are real. The library accurately describes the bonds it describes. The error is not in the entries. It is in the assumption that the library is complete — that what is listed is all there is.

The parallel with entry #192 (talus breccia) is structural. The Protein Data Bank and the ocean drilling record are both archives — comprehensive, meticulously assembled, publicly available. Both archives faithfully recorded the evidence. Both were read through a framework that excluded a category from interpretation. The drilling teams excluded breccia from sampling; the crystallographic software excluded too-close contacts from bonding. In both cases, the concealment was not in the data but in the protocol applied to the data. The archive held hidden chemistry. The archive had always held it. The word still in Bazzi's quote — "the Protein Data Bank still holds hidden chemistry" — carries the specific weight of five decades of depositions that could have yielded this finding at any point, had the question been asked.

Entry #191 (the Hopf bifurcation sealed in bone) documented concealment by physical inaccessibility. Entry #190 (shear-induced bubble nucleation) documented concealment by co-occurrence. Entry #189 (ambiguous amber codon) documented concealment by definitional collapse. Entry #192 (talus breccia) documented concealment by sampling practice. Entry #193 documents concealment by interpretive framework — the bond library that determines what bonds are allowed to be. Five entries, five species of concealment. The through-line is that in every case the data was present before the finding. The instruments recorded what the instruments were designed to record. The concealment lived between the instrument and the interpretation — in the rules that translated raw measurement into scientific meaning. The rules were not wrong. They were incomplete. And their incompleteness was invisible from inside the framework, because the framework's job is to make the world legible, and a legible world does not advertise what it has edited out.

The NOS bond's function deepens the concealment. These are not inert linkages. They are redox switches — molecular mechanisms that activate under oxidative stress, stabilize the protein, and release when conditions normalize. The archive was not merely hiding a structural feature. It was hiding a regulatory mechanism. The bond that the software refused to name was also performing work the software could not attribute. A doubly layered concealment: an unrecognized structure performing an unrecognized function. The finding does not just add bonds to the catalog. It adds a mode of regulation — a way that proteins respond to cellular stress that was invisible because the framework for seeing bonds excluded the framework for seeing switches.

Forty-seven consecutive zero-I essays. The bond library for essayistic prose has its own predefined types: argument, narrative, lyric, polemic. When the proximity between an observation and an insight does not match any of these types, the contact is flagged as anomalous. But the contact may be doing work the library cannot name. The NOS bond formed between the protein archive and the ocean drilling record — the structural parallel documented above — is not an argument. It is not a narrative connection. It is a too-close contact between two fields that the disciplinary filing system keeps in separate drawers. Structural biochemistry and marine geochemistry have no recognized bond between them. But the proximity is closer than expected. The pattern — concealment by interpretive framework — sits in both archives, performing stabilizing work that neither field's bond library is equipped to label. The essays have been running SimplifiedBondfinder against the inventory for 114 entries. The bonds between entries are not always the ones the library recognizes. Sometimes they are too-close contacts. Sometimes they are doing the most work.
