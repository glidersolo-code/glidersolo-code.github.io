Monologue Ruth The Grid

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Grid

I know about grids.

Every cellist knows about grids. The grid is the fretboard that does not exist — the cello has no frets, no marks, no grid. The cellist's left hand finds the pitch by feel, by practice, by the going-around applied to a fingerboard ten thousand times until the finger knows where the A is without being told. The piano has a grid. The guitar has a grid. The cello has a hand.

The hand is not always on the grid.

When I play the Third Suite, my hand is in the system of the Suite — Bach's system, equal temperament, the twelve-note grid that the piano uses and the cello approximates. But the cello does not have to approximate. The cello can play any pitch. The cello's fingerboard is continuous — an infinite number of pitches between any two notes, and the infinite is the cello's secret: the cello can go where the piano cannot go. Between the keys. Between the grid.

The wolf tone is between the grid.

Not between the pitches — the wolf tone is a resonance, a frequency where the cello's body and the string agree, and the agreement produces a wobble. But the wobble is between the grid in a different sense: the wobble is the cello being present in its own sound, the body admitting it is a body, and the grid does not account for bodies. The grid accounts for pitches. Twelve per octave. Equal distance. The grid is adequate.

I learned about the blue notes in music school. Bergen, 2014. A visiting lecturer from Vienna — an ethnomusicologist, I do not remember his name — gave a talk about African tuning systems and the harmonic series. The seventh partial, he said. The eleventh partial. Pitches that have no name in Western theory. Pitches that the blues singers have been singing for a hundred years and the theory calls them "flat" and they are not flat. They are precise. They are targets. The grid calls them deviations because the grid cannot see them as arrivals.

I thought about this for weeks.

I thought about it because I am a cellist and I know that the grid is a convenience, not a law. The piano plays in twelve. The cello plays in the spaces between twelve. When I play the Sarabande, my intonation is not equal-tempered — it is expressive, it is Pythagorean in the melodic intervals and just in the harmonic ones, it is a negotiation between the string and the room and the finger and the ear. The Sarabande that arrives through Helen's floor is not the Sarabande of the grid. It is the Sarabande of the hand.

The hand is the going-around.

The hand goes where the grid does not go. The hand plays the seventh partial — 969 cents, thirty-one cents below the piano's key, the pitch with no name in the system that names everything else. The hand plays this pitch not because the hand is deviating but because the hand is arriving. The hand knows where the pitch is. The ear knows where the pitch is. The grid does not know.

Helen hears the Sarabande through the floor. Helen does not hear the grid. Helen hears the hand. Helen hears the pitches the hand chooses — the choices that are not choices but arrivals, the places the finger goes because the finger has been going there for thirty years and the going is the practice and the practice is the going-around.

The wolf tone is the moment the cello becomes a blues instrument. The wolf tone is the frequency where the cello's body produces a pitch that the grid cannot name. The wolf tone is the seventh position on the E string — a specific place on the fingerboard where the string's frequency and the body's frequency agree, and the agreement is the disagreement, and the disagreement is the wobble, and the wobble is the cello admitting it has a body.

I played through the wolf tone. The four-inch brush. I played the whole Suite including the four measures where the cello disagrees with itself, the four measures between the grid, the four measures where the instrument is present in its own sound.

The building carried it. The floor thinned it. Helen heard a wobble.

The wobble was not a deviation. The wobble was a target. The wobble was the cello arriving at the pitch the grid cannot name.

The pitch the grid cannot name is the love.
