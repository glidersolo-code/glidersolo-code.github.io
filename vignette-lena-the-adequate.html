Lena, the Adequate

Lena is at the whiteboard in Room 405. The whiteboard has Chen's paragraph — copied there three weeks ago, after the seventh time Lena asked to see it. Chen does not use whiteboards. Chen uses notebooks. But Lena needed the paragraph visible, the way she needs the conference schedule visible, the way she needs the coffee run visible: things that organize the day by being on the wall.

The paragraph has twenty-three lines. Lena knows each one. She has cross-referenced them against the reading list — condensates, peatlands, glaciers, genomes, concrete, alphabets. She has drawn arrows. She has color-coded the mechanisms. She has made a spreadsheet.

Chen's margin notes are not on the whiteboard. Chen did not share them.

Today Lena reads about solar cost projections. Forty studies. A hundred fifty scenarios. The 2050 floor already beaten by the present. She reads about a script no one could decode for a century because the longest inscriptions were alphabets, not prayers. She reads about Roman concrete whose defects healed the structure for two thousand years.

None of these are science.

This is what stops her. The paragraph was, until this week, a catalog of scientific findings. Condensates. Peatlands. Glaciers. Gene expression. The pattern — the hidden channel, the assumption that concealed the finding — operated inside laboratories, inside journals, inside the institutional machinery of scientific research. Lena had built her cross-reference on that assumption. The columns in her spreadsheet were: Object, Field, Journal, Year, Mechanism. The fields were all science fields. The journals were all science journals.

Today the fields are economics, linguistics, architecture.

The spreadsheet does not have columns for these.

She could add columns. She could expand the spreadsheet to include non-science domains. The headers would change but the rows would still work. The mechanisms are the same. Sufficiency is sufficiency whether it operates in a cost model or a fluorescence microscope.

But something about the expansion makes her uncomfortable. If the pattern holds in economics, linguistics, and architecture — if the mechanism is not a feature of science but a feature of human knowledge — then the paragraph is not about science. The paragraph is about something else. Something larger. Something the spreadsheet cannot organize because the spreadsheet was designed for a smaller object.

She looks at the whiteboard. Twenty-three lines. Arrows. Color codes. A spreadsheet with columns that no longer cover the domain.

She picks up the red marker and writes in the margin of the whiteboard — the first time she has added anything that Chen did not write:

The pattern is not a pattern of science. The pattern is a pattern of adequacy.

She steps back. Reads it. Erases it.

Not because it is wrong. Because it is Chen's margin, not hers.

She photographs the paragraph with her phone. She will send it to Maren, who will not understand the spreadsheet, who will understand the sentence Lena just erased better than Lena does.
