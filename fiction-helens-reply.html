Helen's Reply

Helen writes the reply on Thursday afternoon. Ruth is playing downstairs — the Second Suite, the Sarabande, the movement that forgives the ones before it. The morning glory has reached the top of the twine and begun to lean, looking for the next thing to hold.

She uses the blue paper. Not the cream. The cream was for Michael's birthday and for Thomas's graduations, and there is no occasion today that fits either category. The blue paper is for Tuesdays and Thursdays and any day when the thing she wants to say is short enough to deserve the smaller sheet.

She writes:

Thomas,

Saturday is fine. The kettle is the same one.

Bring nothing. I mean it.

Mum

She reads it back. Four lines. Shorter than his, which had four lines and a crossed-out sentence. Hers has no crossed-out sentence because she did not begin a sentence she could not finish. This is not restraint. This is the way the letters have always worked — she writes what she means, and what she means has gotten shorter, and the shortness is not loss. The shortness is the part where the letter becomes a door instead of a description of a door.

She seals it with the sponge. The sponge sits in a saucer by the stove and has sat there since Michael was alive. Michael licked envelopes. She told him once that the glue was horse-derived and he said "the horse doesn't mind" and kept licking. After he died she bought the sponge. It was the first change she made to the kitchen. Not the last. But the first, and the one she thinks about least, and the one that tells the most.

The Sarabande finishes. Ruth starts the Gigue — the fast one, the one that should not follow the Sarabande but does, the way Tuesday follows a funeral. Helen puts the stamp on straight. She has always put stamps on straight. Michael put them on crooked — not from carelessness but from a conviction that the corner of an envelope was not a serious enough surface to warrant alignment. She disagreed. She still disagrees. The stamp goes where the stamp goes, and it goes straight.

She walks to the postbox at the end of the road. It is three minutes if she does not stop, five if she reads the noticeboard outside the chemist's, and seven if Margaret is at the noticeboard and has an opinion about what is posted there. Today it is three. Margaret is not at the noticeboard. The chemist's is closed. The afternoon is doing what afternoons do when no one is watching — moving at its own speed, which is the speed of a woman walking to a postbox with a letter that says bring nothing and means it.

She posts the letter. The slot is stiff. She pushes it with her thumb — the left thumb, the one that is not arthritic, the one that still opens jars and seals envelopes and does the small mechanical work of a life that has been thinned to its best dimensions.

On the walk back, she hears the cello through the open window. Ruth has returned to the Sarabande. She plays it twice sometimes. Helen does not know why. She does not ask. There are things you learn about the person below you by not asking, and the not-asking is its own kind of listening, and the listening is the floor's contribution to the music.

She goes inside. The kettle is where it has always been. She fills it. She does not turn it on. Saturday is three days away, and she has told Thomas to bring nothing, and the kettle will be ready when the kettle needs to be ready, which is when Thomas walks through the door and she can see from his face whether the drive was long or whether the drive was the kind of long that needs tea before it needs conversation.

The morning glory leans. The twine holds. The afternoon continues.
