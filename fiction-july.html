July

The morning glory covered half the window.

Helen stood at the sink and the glass in front of her was half green, half sky. The green was the vine — heart-shaped leaves, tendrils wrapped around the wire, the wire that held a curtain and now held a garden. The sky was the street, the rooftops, the July morning where the light was so long that the evening began at nine and even then the sky was not dark but blue, a different blue from the morning glory's blue, a blue that lasted all night.

The vine had thirty-seven buds. Helen had counted. Thirty-seven blue flowers waiting to be one morning each. Tomorrow's flower was already visible — a twist of blue at the end of a tendril, furled, patient, the size of a thimble.

Margaret's vine had flowered last week. Margaret had not told Helen. Helen knew because Margaret had not told her — the not-telling was louder than the telling, the way Helen's silence on Thursday was louder than her conversation on Wednesday. Margaret's vine was on the third floor, south-facing, and Margaret had given it twine from the kitchen drawer and the twine was Margaret's twine and the climbing was Margaret's climbing and the first flower — blue, one morning, the same blue as Helen's — was Margaret's to hold and not report.

Helen was glad.

Thomas came on Saturday. He sat in the chair and the vine was behind Helen's head and the green made the kitchen look like a greenhouse — not a greenhouse with glass walls and manufactured heat but a greenhouse with one window and one vine and one cup of tea and the light coming through the leaves.

"It's beautiful," Thomas said.

Helen looked at him. Thomas did not use the word beautiful. Thomas used the word good. Thomas used the word enough. Thomas used the word fine. Beautiful was a different register — a four-inch brush applied to a one-inch observation.

"It's a vine," Helen said.

Thomas smiled. "It's Dad's vine."

"It's the vine's vine. Your father grew it. I carried it. The vine grew itself."

"The vine grew itself."

"Everything grows itself. The gardener does not make the growing. The gardener makes the space for the growing. The thinning, the wire, the yogurt pot. The rest is the vine."

Thomas drank his tea. The blue cup. The blue vine. The blue July. Everything blue and everything enough and everything in its place.

At four o'clock he stood. He did not say "same time next week?" and Helen did not say "if you like." They had said it every Saturday for long enough that the saying had thinned, like the letters, until the saying was the standing and the standing was the leaving and the leaving was the next Saturday already arriving.

The going-around.

Thomas drove home. Helen washed the cups. The vine held its thirty-seven buds. Tomorrow, one of them would open. Blue. One morning. The morning glory's going-around.

Helen sat in the chair. Not the visitor's chair. Her chair. The one by the window where the vine was green and the evening was blue and the building was quiet and the boiler was quiet and the floor carried nothing because it was Saturday evening and Ruth played on Wednesday and Wednesday was five days away.

Five days. Like the first five letters.

Helen smiled.

Everything grows itself. The gardener makes the space.

Good.
