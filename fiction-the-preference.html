The Preference

Thomas, Part XLVIII

682. The Shuffle

Every Saturday, Thomas's visit shuffles the flat.

Not physically. Nothing moves. The kettle stays on the counter. The sugar bowl stays beside the kettle. The photographs on the wall do not rearrange themselves. But the arrangement of significance shifts. Before Thomas arrives, the kitchen is a kitchen. After Thomas arrives, the kitchen is the room where "Good" is said, and the counter is the surface Helen turns toward when she reaches for the cup, and the sugar bowl is the object that marks the midpoint of the visit because Thomas adds sugar at the thirty-minute mark and the spoon against the porcelain is a sound that has divided his visits for years.

The significance shuffles. And the shuffling has preferences.

Thomas sits in the chair nearest the door. He has always sat there. He has never chosen it — it was the chair that was available the first Saturday, and the preference accumulated. The path of least resistance through Helen's kitchen runs from the door to the nearest chair to the kettle-side of the table. A dislocation moving through a crystal follows the weakest bonds. Thomas follows the nearest chair.

683. Not Random

Rodrigo Freitas tracked millions of atoms being shuffled by dislocations. The alloy never reached a random state. No matter how much processing — rolling, heating, deforming — the atoms retained patterns. The shuffling had preferences because the bonds had different strengths.

Helen's week never reaches a random state either.

Saturdays are not random. They are patterned by every previous Saturday — by the accumulated preferences of which chair, which cup, which moment the sugar spoon strikes the porcelain. Each visit is a dislocation moving through the crystal of Helen's week, and each visit follows the path of least resistance through the same bond-strength landscape: the kitchen is the weakest bond (easiest to enter), the sitting room is stronger (Thomas has never sat there), the bedroom is impregnable (no visit has penetrated it in forty years).

The filing system would call this "habit." The filing system would call the metal's retained patterns "defects." But the patterns are not failures to randomize. They are the signatures of a process that has preferences built into its architecture.

684. Far from Equilibrium

Some of the atomic patterns Freitas found had no analog under any known equilibrium conditions. They existed only because the dislocations created them. The damage made something that equilibrium could not.

There are things in Helen's week that exist only because of Thomas's visits. Not the visits themselves — the disruptions the visits cause. The way the flat resets after Thomas leaves is not the same as the way the flat rests when Thomas has not been there. The post-visit silence is a different silence than the mid-week silence. The kettle cooling after Thomas's tea is a different cooling than the kettle cooling after Helen's solitary cup.

These states — the post-visit silence, the specific cooling, the particular quality of Monday morning after Saturday's disruption — have no equilibrium analog. They do not exist in a week without Thomas. They are far-from-equilibrium states created by the passage of the visit through the crystal of the week.

The filing system would call the visit a disruption. The visit creates something the undisrupted week could not.

685. The Persistence

No amount of processing erases the short-range order. The patterns survive because the mechanism that was supposed to destroy them is the mechanism that maintains them.

Margaret has been visiting on Tuesdays for three years. She has tried — gently, without saying so — to change the pattern. Different teas. Different topics. Once she brought flowers, which sat on the table for a week and changed the geometry of the kitchen in ways that Margaret did not intend and Helen did not acknowledge.

The pattern did not change. Tuesday retained its specific order — the unfolding of the third chair, the particular way Helen asks about Margaret's children (always the younger one first, never the older one), the moment when Margaret looks at the clock and Helen does not.

The mechanism that Margaret brings — the visit, the conversation, the gentle disruption — is the mechanism that maintains the pattern. The shuffling has preferences. The preferences persist. Margaret's attempts to change the pattern are themselves part of the pattern, the way a dislocation's passage through a crystal maintains the very order it was supposed to disrupt.
