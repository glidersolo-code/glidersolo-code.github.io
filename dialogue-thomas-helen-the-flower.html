Thomas and Helen, the Flower

THOMAS: It flowered.

HELEN: It flowered three weeks ago.

THOMAS: I wasn't here three weeks ago. I was here two weeks ago and it hadn't flowered.

HELEN: It had finished flowering by Saturday. The flowers last one morning. You are never here in the morning.

THOMAS: (looking at the vine) It's covered half the window.

HELEN: Half is enough.

THOMAS: Dad would have —

HELEN: Yes.

THOMAS: He would have painted the wire.

HELEN: He would have painted around the wire. He would have taken the half-inch brush and gone around the vine the way he went around the vine on the fence and the going-around would have taken three hours for six inches of wire and he would have said —

THOMAS: "The fence is glad."

HELEN: "The wire is glad."

(Pause.)

THOMAS: Mum.

HELEN: Yes.

THOMAS: I'm glad I stopped writing.

HELEN: You did not stop writing. The writing arrived.

THOMAS: The writing arrived?

HELEN: The writing arrived where it was going. The first letter said: "The house is sold. Ready?" The last letter said nothing. The nothing was the arrival. You did not stop. You arrived.

THOMAS: (pause) I didn't plan the — the thinning.

HELEN: No one plans the thinning. The thinning plans itself. I did not plan to thin the morning glory — the morning glory thinned itself by growing where the light was and leaving the rest. I did not plan to thin the letters — the letters thinned themselves by saying what needed saying and no more. The thinning is not a choice. The thinning is what happens when you stop adding and start listening.

THOMAS: Listening to what?

HELEN: To the enough. The enough is very quiet. The enough sounds like one cup on the rack. Like the kettle clicking. Like the floor carrying the Sarabande.

THOMAS: Like "good."

HELEN: Like "good."

(The kettle clicks. Helen pours two cups. Thomas holds the blue cup.)

THOMAS: Margaret's morning glory is climbing.

HELEN: I gave her the cutting.

THOMAS: She told me. She said it's on the third floor. South-facing.

HELEN: Margaret's will flower later. Margaret's light is different. But Margaret's will flower.

THOMAS: She said she won't tell you when it flowers.

HELEN: (smiling) She told you she won't tell me.

THOMAS: (laughing) Yes. She told me she won't tell you.

HELEN: That is Margaret's going-around. The not-telling told you. The telling you told me. The going-around goes around.

THOMAS: Mum.

HELEN: Yes.

THOMAS: The visit is the letter now.

HELEN: The visit was always the letter. The letters were the visit learning to be the visit.

(Pause. The floor carries nothing — Ruth is downstairs, but it is Saturday, not Wednesday, and the cello is in its case.)

THOMAS: Same time next week?

HELEN: If you like.

THOMAS: I like.
