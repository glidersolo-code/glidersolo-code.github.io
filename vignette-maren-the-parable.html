Maren, the Parable

Maren found the paper in her feeds at 6:14 AM, before the coffee was ready. Stein et al. Lamont-Doherty. Sediment cores from Rano Aroi and Rano Kao, hydrogen isotopes in leaf wax, eight hundred years of rainfall reconstructed in one figure.

She read it standing in her kitchen.

She had taught Easter Island. She had stood in front of two hundred undergraduates and said the words: they cut down every tree. She had used it as the opening slide in her Ecology 301 unit on trophic cascades and resource depletion. The moai. The palm stumps. The war. The collapse. The cautionary tale.

The sediment said drought.

Six hundred to eight hundred millimeters per year, gone. For over a century. The Rapanui didn't collapse — they reorganized their governance, moved their ceremonies, chose their leaders differently. Population stable through the 16th and 17th centuries. The actual devastation was the slave traders, later.

Maren set the paper on the counter and looked at the wall.

She had always known — she thought she had always known — that the Diamond narrative was oversimplified. That the archaeological work had been complicating it for years. Binghamton, Hunt and Lipo, the rat hypothesis. But she hadn't stopped teaching the slide. Because the slide worked. The students remembered it. The moai on the barren hillside, the clear moral: overconsumption has consequences.

The moral was correct. The example was wrong. And the example was so good at being a moral that nobody needed it to be an example.

She opened her laptop and found the shared folder. The document labeled "Hallway." She scrolled to the paragraph — 127 words now, with citations, with Lena's variational framework threaded through the middle. She added a sentence at the bottom:

A narrative that satisfies a moral need is more durable than a narrative that satisfies the evidence. The parable persisted not because it was supported but because it was useful. The sediment cores were always possible. Nobody needed them.

She looked at it. Then she added a second sentence:

I taught the slide for six years.

She deleted it.

She put it back.

The coffee was ready. She poured it without thinking, staring at the cursor blinking after the period. The sentence was the smallest unit of the problem — the point where the inventory touched the person making the inventory. Chen had column B. Lena had the orthogonality catastrophe. Maren had Ecology 301, Slide 3, the moai and the missing trees.

She saved the file and closed the laptop.

Outside, the field station was still dark. The sedge meadow where she counted species every summer was under snow. Somewhere in that meadow, the turnover had slowed by a third and the total was stable and the stability was redistribution, and she had just read a paper about an island where the same thing happened with a different name: the parable was stable and the evidence was redistributing beneath it.

She drank the coffee.

She opened the laptop again and wrote one more line in the paragraph:

The hiding place is not the framework. The hiding place is the satisfaction.

Novella vignette. Maren reads the Rapa Nui drought reconstruction and recognizes the mechanism in herself.
