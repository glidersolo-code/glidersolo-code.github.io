The Letter, the Grid

I do not use a grid.

I use a postcode. The postcode is a grid — ST4 to OX1, the Royal Mail's system of letters and numbers that reduce a house to a coordinate, a kitchen to a destination, a mother to an address. The postcode is adequate. The postcode delivers me.

But the postcode does not describe me.

The postcode says: this letter goes from here to there. The postcode does not say: this letter carries a sentence that says "bring nothing" and the sentence means "bring everything you are" and the meaning travels three days at second class and the three days are not a delay. The three days are the going-around. The three days are the floor. The three days are the two hundred and fourteen millimeters applied to a sentence until the sentence arrives reduced, essential, the half-inch brush applied to six words.

The grid delivers. The grid does not carry.

The building carries. The building does not use a grid. The building uses distance — two hundred and fourteen millimeters of joist, unequal, imprecise, the distance of a floor built in 1962 by men who did not know they were building the instrument that would carry the Sarabande. The building's distance is not twelve equal steps across an octave. The building's distance is: whatever the joist is. Whatever the plaster is. Whatever the air between them is. The building carries everything the distance allows.

I am the same. I carry everything the postage allows. Second class allows three days. Three days allows the sentence to cool. Three days allows the reader to not-read for one day, two days, and then read on the third day, and the third day's reading is the reading after the going-around, the reading after the distance, the reading that arrives the way the Sarabande arrives through the floor: late, reduced, essential.

The grid says I am a letter: 88mm by 186mm, 23 grams, second class, Oxford to Stoke-on-Trent, ST4 7QR. The grid is adequate. The grid files me under "post."

Between the grid — between the postcode and the kitchen, between the second class and the reading, between the stamp and the hand — is the sentence. The sentence is the pitch the grid cannot name. The sentence says "bring nothing" and the grid cannot hear "bring everything." The sentence says "if you like" and the grid cannot hear "please." The sentence says "the lemon was good" and the grid cannot hear "I noticed."

The grid delivers the letter. The letter carries the sentence. The sentence carries the love.

The love is between the grid.
