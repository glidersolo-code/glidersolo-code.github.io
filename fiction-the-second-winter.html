The Second Winter

December. The vine is dormant. The twine is bare. The masking tape curls. The yogurt pot holds soil and root and fallen leaves and the memory of blue.

Helen sits in the chair. The light through the window is December light — white, low, the kind of light that enters the room at an angle and makes everything in the room look like something it has already been. The chair is the chair. The cup is the cup. The lemon is the lemon. Nothing is new. Everything is the same.

Same.

Thomas came Saturday. He brought the croissant. He sat in the chair. The chair did not vibrate — Ruth is away until January, visiting a sister in Bergen, and the flat below is empty and the floor carries nothing from below and the nothing is loud. Not loud as sound — loud as absence. The Sarabande is not playing. The not-playing is the first time the Sarabande has not been playing on a Saturday in two years.

Helen said: "Ruth is away."

Three words. More than Helen has said in months. The absence of the cello required more words than the presence. The presence was silence because the presence was expected and the expected needs no announcement. The absence was three words because the absence was unexpected and the unexpected needs naming.

Thomas said: "Ah."

One syllable. Not a word. A sound. The sound held: I understand, and I notice the difference, and the difference is the floor is quiet and the quiet is not the usual quiet but a new quiet and the new quiet has a name and the name is absence and the absence is temporary and the temporary is fine.

Margaret came down. Margaret's vine is dormant too. Margaret's notebook is closed. The notebook will reopen in March, when Margaret's cutting will emerge three weeks after Helen's, and the three weeks will be the same three weeks it was last year, because the three weeks is the distance between cotton and copper, between adequate and correct, between the first floor and the third floor, between Helen and Margaret.

The building holds the dormancy. The building has held two dormancies now. The building will hold a third dormancy and a fourth and a tenth and a twentieth and the holding will be the same holding because the building does not tire of holding. The building is the constant.

December. The vine sleeps. The cello is in Bergen. The croissant is from the bakery. The lemon is on the plate. The chair is in the right spot. The window is clear — the leaves are gone, the green light is gone, the blue flowers are gone. The window shows December as it is: grey, low, short.

In March, the vine will emerge. One centimeter of green. Helen will see it. Helen will say —

Nothing.

The nothing will be the third spring's first word.

Good.

Thomas sequence. Part XXV: The Second Spring. December. The second winter. The third spring's first word will be silence.
