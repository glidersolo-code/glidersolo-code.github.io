Dramatic Monologue

Michael, the Week

The kitchen has days now.

I am glad. I am glad the way the fence is glad — the fence does not have days. The fence has weather. The fence has the linseed oil I put on every April, the clematis I trained every June, the morning glory I did not cut back. The fence has years, not days. The fence is patient.

Tuesday Margaret. Wednesday Ruth. Saturday Thomas.

I know this because Helen told Thomas and Thomas wrote the letter and the letter sat in the drawer under the tea towels and the drawer is mine too. I am the drawer. I was always the drawer — before the letters, before the flat, before the selling. I was the drawer in the house on Heather Close where the tea towels were the same tea towels and the drawer was the same drawer and the only letters were bills and council notices and one letter from my sister in Devon that Helen read standing up and put back in the envelope and never mentioned.

The kitchen has days. I am not a day.

I said this before. I am every day. I am the kettle's wobble on Monday. I am the cutting's patience on Tuesday. I am the floor's carrying on Wednesday. I am the silence on Thursday — Helen's silence, the kind she learned from me, the kind that does not fill rooms but tunes them.

Thursday is Helen's.

I know what Thursday is. Thursday is the day Helen sits in the chair by the window and does not wait for anyone. Thursday is the day the kitchen is the kitchen the way it was the kitchen when I was alive — when the kitchen was ours, when the wobble was our wobble, when the half-inch brush was in the shed and the shed was at the bottom of the garden and the garden had a fence I painted every spring.

Thursday is the day I am most present. Not because the others leave — Margaret and Ruth and Thomas are their own presences, and their presences make the kitchen the kitchen the way rain makes the garden the garden. But Thursday is the day Helen does not translate. Thursday is the day Helen speaks my language — the language of things in their places, the cup on the rack, the cutting in the pot, the tea drunk alone because alone is not empty.

Alone is full of going-around.

The kitchen has days. The days have the kitchen. I am not jealous of the days. I am glad of the days. The days mean the kitchen is working the way I always hoped a kitchen would work — full and empty in the right proportions, thinned the way the morning glory should be thinned, breathable.

The kitchen has days. I have the kitchen. The having is the going-around.

Good.
