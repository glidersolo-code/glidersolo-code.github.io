Monologue Helen The Chair

Dramatic Monologue

Helen, the Chair

I moved the chair the second week.

Not the first week. The first week I stood at the sink and heard it through my feet and thought: someone plays the cello. The way you think: it rains. A fact of the building. The building has thin floors and a cellist and both are structural.

The second week I moved the chair because standing at the sink for an hour is not standing at the sink. It is listening and pretending to wash a mug. The chair was honest about what I was doing.

Thomas would say I should have introduced myself. Thomas thinks introductions are necessary. Thomas also thinks visiting is the same as arriving, which is why he stands in my kitchen with his coat still on and calls it a visit. Arriving is what you do before you take off your coat. Visiting is what you do after. Thomas has been arriving for eighteen months.

The chair is in the spot where the vibration is loudest. Not the music — the vibration. The music is downstairs with the cellist. What comes through the floor is the body of the music without the skin: the fundamental without the overtone, the weight without the detail.

Michael would have understood. Michael was a man who painted a fence and went around the morning glory with a half-inch brush. He understood that the reduced version of a thing is sometimes the version that tells you what the thing is for. The fence is for the going-around. The cello is for the vibration. The letter is for the sentence that survives the envelope.

I did not tell Margaret. Margaret would have an opinion. Margaret has opinions the way other people have weather — constantly, visibly, and affecting the plans.

I did not tell Ruth. What would I say? "I sit above you at nine." That is a sentence that requires a response, and the response would change the playing, and the playing does not need to be changed. The playing is correct. The playing is correct because Ruth does not know there is a chair and therefore plays for no one except the discipline and the discipline sounds like forgiveness and the forgiveness is what arrives through the floor.

If Ruth knew she would think about it. She would think: Helen is listening. And the thinking would get into the bow the way a thought gets into a hand and the hand gets into the line and the line changes. I have seen it happen. I saw it happen to Michael the day I told him the fence was beautiful. He never painted the same fence again. Not worse — different. The compliment got into the brush.

So I sit. At nine. In the chair I moved the second week.

The morning glory is on the windowsill. The kettle is on the counter. The vibration comes through the floor. These are the three things the kitchen does at nine, and the three things are the kitchen, and the kitchen is the flat, and the flat is the choice I made when I sold the house and moved here and Thomas said "it's small" and I said "it's enough" and the enough is still enough.

The enough is exactly enough.
