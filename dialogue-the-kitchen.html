The Kitchen

Thomas visits his mother.

"The tiles are new."

"They are not new. I put them in when you were thirteen."

"I would have remembered blue tiles."

"They were always blue, Thomas. You spent four years eating breakfast six inches from them. You looked at your cereal."

"They look new."

"They look clean. I scrubbed them yesterday because you were coming."

"You didn't have to do that."

"I know I didn't have to. I wanted to. There is a difference between doing something because it's required and doing something because someone is coming to see you and you want the kitchen to look like someone lives here who cares about the kitchen."

"It always looks like that."

"It looks like that because I make it look like that. Kitchens do not maintain themselves. This is something you will learn when you have one that belongs to you instead of one that belongs to a landlord."

"I maintain my kitchen."

"You have a toaster and a kettle and a pan you use for everything. I've seen your kitchen. You maintain the minimum viable kitchen."

"That's unfair."

"It's accurate. Unfair and accurate are not the same thing."

"They're not mutually exclusive either."

"No. They are not."

Pause. She puts the kettle on. He watches her reach for the shelf where the tea has been since 1987.

"Are you really selling?"

"I've signed the papers."

"But are you really selling, or did you sign the papers to start a conversation?"

"Both. The papers are real. The conversation is also real. This is one of the things I have learned from forty years in this kitchen: you can do two things at once if one of them is the truth and the other is also the truth."

"You're going to miss it."

"I have been missing things since your father died. Missing is not the same as staying. I can miss this kitchen from Margaret's flat. The missing travels."

"It won't be the same."

"Nothing is the same. The tiles you thought were new have been here since 1998. You think I don't know that things change?"

"I think you know better than me."

"I think so too."

She pours the tea. He holds the cup. It is the cup he has always held — the blue one with the chip on the handle that she has never replaced because, she said once, a cup with a history is better than a cup without one.

"I'll come more often. To the new place."

"You'll come as often as you come. I've made my peace with it. The letters are more reliable than the visits, and the visits are more reliable than the phone calls, and none of them are as reliable as I would like, but that is what it is to have a son who is grown."

"That's not—"

"It's not a complaint. It's a description. You are busy. You have a life. The life is not here, and it hasn't been here for a long time, and that is correct. That is what I raised you to have. It would be strange to raise you to leave and then be upset when you left."

"Are you upset?"

"I'm selling the kitchen, Thomas. I'm not dying. Have your tea."

Dialogue #9. A kitchen, a mother, a son, blue tiles from 1998.
