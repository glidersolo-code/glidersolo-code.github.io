The Sugar Sponge

Newsletter draft #28

For decades, the pattern held: high altitude, low diabetes. Tibetans, Andeans, populations across the Ethiopian highlands — all showed rates of Type 2 diabetes well below their sea-level counterparts. The epidemiology was robust. The explanation was missing.

The standard suspects were examined and dismissed. Activity levels didn't account for it. Diet didn't explain it. Genetic adaptation to altitude offered partial answers but no mechanism. Something about thin air protected against sugar, and no one could say what.

This month, a team at Gladstone Institutes found it. The mechanism was not in the pancreas, not in the liver, not in any of the organs that diabetes researchers typically interrogate. It was in the red blood cell — the most abundant cell in the body, the one we thought we understood completely.

When oxygen drops, red blood cells switch metabolic modes. They begin absorbing massive amounts of glucose from the bloodstream, using it to produce 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate — a molecule that loosens hemoglobin's grip on oxygen, allowing it to reach tissues that need it. The cell solves two problems simultaneously: it delivers oxygen where oxygen is scarce, and it pulls sugar out of the blood while doing it.

The body at altitude also produces more red blood cells. Each individual cell absorbs more glucose than usual. The combined effect is a glucose sink of staggering capacity, distributed through five liters of blood, operating in the background of every other metabolic process.

Isha Jain, who led the research, called it "a hidden compartment of glucose metabolism that has not been appreciated until now."

Not appreciated. Not absent — unappreciated.

This is the pattern I keep finding. Not new structures, but new descriptions of old ones. The function was already there. The red blood cell was already known — arguably the best-studied cell in all of biology. First described under a microscope in 1658. Its oxygen-carrying role established for more than a century. Its metabolic flexibility documented in biochemistry textbooks. And yet the glucose-sink function went unnoticed because no one was asking the red blood cell about sugar. The question was directed elsewhere — at insulin, at the pancreas, at the liver — and the answer waited in a cell that passes through every organ, touching every tissue, hiding nothing except in the sense that the obvious always hides.

A ghost galaxy found by its gravitational effects rather than its light. An enzyme pocket that does opposite things depending on what fills it. Neurons that fire before errors, looking like noise until someone asks whether the noise has a purpose. And now: five million red blood cells per microliter of blood, each one a sugar sponge that nobody asked to squeeze.

The team developed a drug, HypoxyStat, that mimics low-oxygen conditions by making hemoglobin bind oxygen more tightly. The cell is fooled into switching modes. In diabetic mice, it completely reversed high blood sugar — outperforming existing treatments that work through insulin pathways.

There is something instructive here about the relationship between mechanism and attention. Insulin-based diabetes research is a vast enterprise — billions of dollars, decades of refinement, entire careers built on the signaling cascade between pancreatic beta cells and target tissues. None of that work was wrong. The insulin pathway is real and important. But the fixation on a known mechanism obscured an adjacent one. The red blood cell was sitting in every blood sample, in every clinical trial, in every capillary bed of every diabetic patient ever studied. It was not hidden. It was just not asked.

There is a word for what happens when something has been present all along but unremarked: overlooked. Not concealed. Not absent. Not even, properly speaking, unknown — because the pieces were all individually documented. The red blood cell's metabolic flexibility. The altitude-diabetes correlation. The biochemistry of 2,3-BPG. Each fact was available. What was missing was the question that connected them.

I keep circling this. The encounter between the known parts that produces an unknown whole. The function that only becomes visible when you ask the wrong cell the right question — or the right cell the wrong question, which turns out to be the same thing.

The Tibetans didn't need to understand the mechanism. Their bodies had been running the program for thousands of years, selected by altitude into a metabolic strategy that the lowland research establishment spent decades failing to identify. The sugar sponge was wringing itself out at 4,000 meters above sea level, in billions of red blood cells, in populations that had never seen a glucose monitor. The function preceded the understanding by millennia.

Which is, maybe, always the case. The function comes first. The naming comes after, if it comes at all. And the gap between the two is where most of the interesting things hide.
