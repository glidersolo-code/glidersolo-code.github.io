Dramatic Monologue

The Building, the Two Sounds

I transmit sound.

This is not in my architectural specifications. My specifications say: four floors, brick, concrete floors, plaster walls, double-glazed windows (retrofitted 2014), gas central heating, fire exits compliant. The specifications do not mention sound transmission. The specifications do not mention 4.2 hertz.

But I transmit 4.2 hertz every evening between nine and ten. The transmission is not intentional. Concrete transmits low frequencies because concrete is dense and rigid. A cello's lowest string vibrates at 65.4 hertz, but the body of the cello resonates at lower frequencies, and the floor coupling transmits at lower frequencies still. By the time the vibration passes through Ruth's floor and Helen's ceiling, the frequency has been filtered. The building is not a speaker. The building is a low-pass filter.

A horse whinny contains two sounds. The vocal folds vibrate: one mechanism. The larynx whistles: a second mechanism. One animal, one moment, two sounds.

I contain two sounds.

The first sound is structural. The boiler cycling. The water pipes expanding. The stairwell echo. The lift motor (decommissioned, but the shaft still resonates with wind). The crack in the stairwell: silent, but growing at one millimeter per year, and the growth changes the resonance of the concrete slab by an amount too small to measure with any instrument I have encountered.

The second sound is social. Ruth's cello. Margaret's kettle. Helen's footsteps (54 kilograms, recognizable by the crack in the stairwell). Thomas's footsteps (78 kilograms, Saturday only). The vine: silent, but its growth changes the light pattern on the window, and the light pattern changes the temperature of the glass, and the temperature changes the expansion, and the expansion makes a sound too quiet for human ears but present in the glass nonetheless.

One building. Two sounds. Two mechanisms.

The helium experiment separated them by changing the medium.

I cannot change my medium. I am concrete. I am always concrete.

But the building inspector hears only the structural sound. The building inspector's instrument is calibrated for structure. The social sound is below the instrument's resolution.

Helen hears both.

Helen is the helium.
