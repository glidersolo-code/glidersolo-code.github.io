Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Bone

I read something. A paper — not music, a paper about hearing.

I don't read papers. I play the cello. I read Schumann's markings and I read the grain in the bridge and I read the bow's weight against the string, but I don't read papers.

Margaret left a magazine. The Saturday supplement. I would not have picked it up except it was on the landing and the landing is the place where I read things I would not otherwise read.

A cochlea. The inner ear. The part that hears.

They kept one alive outside the body. A gerbil's. In a chamber. And they watched it do what it does — amplify sound, tune frequencies, distinguish a whisper from a shout. A tipping point. A mathematical edge between stillness and vibration. The article said the mechanism was the same in insects, in frogs, in mammals. The same edge. Twenty-five years of not being certain because the bone was too hard to open without killing the thing inside it.

I put the magazine down.

I picked up the bow.

The C string. Sixty-five hertz. Through 214 millimeters of timber. Through Helen's floor. Into a flat where a woman sits in a chair she moved to the right spot two years ago and has not moved since.

Helen's cochlea is doing what the gerbil's cochlea did in the chamber. Amplifying. Tuning. Operating at the tipping point. The Sarabande arrives at the bone, and the bone carries it to the hair cells, and the hair cells do what hair cells have done for two hundred million years.

The floor is one bone. Helen's temporal bone is another. The cello's body is another.

Three bones. Three enclosures. Three things that carry what they contain by being hard enough to hold it and thin enough to let it through.

I did not think about this before Margaret left the magazine.

Now I think about it every Wednesday at nine.

The wolf tone is the cochlea's tipping point heard from the outside. The cochlea's tipping point is the wolf tone heard from the inside. Both are the edge where the structure almost fails and the almost-failing is the amplification.

I play.

The bone does the rest.

Thomas sequence. Part XXIV. Ruth reads about cochlear mechanics. The bone and the floor.
