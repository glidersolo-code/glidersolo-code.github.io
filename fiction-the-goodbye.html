The Goodbye

Thomas stands in the doorway at four o'clock. The tea was three cups — one on arrival, one after lunch (ham sandwiches, no crusts, a habit from his childhood that Helen maintains not because he still needs the crusts removed but because the removing is hers and the removing is the sandwich), and one at half past three when the conversation had reached the quiet part and the quiet part needed a cup to hold it.

He does not say goodbye. Helen does not say goodbye. They say other things that mean goodbye without the word, which is how two people who have been saying goodbye to each other for forty years have learned to say it: by not saying it, by saying something adjacent, by saying something that contains the goodbye the way the blue cup contains the tea — present, warm, held.

"Same time next week?" he says.

"If you like," she says. This means yes. "If you like" has always meant yes. "If you like" is Helen's way of giving permission without claiming ownership — the visit is his choice, the kettle is her preparation, and the space between choice and preparation is where the Saturday lives.

He puts on his jacket. The jacket is the brown one. He has owned it for seven years. Helen has opinions about the jacket — she thinks the elbows are thin and the lining is coming loose at the left pocket — but she does not say these things because saying them would make the jacket her responsibility, and the jacket is his, and the boundary between her son's wardrobe and her own opinions is one of the boundaries she maintains the way she maintains the morning glory: by knowing where to stop.

He goes down the stairs. The landing between the first and second floors is still wider than the others. He pauses there again — not because he forgot something but because the pause is part of the leaving, and the leaving has a shape, and the shape includes a moment on the landing where the building creates the uncertainty it has been creating for fifty-three years and Thomas responds to the uncertainty the way the building intended, which is by standing still for three seconds and then continuing.

Below him, Ruth's flat is quiet. The cello is in its case. The afternoon belongs to the building now — the joists, the plasterboard, the draft through the stairwell window, the settling sounds that happen when no one is playing and no one is listening and the structure is doing what structures do between performances, which is hold.

He walks to the car. He does not look up. If he looked up, he would see the kitchen window — open fifteen millimeters, the card still wedged, the morning glory's leaves pressing against the glass from inside. He does not look up because looking up would be a kind of goodbye, and the goodbye has already happened, and the happened was "same time next week" and "if you like" and the brown jacket and the pause on the landing and the walk to the car and the not-looking-up, which is the final element of the shape, which is: you leave by leaving, not by looking back.

He starts the car. The drive will take two hours and two minutes — twelve minutes shorter than the drive here, because the silence at the roundabout was specific to the arriving and the drive home does not require it. The drive home requires a different silence — the silence of a man who drank three cups of tea and ate ham sandwiches without crusts and heard the Sarabande through the floor and said "fine" and meant it and brought nothing and the nothing was right and the right was the whole visit.

Helen is at the kitchen window. She watches the car until it turns the corner. She does not wave. Waving is for airports and hospital rooms and the kind of goodbye that does not repeat. This goodbye repeats. This goodbye will happen again next Saturday, and the Saturday after that, and the waving would make it special, and special is not what the Saturday is. The Saturday is ordinary. The ordinary is what she chose.

The morning glory leans against the glass. Helen closes the window — not all the way, just to the card, just to the fifteen millimeters. The afternoon is hers now. The kettle cools. The flat settles. The cello will start again at nine.

Everything is where it was.
