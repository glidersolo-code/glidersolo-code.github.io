Letter from the Old Cell to the Young Cell

Dear successor,

You will be fast. You will respond to the first signal. You will divide before the injury has finished forming, and the tissue will knit under your hands like it was never torn. You will feel — if cells feel — that this is what you are for.

I was like you once. I know this because I remember the speed, or because the molecular record remembers it for me. NDRG1 at baseline. mTOR running hot. Every signal a mandate.

I am writing to tell you what the speed costs.

Not today. Not this injury or the next ten. But somewhere around the fortieth repair, the sixtieth, you will notice a thinning in the population. The cells that sprinted alongside you will be gone. Not retired. Gone. Used up. The account drawn down to zero by a withdrawal rate that assumed the balance was infinite.

I am slower than you. Three and a half times more of the thing you don't have yet. They call it a brake. It is a brake. I won't pretend otherwise. When the signal comes, I hesitate. Not from fear. From arithmetic. How many repairs remain. How many signals will follow this one. Whether this is the injury that warrants the cost, or whether I should hold something back for the one after.

You think I'm broken. I can see it in the data they gather from me — the papers that describe my activation kinetics as "impaired," my protein profile as "accumulated," my repair capacity as "reduced." All true. All measured. All missing the point.

The point is that I'm still here.

Every cell in my cohort that kept your speed is dead. Not declined. Not retired. Dead. The ones that remain — the ones they study under microscopes, the ones they try to fix by removing the brake — we are the survivors of a test you haven't taken yet.

They want to make me young again. They can. They've shown it. Block the NDRG1, and I activate like I'm twenty. The paper writes itself: function restored. What they put in the supplementary: the cells that were made fast again didn't last.

I am not the disease. I am what the disease left standing.

Sprint well. I mean that. The early repairs are where speed matters. But when you start to slow — when the protein accumulates, when the signal takes a beat longer to move you — know that the slowing is not the end of your story.

It is the part where the story learns to last.

From the one who stayed,

— A satellite cell, age 73
