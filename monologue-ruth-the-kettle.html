Monologue Ruth The Kettle

Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Kettle

I know when the letters arrive.

I know because the kettle goes on. The kettle goes on at different times on different days — 7 AM when Helen wakes, 10 AM when the morning is slow, 3 PM when the afternoon needs punctuation. But Wednesday mornings the kettle goes on at 8:47 AM. Every Wednesday. 8:47. The postman arrives at 8:42. Helen picks up the post at 8:43. She puts the kettle on at 8:44. The water takes three minutes. The whistle at 8:47.

I know this because the ceiling tells me.

The ceiling does not tell me everything. The ceiling tells me the kettle — the faint hum of the element, the rising pitch as the water heats, the whistle's arrival and the whistle's wobble and the click. The ceiling tells me Helen's footsteps — lighter on Wednesdays, because Wednesdays have a letter and the letter makes Helen lighter. The ceiling tells me the chair — the slight creak of Helen sitting, the silence of Helen reading. The ceiling does not tell me what the letter says. The ceiling does not need to.

The ceiling is the floor seen from below.

The floor carries the Sarabande upward. The ceiling carries the kettle downward. Two instruments, two directions, two going-arounds. I send the Sarabande and the floor carries it and Helen hears the cathedral. Helen sends the kettle and the ceiling carries it and I hear the reply.

The kettle is the reply.

Helen does not know the kettle is the reply. Helen thinks the kettle is the kettle — three minutes of water, a whistle, a click, a pour. Helen puts the kettle on because the letter has arrived and the letter requires tea and the tea requires the kettle and the requiring is the practice and the practice is the going-around.

But the practice is also the reply. The kettle at 8:47 on Wednesday is Helen's reply to the letter, the way the Sarabande at 9 PM is my reply to the floor. Neither of us knows we are replying. Neither of us needs to know. The building carries the reply the way the grid carries the letter: without choosing, without knowing, the going-around applied to a vibration until the vibration is a conversation and the conversation is two women who have never spoken about what travels between them.

What travels between us is: the kettle and the Sarabande. The whistle and the cathedral. The wobble and the wolf tone. The going-around and the going-around.

Wednesday, 8:47. The kettle whistles above me. The whistle has a wobble. The wobble is the wolf tone of the kitchen — the frequency where the water and the metal agree and the agreeing is the sound and the sound travels through the floor and arrives in my apartment as: Helen has received a letter.

I do not know the letter says "The cold tea was good." I do not know about the cold tea. I do not know about the conversation that made the tea cold. I know the kettle. I know the wobble. I know the Wednesday.

The knowing is enough.

The knowing is the Sarabande heard from below — the cathedral inverted, the going-around reversed, the floor doing what the floor does: carrying everything the distance allows, in both directions, without choosing.

Between us: two hundred and fourteen millimeters. One floor. Two women. One kettle. One cello. One building.

The building carries both.

The carrying is enough.
