The Tea

Helen pours the tea the way she has always poured tea: kettle to pot, pot to cup, milk after, one sugar for Thomas and none for herself. The sugar is in a bowl with a lid. The lid has a chip on the rim where Michael once put it down too hard after an argument about whether sugar should have a lid, and the chip has been there for thirty years, and the argument is over, and the chip remains as the only record of a disagreement so small that neither party could remember the positions taken, only that positions were taken, and that the lid survived.

Thomas wraps his hands around the cup. The cup is the blue one. There are four cups and he always gets the blue one. He does not choose the blue one. Helen gives him the blue one. He has not asked why. The asking would change the giving, and the giving as it stands — wordless, automatic, the cup placed on the table in the position closest to his left hand because he is left-handed and Helen knows he is left-handed in the way she knows everything about him, which is: without needing to prove it — is better than the asking.

The kitchen is small. Margaret would say too small. The window is open fifteen millimeters — the card is still there, the morning glory is still climbing, the twine is taut with the weight of a plant that weighs nothing and holds everything. Thomas looks at the morning glory. He does not know its history. He sees a plant on a string in a kitchen window. He does not see the yogurt pot, the cutting, the clematis it descended from, the fence at Heather Close where Michael's half-inch brush went around it every May. He sees green leaves against glass and light coming through the leaves and the light is the kind of light that exists in small kitchens at midday when the window is open and the tea is hot and the person across the table is your mother and your mother is happy and the happiness is the size of the room.

"How's the drive?" she says.

"Fine," he says. This is the correct answer. The drive was two hours and fourteen minutes with a twelve-minute stop at a petrol station where he bought nothing and a roundabout where he sat through a green light, but "fine" is the word that means I am here, the drive is over, the being here is the point. Helen knows this. She taught him this, not with words but with letters that got shorter, and the shortness taught him that "fine" is not evasion — "fine" is efficiency, and efficiency in a conversation between a mother and a son who have been talking for forty years is a form of trust, not a form of avoidance.

She sits. The chair is the wooden one, the one she bought when she moved in, the one that does not match the table. The not-matching is deliberate in the way that all of Helen's decisions are deliberate: she saw the chair, she liked the chair, the chair did not match, and the not-matching was acceptable because matching is a form of coordination and coordination is a form of effort and the flat is the place where certain kinds of effort have been retired.

Below them, Ruth begins the Sarabande. Thomas hears it through the floor — muffled, warm, the high frequencies absorbed by the joists and the plasterboard, the low frequencies arriving like a pulse. He does not know Ruth plays it twice. He does not know Helen sits in a chair positioned six feet from the table, above the cello, in a spot she chose the second week. He knows the sound is there. He knows the sound has been there every time he has visited. He knows the sound is part of the flat the way the morning glory is part of the window — present, unexplained, essential.

"That's nice," he says.

"Yes," she says.

The Sarabande continues. The tea cools. The morning glory leans. The Saturday unfolds at the speed of a kitchen in March, which is the speed of two people who have subtracted everything they do not need and are left with the tea, the cup, the window, the sound through the floor, and each other, and each other is enough.
