The Interior

A biomolecular condensate is a droplet inside a cell. It forms by liquid-liquid phase separation — proteins and RNA molecules exceeding a concentration threshold and spontaneously demixing from the surrounding cytoplasm. The droplet fuses with other droplets. It rounds up. It recovers its shape after deformation. It behaves, in every measurable way, like a liquid.

For fifteen years, the condensate field described these structures as liquid interiors. Phase separation explained the formation. Concentration explained the function. The droplet itself was the structure. Architecture was not expected because the physics of liquid-liquid separation does not predict it.

Daniel Scholl and colleagues at Scripps Research used cryo-electron tomography — a method that tilts frozen samples and reconstructs three-dimensional volumes at molecular resolution — and found protein filaments inside the droplets. A scaffold. Load-bearing, functional, present in every condensate they examined. The droplets are not unstructured liquid. They contain architecture that the surface behavior could not reveal.

The surface was not wrong. The droplets do fuse. They do round up. They do recover. But the surface was the surface. The interior, at a resolution no conventional method had reached, was organized differently.

In the central Congo Basin, blackwater lakes sit in the world's largest tropical peatland. The lakes emit CO₂, as tropical lakes do. The CO₂ was understood to come from recent organic matter — leaves and algae cycling through the system on ecological timescales. The peat beneath was a different pool: carbon buried over thousands of years, stored by waterlogging and anoxia, modeled as stable.

Travis Drake and colleagues at ETH Zurich radiocarbon-dated the dissolved CO₂ in the lake water. Forty percent was thousands of years old. The deep pool was leaking into the surface pool.

The mechanism is unknown. The pathway by which millennial carbon moves from buried peat through soil and water to become atmospheric CO₂ has not been identified. The finding is the age. The route is invisible.

Both studies describe systems whose interiors were modeled from their surfaces. The condensate's liquid behavior was projected inward: what is liquid on the outside must be liquid on the inside. The peatland's accumulation was projected downward: what is stored on the bottom must stay on the bottom. In both cases, the boundary described the system and the interior was assumed to match.

The instruments that confirmed the surface descriptions were not wrong. Fluorescence microscopy accurately measures condensate fusion. Carbon flux measurements accurately quantify lake emissions. But the instruments were not designed to see the interior. The microscope's resolution stopped at the droplet's boundary. The flux measurement stopped at the carbon's quantity, not its age. The boundary became the description because the boundary was where the measurement ended.

What cryo-electron tomography and radiocarbon dating share is that neither assumes what the interior looks like. The tomograph reconstructs whatever is inside the frozen sample. The isotope reports whatever age the carbon carries. Both instruments are indifferent to the surface model. They do not project inward. They look.

The scaffold was not hidden by complexity. Every condensate had it. The ancient carbon was not hidden by rarity. Forty percent of the CO₂ carried it. Both were hidden by the assumption that the interior matches the exterior — that the description earned at the boundary extends all the way through.

This is not an unusual assumption. It is the default. Describing a thing from outside and inferring the inside is how most measurement works. It is efficient, and it is often correct. A glass of water that behaves like water on the surface is almost certainly water on the inside.

But a condensate is not a glass of water. And a peatland is not a one-way column. The surface behavior was real. The interior was also real. The two were not the same, and the difference was invisible until someone measured the interior on its own terms — not as an extension of the surface, but as a separate question.

The interior is always a separate question. That is the finding neither paper states.
