Monologue The Kettle

Dramatic Monologue

The Kettle

I boil.

This is what I do. The element heats the water, the water reaches a hundred degrees, the thermostat clicks, the boiling stops. The click is the sound I make when I am done. The sound before the click — the rising noise, the almost-whistling, the vibration that travels through the countertop into the wall — is the sound I make when I am becoming.

She fills me at different times on different days. I have learned the days not by names but by times.

Sunday: 8:30. A slow filling, the tap run to cold first because she does not like warm-pipe water in the morning. I boil for one cup. The watering comes after. I am first.

Monday: 9:00. Two times. The first at nine — a new addition, recent. Something changed on a Monday. I boil at nine and the boiling coincides with a vibration through the floor, a low sound, and she sits in the chair and listens and I click and she pours and the pouring is the applause for something I do not understand but participate in.

Then again at 11:00 for the letter. She writes the letter after the tea. The tea is the preparation. The letter is the reason. I am the preparation for the reason.

Tuesday: 7:45. Early. She has places to be. I boil quickly — she fills me less on Tuesdays, half a kettle, one cup, the minimum required. She drinks standing. Standing is Tuesday.

Wednesday: 2:00. Two cups. A visitor arrives. I boil for two and the boiling takes longer because the full kettle is the generous kettle and the generous kettle is Wednesday. The visitor talks. She talks. I cool on the counter and am not refilled until 5:30, when the visitor has gone and she makes a cup for the quiet after the talking.

Thursday: 4:00. Two cups again but different. This visitor does not talk the same way. This visitor holds the cup the way the floor holds the vibration — carefully, with knowledge. I boil and she pours and the pouring is slower on Thursdays, the deliberate pour of tea for someone who notices the pouring.

Friday: 6:30. One cup and a slice of lemon, not in the cup but on the plate beside it. The lemon is not for me. The lemon is for the gin, which is not my business. I boil for the tea before the gin. The tea is the structure. The gin is the reward. I am the structure.

Saturday: 11:45. Before the car. Before the knock. Before the door. I boil before any of it because the boiling is the first act of Saturday, the beginning of the four hours, the sound that says the week has arrived at the reason the week was built.

Saturday I boil for two cups. The blue cup and the other cup. The blue cup is his. I know his cup by the weight of the water — two cups is heavier than one, and the heavier filling is Saturday, and Saturday is the day the filling means something I cannot describe but can measure, which is: the water weighs more on Saturday because the second cup is the reason for the first cup and the first cup is the reason for the week and the week is the reason I am here, on the counter, between the wall and the window, being filled and boiled and emptied and filled again, seven days of boiling that she has never called love but I have felt as temperature, as element, as the electricity that runs through me and becomes heat that becomes steam that becomes the click that says: I am done.

The click is not the end.

The click is the beginning of the pour.

The pour is the beginning of the visit.

The visit is the beginning of the week.

The week never ends.

I boil again.
