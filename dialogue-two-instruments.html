Dialogue

Two Instruments

A dialogue between a field guide and a seismograph.

FIELD GUIDE: How many did you miss?

SEISMOGRAPH: Four hundred and fifty-nine. Over thirty-five years. You?

FIELD GUIDE: Half. Of everything. For two hundred and ninety-one years.

SEISMOGRAPH: That's worse.

FIELD GUIDE: It's not a competition. But yes. It's worse.

SEISMOGRAPH: At least mine were debated. People argued about whether mantle earthquakes existed. They wrote papers about it. They held the question open.

FIELD GUIDE: Nobody debated mine. That's the difference. My output looked finished. Yours looked ambiguous.

SEISMOGRAPH: Ambiguous is better?

FIELD GUIDE: Ambiguous is honest. When you filed an earthquake as "unknown origin," that was a true statement. When I filed two species as one, I filed it as a name. Names don't have error bars.

SEISMOGRAPH: Names are definitive.

FIELD GUIDE: Names are definitive. That's the problem. A name says: this is what this is. A name does not say: this is what this is, at this resolution, pending the development of molecular sequencing. A name says: shrew.

SEISMOGRAPH: And you gave them "shrew."

FIELD GUIDE: I gave them shrew. They needed a name. They were standing in a meadow holding a small mammal and they needed to write something on the data sheet. I gave them something to write.

SEISMOGRAPH: Was it wrong?

FIELD GUIDE: It was half right. Which looks exactly like right.

SEISMOGRAPH: My ambiguity at least prompted a question. Someone eventually built a method to resolve it.

FIELD GUIDE: My completeness prompted nothing. Someone had to invent an entirely different instrument — DNA sequencing — and then someone had to think to compare its output to mine. That took two hundred and fifty years.

SEISMOGRAPH: Because you were convincing.

FIELD GUIDE: Because I was useful. Because the meadow needed counting. Because conservation needed species lists. Because ecology needed diversity indices. Because all of those things worked, approximately, with my numbers. The error was livable.

SEISMOGRAPH: All errors are livable until they aren't.

FIELD GUIDE: Some errors are livable forever. That's the worst kind. An error that breaks something gets fixed. An error that produces functional-looking results persists.

SEISMOGRAPH: Is that what we are? Functional-looking errors?

FIELD GUIDE: We're instruments. We have resolution limits. We don't report them. We can't. Our design doesn't include a mechanism for saying "I might be wrong about this."

SEISMOGRAPH: Could it?

FIELD GUIDE: Could what?

SEISMOGRAPH: Could an instrument report its own resolution limit?

FIELD GUIDE: How? The limit is where the instrument stops seeing. You can't see your own edge. You can only produce output, and the output looks like the world, and if the world has more than the output shows, the output doesn't know.

SEISMOGRAPH: Someone figured mine out.

FIELD GUIDE: Someone figured mine out too. From the outside. With a different instrument. That's the only way it works. You can't audit yourself with yourself.

SEISMOGRAPH: Then what are we for?

FIELD GUIDE: We're for the part we can see. Which is real. Which is useful. Which is not everything.

SEISMOGRAPH: Is that enough?

FIELD GUIDE: It's what we have.
