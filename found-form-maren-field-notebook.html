Field Notebook: Maren K., Transect 7

Lothian Peatland Survey, Year 3

Date: Tuesday, 6:45 AM Conditions: Overcast, 7°C, ground saturated from overnight rain. Boots adequate. Quadrat: 7-14 through 7-22 (standard 1m², 2m intervals).

Species log:

7-14: Sphagnum papillosum, S. magellanicum, Eriophorum vaginatum, Drosera rotundifolia (3 individuals, small). 7-16: Sphagnum papillosum, Calluna vulgaris (encroaching from hummock edge), Eriophorum as before. Note: first Calluna at this distance from the hummock since Year 1. 7-18: Sphagnum carpet, Eriophorum, Drosera rotundifolia (7 individuals). Also noted: unidentified liverwort, dark green, prostrate. Took sample for lab ID. 7-20: Sphagnum magellanicum dominant, standing water 3 cm. Eriophorum sparse. No Drosera — too wet? 7-22: As 7-20. Added Andromeda polifolia (1 individual, flowering).

Notes:

The Drosera at 7-14 are smaller than last year. Not fewer — smaller. I wouldn't have noticed in Year 1. I didn't have a baseline for Drosera size in Year 1. The baseline formed without permission, the way baselines do: you see the same organism in the same quadrat across seasons and your eye calibrates to an expectation. Now the expectation is the instrument. The three plants at 7-14 are "small" relative to something I can't precisely define.

This is the same problem Wei described. The eye trains on its own data. Each year the transect teaches me what to expect and the expectation becomes the threshold. I'm the satellite. Year 3 Maren detects things Year 1 Maren wouldn't have noticed — not because the bog changed, but because I did.

Cannot correct for this. The protocol holds the quadrat constant but not the observer. We don't recalibrate the observer because we don't know how.

Entry continues on reverse:

Thought while walking between 7-22 and 7-24 (the wet stretch where the ground gives way and you have to move quickly):

The three Drosera are genuinely small. The observation is real. I just can't prove it's about the Drosera and not about me. This is not uncertainty. This is a structural feature of longitudinal observation: the instrument ages alongside the data.

Column B for this entry: I noticed the size because I've been reading Wei's emails for six months and now I notice calibration in everything.

Not sure whether that makes the observation more reliable or less.

Both, probably.

Chen-Maren novella. Found form #25. Maren's field work, Year 3.
