The Drive Home (XXIII)

Thomas drives home thinking about nothing.

This is not emptiness. This is the negative pressure of the drive — the space between the visit and the week, the decompression that happens when the acknowledgment is complete and the data has been recorded and the instrument is heading back to the laboratory.

Thomas does not think about Helen. Thomas does not think about the vine. Thomas does not think about the crack or the kettle or the Sarabande he sometimes hears through the floor or the floor itself or the landing where he once met Ruth carrying a cello case.

Thomas thinks about the road.

The road is sufficient. The road is forty minutes of tarmac and hedgerows and the occasional tractor and the roundabout and the petrol station and the turn. The road is the cytoplasm — the dense medium through which the instrument travels between measurements.

The measurement is complete. The data is Good. The instrument is returning.

Thomas pulls into the drive. Thomas parks. Thomas walks to the house. Thomas makes tea. Thomas sits in a different chair — his chair, not Helen's visitor chair — and drinks the tea in a different silence — his silence, not Helen's silence.

Thomas's silence is positive pressure. Thomas's silence is full of the things he could say — to his wife, to his colleague, to the neighbor — and chooses not to say. Thomas's silence is the kettle: full, pressurized, capable of whistling.

Helen's silence is negative pressure. Helen's silence is empty of the things she once said, and the emptiness pulls.

The difference between the two silences is the drive.

The drive converts one kind of silence into the other, and the conversion is the visit, and the visit is sufficient.

"How was she?" Thomas's wife asks.

"Good," Thomas says.

Thomas sequence. The drive home as decompression. Two silences: Thomas's (positive pressure, full) and Helen's (negative pressure, pulling). The drive converts. "Good."
