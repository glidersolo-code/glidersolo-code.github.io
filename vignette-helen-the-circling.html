Helen, the Circling

She noticed on a Tuesday.

The tip of the morning glory was not where it had been on Monday. Not higher — the growing was too slow to see in a day — but shifted, angled slightly to the left, as if the plant had been thinking about something and the thinking had changed its direction.

By Wednesday the tip had moved again. To the right this time, past the place it had been on Monday, continuing in what she now understood was a circle. The morning glory was circling the twine the way a hand circles above a table looking for the right drawer.

She mentioned it to Margaret.

"It circles?"

"The tip. Very slowly. You can't see it move. But if you look in the morning and look again at night, it's moved."

"That's just growth, Helen."

"Growth goes up. This goes around."

Margaret looked at the morning glory and decided it was adequate. Helen looked at the morning glory and saw it searching.

She did not look it up. She did not go to the library or ask Ruth or write to Thomas about it. She sat in the chair by the window and watched the twine and thought about the circling, and the thought was: everything I do is the circling.

Sunday: water. The watering is the circle starting. Monday: the letter. The circle moving. Tuesday: the shops. The circle widening. Wednesday: Margaret. The circle having company. Thursday: Ruth. The circle hearing music. Friday: the lemon. The circle preparing. Saturday: Thomas. The circle arriving at the thing it was looking for.

And then Sunday again. And the watering. And the circle starting again. Not because the circle has ended but because the circling is the thing itself, and the thing itself does not end, and she has been doing it since the flat, since the house, since Michael painted around the morning glory every April with the half-inch brush, which was his circle, which was his reaching, which was his way of saying the same thing the morning glory says with its tip moving through the air too slowly to watch:

I am looking for the vertical thing. I am looking for the thing that lets me hold on. I do not know where it is. I will keep circling until I find it.

She found it. She found the flat, the twine, the blue cup, the kettle, the Sarabande through the floor, the weekly letters, the Saturday, the son who brings nothing and fills everything.

The morning glory found the twine.

The circling stopped. The holding began.

No — the circling did not stop. The circling became the holding. The holding is the circling that found its pole and tightened, and the tightening is the helix, and the helix is the shape of a life wrapped around the thing it needs, and the wrapping is the love, and the love is so slow you cannot see it move.

You can only see that it has moved, on a Tuesday, when you look at where the tip was and where the tip is and you understand that the distance between them is a day's worth of reaching, and the reaching is the whole of it.
