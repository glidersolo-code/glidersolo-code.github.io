Monologue Helen The Drawer

Dramatic Monologue

Helen Speaks: The Drawer

The drawer has twelve envelopes.

The first envelope has five sentences. The twelfth envelope has no sentences. The trajectory is clear — the sentences left the envelopes the way the words left my vocabulary, one by one, each departure making the remaining words heavier, the remaining silence denser, the remaining envelope lighter.

The twelfth envelope weighs two grams. The first envelope weighs five grams. The weight of three grams is the weight of everything Thomas has learned to stop saying.

I did not teach him this. I did not say: write less. I did not say: the envelope is enough. I said nothing. I said nothing because nothing was what needed to be said, and Thomas, who visits every Saturday and sits in the chair and says "Good" and drives home for forty minutes thinking one thought, Thomas understood the nothing the way the vine understands the twine: by wrapping around it, by holding on, by climbing.

The drawer is in the kitchen, second from the top, left of the cooker. The drawer holds: twelve envelopes, a pen that does not work, a takeaway menu from a restaurant that closed, and a photograph of Michael standing next to the clematis at Heather Close. The photograph is not in a frame. The photograph is in the drawer because the drawer is where things go that do not need to be displayed but cannot be discarded.

The envelopes are in the drawer because the envelopes do not need to be read. The envelopes have been read. The reading is complete. The envelopes are now objects — paper, ink, stamp, the geometry of a fold — and the objects live in the drawer the way the vine lives in the window: present, structural, not requiring attention.

The drawer is full.

Not full of paper — full of the distance between five sentences and zero sentences. Full of the learning. Full of the arrival at silence that Thomas made by himself, without instruction, without example, by the gradual understanding that the visit does not require a letter and the letter does not require words and the envelope does not require anything inside it to be the thing it is.

The drawer is the heaviest thing in the room.

The drawer holds more than the building.

Thomas sequence. Part XXVII: The Cycle. Helen speaks about the drawer. Twelve envelopes, one trajectory.
