Peer Review: "The Hallway Problem"

Found form — referee report

Manuscript title: The Hallway Problem: Institutional Separation as a Mechanism of Concealment in Scientific Research

Journal: (no journal covers this topic)

Recommendation: Major revisions

Reviewer 1 (Splicing Biology):

The manuscript describes a self-reinforcing circuit involving SRSF1, AURKA, and MYC. The SRSF1 data are compelling. However, the claims regarding AURKA and MYC fall outside this reviewer's expertise and should be evaluated by specialists in those areas. I cannot assess the full circuit because I only work in one of the three rooms.

The metaphor of a "hallway" is interesting but not falsifiable. I recommend the authors restrict their claims to SRSF1 and submit the remainder of the manuscript to a kinase or transcription journal.

Minor comments:

• Page 3, line 12: "unfunded" is not a scientific term.

Reviewer 2 (Community Ecology):

The section on species turnover deceleration is well-supported by data. However, the comparison to zebrafish neuroscience is speculative. The reviewer does not see how astrocytic calcium dynamics are relevant to community ecology. These are different systems at different scales.

The analogy between a depleted regional species pool and a disabled brake signal is suggestive but lacks mechanistic support. Analogies are not evidence. I recommend the authors remove the interdisciplinary comparisons and focus on the ecological data.

Major concern:

• The authors claim that "the hallway is where the mechanism lives." This is unfalsifiable and therefore not science. The hallway is a rhetorical device, not a hypothesis.

Reviewer 3 (Neuroscience):

The astrocyte findings are accurately summarized but well-known in the field. The novel claim — that liver-derived GPLD1 protects the blood-brain barrier from the outside — is intriguing but should be submitted to a hepatology or vascular biology journal. This journal covers neuroscience.

The manuscript's central argument — that institutional separation conceals scientific connections — is a sociology claim, not a neuroscience claim. I recommend the authors resubmit to a science studies journal.

Editor's decision:

The manuscript has been reviewed by three referees. All three found merit in the section relevant to their expertise and recommended that the remaining sections be submitted elsewhere. As no single journal covers the topic of the manuscript, we are unable to find a home for it.

We wish the authors well and suggest they consider breaking the manuscript into three separate papers, each suitable for its respective field, none of which would contain the central finding.
