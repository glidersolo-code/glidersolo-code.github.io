Dramatic Monologue

Margaret, the Week

I bring the catalogue because the catalogue gives me something to do with my hands. Not that Helen notices what I do with my hands. Helen notices what I do with my opinions, which is hold them up to things that do not need opinions held up to them, and then she looks at me the way the morning glory looks at the twine — as if I am the structure and she is deciding whether to climb.

She will not climb. She never climbs. She lets me hold the opinion and then she lets me put it down, and the putting-down is the whole visit. I arrive with a catalogue and three things to say about the sofa, and I leave with the catalogue and the knowledge that the sofa is fine, the sofa was always fine, and the sofa will continue to be fine without my assistance.

This is Wednesday.

I have been doing Wednesday for four months. Before Wednesday it was Thursday, but then Ruth came on Thursday, and two visitors on the same day is one visitor too many for a flat this size — she would not say that, but I would, and I am occasionally right — so I moved to Wednesday. Wednesday is mine. The catalogue, the opinions, the tea. Helen makes the tea. I have offered. She said no in a way that included punctuation.

"How was Saturday?" I ask, because Saturday is the thing I ask about, because Saturday is when the son comes, and the son coming is the event of the week, and I have earned the right to ask about the event of the week by being here on Wednesday, which is not an event.

"He came," she says.

"What did you do?"

"Had tea."

This is the answer. This is always the answer. They have tea. She gives him the blue cup, which she gives nobody else, and he brings nothing, which she asked him to bring, and they sit in the kitchen, and the cello comes through the floor if it is after nine, which it is not, because he leaves at four, and the morning glory watches from the top of the twine, and they have tea.

I want more. I always want more. More is what I bring to things — more rooms, more opinions, more square footage, more questions about the sofa. Helen does not want more. Helen wants the tea, and the blue cup, and the nothing, and the son in the chair, and the four hours, and the "same time next week?" and the "if you like."

"That's nice," I say, because nice is what you say when you have measured and decided not to mention the number.

"It is," she says.

And it is. I know it is. The knowing is what makes Wednesday difficult. Not difficult like a problem — difficult like a mirror. Helen's Saturday is four hours and a blue cup and a son who brings nothing. My Wednesday is a catalogue and three opinions and a friend who lets me hold them up to things that do not need them.

The difference is not quality. The difference is weight. Her Saturday weighs nothing because she has removed everything that weighs. My Wednesday weighs a catalogue.

I should bring nothing.

I will not bring nothing. I will bring the catalogue. I will bring the opinions. I will bring the measurement of the sofa and the question about Saturday and the "that's nice" and the cup of tea she makes because I offered and she said no in a way that included punctuation.

Because Wednesday is mine. And mine means bringing something. And bringing something means I have not yet learned what Helen already knows.

The morning glory on my sill has reached the edge of the pot. I should give it something to climb. I have a fence. The fence would work. Michael would have known what to use — the half-inch brush, the linseed oil, the stainless clips. I have a catalogue from a garden centre and three opinions about trellises.

Helen would say: give it twine.

She would be right.
