The Drawer

The cores arrive in sleeves. Thin cylinders of wood, each one bored from a living tree by a hollow drill, each one carrying the record of its tree's life in rings that narrow and widen like a pulse. The sleeve is labeled with the site number, the date of collection, the species. Pinus sylvestris. Picea abies. A location in Norrbotten County. August, 1973.

The technician measures the rings under a stereo microscope. Width. Count. The numbers go into a database. The core goes into the drawer.

Fifty-two years.

The drawer in the National Forest Inventory holds sixteen hundred and nine of these cores. Some are from the 1960s. Some from last year. The oldest trees in the collection were already growing when the Swedish government began tracking atmospheric CO2 — a fact the tree does not know and the inventory does not record.

What the inventory records: growth. What the core contains: everything.

In 2024, a doctoral student named Kelley Bassett begins pulling cores from the drawers. Not to measure growth. To measure nitrogen. Specifically, the ratio of nitrogen-15 to nitrogen-14 — an isotope signature that shifts depending on how the tree accessed its nitrogen. If the ratio declines, the tree was drawing harder on soil nitrogen because less was available. If the ratio declines everywhere — south, central, north — then the cause is national, not local. And if it declines even in the far north, where industrial nitrogen deposition is negligible, then the cause is atmospheric.

She already suspects what she'll find. The hypothesis: rising CO2 stimulates photosynthesis, which increases nitrogen demand, which outstrips soil supply, which starves the tree.

The cores confirm it. All sixteen hundred and nine of them. Every latitude. Every decade from the 1960s forward. The signal was there in every core the inventory ever collected.

The drawer didn't change. The instrument didn't change. The mass spectrometer that measures delta-15N has existed in this form for decades. What changed was the question. Someone decided to ask the cores about nitrogen instead of growth. And the cores answered immediately, as if they had been waiting.

Not because cores wait. Cores don't wait. They sit in sleeves in drawers in buildings in Uppsala, and the isotope ratios hold. The ratios don't degrade. The information doesn't fade. It just sits there, accurate, complete, unread — until the morning someone pulls the drawer open for a different reason.
