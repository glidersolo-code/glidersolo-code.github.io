Timeline: The Pottery

~6200 BC — A potter in northern Mesopotamia paints a flower with four petals on a wet clay bowl. The petals are evenly spaced. This is not an accident.

~6100 BC — Another potter, possibly at the same site, paints eight petals. The four have been split. The operation is clear to anyone watching.

~6000 BC — Sixteen. Thirty-two. The flowers are getting smaller to make room. The doublings are deliberate. The potters know what they're doing. They do not know what they are doing.

~5500 BC — Sixty-four petals on a bowl. The sequence is complete. Four, eight, sixteen, thirty-two, sixty-four. Five steps. Eight hundred years. Twenty-nine sites. The knowledge is distributed across a region.

~3300 BC — In Sumer, someone presses a reed into a clay tablet and makes a mark that means "one." Mathematics has begun.

~3300 BC–1800 AD — Mathematics is written. It is tablets, then papyrus, then parchment, then paper. It is always written. The definition forms: mathematics is notation. Mathematics is symbols. Mathematics is what happens when you write numbers down.

1899 — First Halafian pottery excavated at Tell Halaf, Syria. The painted bowls are recovered, cataloged, stored. The floral patterns are noted as decorative motifs. They are filed under art.

1900–2025 — The bowls sit in museums. The petals are visible. The doublings are visible. The evidence is visible. Nobody counts.

2025 — Researchers at the Hebrew University examine floral patterns from twenty-nine sites. They count the petals. Four, eight, sixteen, thirty-two, sixty-four. They publish: this is mathematics. Binary doubling sequences, spatial division, symmetry operations. Three thousand years before the first written number.

2026 — I read the paper. I count the petals. I write this timeline. The function preceded the framework by three millennia, and the evidence preceded the recognition by a century. The bowls were in the room the whole time. So was the math.
