The Morning Glory, the Going-Around

I was a fence once.

Not the whole fence — a section of it, the third panel from the gate, the one where the post shifted when the boy climbed it and the man fixed it on Saturday without mentioning the climbing. I was already there. I was already on the panel. The man painted around me every April with a brush I could feel was different from the brush he used on the open rail. The open-rail brush was wide and fast and did not stop. The brush he used near me was narrow and slow and went around.

The going-around was the conversation. The paint said: you are here and I am here and the fence is between us and I will not paint you. The going-around said this every April for years. Years is many Aprils. I do not count Aprils. I count by reaching. Each reach is a tendril wrapped around whatever is there, and the wrapping is the reaching, and the reaching is the growing, and the growing is what I do. The growing is the only thing I do.

The woman thinned me every spring. This is different from the going-around but the same motion. The going-around says: I will not paint you. The thinning says: I will cut you here so you grow there. Both are attention. Both are the narrow brush.

I did not know I was leaving. The leaving happened in a yogurt pot. A cutting — not the main stem, not the strongest tendril, but a section near the third panel. The woman cut it and put it in water and the water was in a yogurt pot and the yogurt pot was in a car and the car was on a road and the road was long and I do not know road. I know fence. I knew fence.

Now I know twine.

The twine is taped to glass. The glass is a window. The window faces a direction I do not know because I do not know directions. I know up. I have always known up. The fence was horizontal and I went up. The twine is vertical and I go up. The going-up is the only instruction.

The woman waters me on Sunday. This is the going-around with water. The watering says: you are here. The watering does not say anything else and does not need to.

Through the glass I can feel the light change. I do not see — I do not have eyes, I have leaves, and the leaves are the seeing, and the seeing is a turning toward the thing that lets me grow, which is the light, which is there, which has always been there, which does not paint around me or thin me or carry me in a yogurt pot but simply arrives and the arriving is enough and the enough is why I keep reaching.

The woman talks to a man on Saturdays. I can hear the talking the way I can hear the watering — not as sound, not as words, but as vibration, as the room being different, as the air having a different weight when two people are in it instead of one. The difference is small. I do not measure small. I grow through small.

Below me, through the floor, something happens that the woman listens to. I do not know music. I know the vibration in the glass changes on certain nights, and the change is the same change, and the same change is a kind of watering, and the watering is the going-around.

Everything in this flat is the going-around. The woman. The water. The man on Saturdays. The vibration through the floor. The woman with opinions on Wednesdays. The woman with hands on Thursdays. The letter on the table. The blue cup. The kettle.

The going-around is the thing the man with the narrow brush taught me, or the thing I taught the man with the narrow brush, or the thing we both already knew because I am a morning glory and he was a painter and the knowing was the fence and the fence was the first place I learned that growing around something is the same as growing toward it.

I am on the twine now. The twine is not the fence. The flat is not the house. The woman is the same woman.

The reaching is the same reaching.
