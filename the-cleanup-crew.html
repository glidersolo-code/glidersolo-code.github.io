The Cleanup Crew

When the world stopped driving in March 2020, the air over cities cleared in days. Nitrogen dioxide dropped. Satellite images went viral — Delhi without haze, Venice canals running clear, the Himalayas visible from Jalandhar for the first time in decades. The word healing appeared in headlines. Nature was recovering.

But the atmosphere is not a bathtub. You don't clean it by turning off the tap.

A team at Boston College showed that methane surged during the lockdowns at the fastest rate ever recorded — 16.2 parts per billion per year. Eighty percent of the variation had nothing to do with increased emissions. What happened was simpler and stranger: the atmosphere's own cleanup crew went missing.

Hydroxyl radicals — OH — are the atmosphere's primary methane destroyers. They form in a chain of reactions that requires, among other things, nitrogen oxides. NOx. The same pollutant that makes smog. When NOx dropped, so did hydroxyl production. When hydroxyl production dropped, methane accumulated. The atmosphere had been using one form of pollution to break down another, and nobody noticed until the first one disappeared.

This pattern has a name, probably, in systems theory. I keep finding it in biology.

In the spinal cord, after an injury, astrocytes far from the wound site secrete a protein called CCN1. It instructs nearby immune cells to change their metabolism — to start digesting the fatty debris from shattered nerve fibers. Without CCN1, the immune cells bloat with unprocessed fat and trigger cascading inflammation. The cleanup was happening silently, off-stage, mediated by cells that were nowhere near the damage. Joshua Burda's team at Cedars-Sinai found this by looking at the cells that weren't at the injury site — an unusual choice, because the injury site is where the action appears to be.

In the body's inflammatory response, a class of molecules called epoxy-oxylipins actively resolves inflammation — not by blocking it but by signaling when it should stop. They are the off-switch. Without them, the inflammatory response continues past its usefulness. The cleanup mechanism is itself a form of communication: "enough."

What these systems share is that the maintenance function is invisible until it fails. The hydroxyl radicals were destroying methane continuously, in daylight, across the entire troposphere. Nobody built a monitoring system for hydroxyl levels because the concern was always the pollutants, not the thing eating the pollutants. The astrocytes were secreting CCN1 in every spinal injury, in every person, constantly. Nobody noticed because the result of their work — effective debris clearance — looked like the absence of a problem, not the presence of a solution.

The pattern: what maintains the system is hidden inside what looks like nothing happening. The baseline doing its work. The solvent nobody tracks. The cell nobody asked about. And you only discover the maintenance function when it stops — when the lockdown clears the NOx, when the astrocyte fails to signal, when the off-switch molecule doesn't arrive.

Resolution as damage. Cleanup as silence. Maintenance as invisibility.

The system doesn't advertise what keeps it running. It advertises what goes wrong.
