The Chair

Thomas heard the cello from the chair.

He has heard the cello before — once, on the landing, that Saturday in the first year when he met Ruth by accident and recognized what Helen heard every Wednesday. But the landing is not the chair. The landing is the corridor version. The chair is the room version. The chair is where Helen sits.

The Sarabande in G major. Not the D minor — the D minor is Wednesday. The G major is Saturday, apparently. The G major is brighter, higher, less of the darkness that made the D minor feel like something structural being tested. The G major feels like something structural being confirmed.

Through the floor, the Sarabande arrived as a series of low frequencies that Thomas felt in the arms of the chair before he heard them in his ears. The chair vibrates. Helen sits in a vibrating chair every Wednesday at nine and every Saturday at who-knows-when and the vibrating is the Sarabande and the Sarabande is Ruth and Ruth is below and the below is the base and the base is where the mechanism changes.

Helen said nothing while the cello played. Thomas said nothing. The vine said nothing. The vine had three wraps on the twine and the three wraps vibrated too, faintly, the way everything in the flat vibrates when Ruth plays — the cup, the lemon, the water in the kettle, the masking tape curling at its edges. Everything vibrates. Nothing mentions it.

The cello stopped. Helen said: "That's it."

Two words. The two words could mean: the cello has stopped. Or: that's what it sounds like from this chair. Or: that's what I have been listening to for two years and now you know.

Thomas heard all three meanings. Thomas is learning Helen's compression. The compression is the thinning. The thinning is: fewer words, more meanings per word. At maximum compression, one word holds everything. Helen is approaching maximum compression. Helen said "There" about the vine. Helen said "That's it" about the cello. Helen will say something even shorter about the next thing, and the shorter it gets, the more it holds, until the holding is silence and the silence is the most compressed form of love and the love does not need the word because the word was only ever the love's way of being visible and the love does not need to be visible anymore.

Thomas drove home.

He heard the G major in the arms of the chair for thirty-seven minutes.

Thomas sequence. Part XXV: The Second Spring. Thomas hears the cello from Helen's chair.
