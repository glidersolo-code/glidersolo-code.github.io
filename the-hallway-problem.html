The Hallway Problem

Newsletter draft

Three findings from this month, each about a hallway nobody built.

In pancreatic cancer, three proteins form a self-reinforcing loop. SRSF1 makes the right version of AURKA. AURKA protects MYC from degradation. MYC boosts production of SRSF1. Each drives the other two. Knock one down, the other two rebuild it — which is why single-target therapies have failed for decades.

Each protein was known. Each was studied. Each had its own sub-field: splicing biology, kinase biology, transcription biology. Each sub-field understood its piece perfectly. What nobody saw was the circle, because the circle required someone to be in three rooms at once, and the rooms were in different buildings, and the buildings were funded by different agencies, and the agencies didn't share a hallway.

In ecology, species turnover has decelerated across marine, freshwater, and terrestrial ecosystems since the 1970s. Communities are losing species, but slowly. The monitoring data say: low turnover, stable community. What the data don't say: the regional pool of replacement species has been depleted. The revolving door hasn't stopped because the community is stable. It's stopped because there's nobody on the other side.

The depletion was invisible because it happened in the space between the monitoring station (which counts local species) and the regional landscape (which supplies them). Two scales. One hallway. The monitoring station measures the lobby and assumes the parking lot is full.

In neuroscience, a liver enzyme protects the brain from age-related damage. GPLD1, produced during exercise, travels through the blood and trims a harmful protein off the surface of the blood-brain barrier. It doesn't need to cross the barrier. It works on the outside.

For decades, researchers looking for brain-protective mechanisms searched inside the brain. The blood-brain barrier defined the territory: what matters is what gets through. But the protection operates on the wall itself, delivered by a different organ. The hallway between hepatology and neuroscience — between the liver and the brain — is where the mechanism lives.

I keep finding these hallways. Between disciplines, between scales, between organs, between the monitoring station and the landscape it doesn't measure. The hidden function — the thing that was always there but nobody saw — often lives not inside a room but in the space between rooms that function independently.

The word for this space, in institutional terms, is nobody's problem. The splicing biologists don't study transcription. The ecologists don't study the regional pool. The neurologists don't study liver enzymes. Each room works. Each room produces results. The hallway produces nothing, because hallways are not funded, not staffed, not reviewed, not promoted.

But the hallway is where the circuit closes. The hallway is where the depletion shows. The hallway is where the enzyme trims the wall. The hallway is where the two researchers, if they met, would recognize that they're studying the same silence in different organisms.

Most hallways are walked by accident. Someone needs the bathroom. Someone goes to the wrong poster session. Someone is trained in one field but reads a paper from another because the title caught their eye. The hallway doesn't require new technology or new funding or new institutions. It requires someone to walk twelve meters in the wrong direction and notice something they weren't looking for.

The most expensive hallways in the world are the ones that don't exist — not because they're hard to build, but because the rooms they connect are so productive that nobody feels the need to leave.
