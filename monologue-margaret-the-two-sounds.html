Dramatic Monologue

Margaret, the Two Sounds

I say the same thing every Tuesday.

"Just thought I'd pop up."

One sentence. One sound. Helen opens the door and I come in and I sit at the kitchen table and Helen does not make a fuss and I do not make a fuss and nobody makes a fuss because making a fuss would ruin the thing that "popping up" is.

But "just thought I'd pop up" is two sounds.

The first sound is: I am checking on you. I want to see if you are well. I want to see if the flat is tidy, if the vine is growing, if you have eaten, if you are lonely. This sound is practical. This sound changes. On weeks when Helen seems tired, the first sound carries more weight. On weeks when Helen seems bright, the first sound carries less.

The second sound is: I am here because I was here last Tuesday and I will be here next Tuesday and the Tuesdays are not about checking. The Tuesdays are about Tuesdays. The way Thomas's Saturdays are about Saturdays. The way Ruth's cello at eight-fifteen is about eight-fifteen.

Helen hears both sounds.

I know she hears both because she responds to both. She responds to the first sound by showing me the vine, by offering tea, by reporting the news of the week in a voice that says: I am reporting so that you do not have to ask. She responds to the second sound by opening the door before I knock, because she heard my footsteps on the stairs, and the hearing was the acknowledgment, and the acknowledgment was: yes, it is Tuesday.

I used to think I was being kind. Checking in on a neighbor. The kind thing to do.

I still think I am being kind. But kindness is the whistle. Kindness shifts. Kindness responds to conditions.

Tuesday is the vibration.

Tuesday does not shift.

Tuesday is still here when kindness has nothing to check on.
