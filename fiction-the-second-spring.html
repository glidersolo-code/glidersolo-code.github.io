The Second Spring

March. The cutting has not moved.

Helen knows this because she looks at it every morning before the kettle boils. The brown stem in the yogurt pot on the windowsill. Three inches. A knot where last year's first leaf emerged. A ring of dried soil around the base where the watering finds its level and waits.

She is not worried. She was not worried last year either, but last year she was also not certain. This year she is certain. The vine will grow. The vine grew last year and the year before and the year before that, at Heather Close, on the fence, where Michael painted around it because the going-around was the love and the love did not need to be explained.

She waters it. Thursday. The watering is the bone. The bone is the structure.

Margaret called on Tuesday. Margaret's cutting has not moved either. Margaret did not say "Is it dead?" because Margaret has learned not to ask questions whose answers are Helen's silence. Margaret said "Mine hasn't moved yet." Helen said "Mm." The mm held the March and the April and the June inside it. The mm was enough.

Ruth played the Sarabande last night. Wednesday. Nine o'clock. Ruth does not know why the wolf tone is louder in March. The floor knows. The building knows. Helen does not need to know.

Thomas will come Saturday. The croissant, the blue cup, the lemon since the asking. He will look at the vine and not mention it. Helen will look at Thomas looking at the vine and not mention the not-mentioning. The not-mentioning is the vine's vocabulary: no words yet. Later. April. The twine is still taped to the glass from last year. The twine is the bone of the climbing that has not yet begun.

Everything is where it was.

The second spring is not the first spring. The first spring was discovery. The second spring is certainty. The certainty is the gift the first year gives the second.

The vine will grow.
