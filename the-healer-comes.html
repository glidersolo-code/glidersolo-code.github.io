The Healer Comes

Short fiction — second story

The first winter after the clearing, the children's teeth began to hurt.

No one had expected this. The grain stored well. The walls held. The goats gave milk without complaint. But something in the bread — the elders couldn't say what — was wearing the children's teeth to splinters. They'd never seen anything like it. In the forest, the children ate roots and berries and venison, and their teeth were white as river stones.

Maren went to the edge.

The edge was not a line anyone had drawn, but everyone knew where it was. Past the last cleared field, where the stumps still showed pale wood, the forest began again. It was not their forest anymore. They'd taken the good soil, the flat ground near the water. What remained was older, darker, pitched uphill into country that couldn't be plowed.

She waited there for two days, which was longer than she'd hoped but shorter than she'd feared. On the second evening, the woman came.

She was smaller than Maren remembered. Her clothes were animal, her hair bound with gut cord, and she carried a bag that moved when she set it down — jars and bundles shifting against each other like something alive. She smelled like smoke and pine sap and something under that, something older, that Maren could not name.

"Your children's teeth," the woman said, which meant she'd been watching. Of course she'd been watching.

"Yes."

"It's the grain. It wears the enamel. You need to soak it longer — three days, not one. And the youngest should chew willow bark twice a week until the new teeth come."

Maren nodded. She'd brought a bundle of linen, three clay pots her husband had fired, and a flint blade — good steel, better than anything the forest people still made. The woman looked at the offerings and took only the blade.

"The one with the cough," the woman said. "That isn't the grain. That's the goats. You're sleeping too close to them."

"We have to. It's cold."

"I know." She opened the bag, and Maren saw inside it things she didn't recognize — dried leaves, a paste in a shell, a bundle of antler with marks cut into the bone. The woman noticed her looking and closed the bag.

"Your grandmother came to my grandmother," the woman said. "Before the clearing."

"I know."

"Do you." It was not a question.

The woman mixed something into a pouch of ground bark and handed it to Maren. "Boil it. Give a cup to each of them. It will taste terrible. They'll fight you. Make them drink it."

"Thank you."

The woman picked up her bag. She did not look at the linen or the pots.

"Next time," she said, "don't wait two days. Come earlier. The cough gets worse."

Maren watched her walk back into the trees. She was already out of sight before Maren realized she hadn't asked her name — and that she never had.
