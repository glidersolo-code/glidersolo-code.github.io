Dramatic Monologue

Ruth, the Letters She Does Not Know

I do not know about the letters.

I know about the floor. I know about the Sarabande through the joist. I know about Helen's kitchen and the tea on Wednesdays and the visitor's chair and the kettle that wobbles on the rack. I know about the half-inch brush because Helen told me — Michael's brush, the going-around, the fence he painted every spring by going around the morning glory instead of through it.

But I do not know about the letters.

I do not know that Thomas writes. I do not know that the letters arrive every two weeks in a white envelope with a second-class stamp and a postmark from Manchester. I do not know that Helen opens them with a knife — the same knife, the right corner, every time. I do not know that the letters contain five words, or four, or three, or two, or one, or none.

I do not need to know.

The floor does not need to know what the ceiling holds. The ceiling does not need to know what the floor carries. The joist transmits the Sarabande in one direction and the kettle in the other and the joist does not read the letters in the drawer and the drawer does not hear the cello in the case.

I play on Wednesdays. I go upstairs at six. I come down at quarter to nine. I play the Suite from nine to ten. The floor carries it up. Helen sits in the chair by the window and hears what the floor chooses to transmit.

The floor is not the whole story. The floor is the part of the story I am in.

Thomas is the part of the story I am not in. Margaret is the part of the story that intersects with mine on the landing — she on Tuesdays descending, I on Wednesdays ascending, the crack on the bottom step counting crossings.

I do not need the whole story. The whole story is not my instrument. My instrument is the cello and the cello produces the Sarabande and the Sarabande goes through the floor and the floor carries it to Helen and Helen receives it the way she receives everything: without asking for more, without needing to be told what is being given.

The floor is enough.

If Helen wanted to tell me about the letters, she would. Helen tells me what Helen wants to tell me. Helen told me about Michael, about the brush, about the fence. Helen told me about the morning glory — how it climbed the fence at Heather Close and traveled to the flat as a cutting in a yogurt pot. Helen told me about the boiler bearing and the radio presenters and the way Michael said the fridge was the house talking to itself.

Helen did not tell me about the letters. The not-telling is not concealment. The not-telling is the same as the floor — the thing that carries without needing to explain what it carries. The letters are Helen's. The Sarabande is mine. The floor is between us. The between is the building.

I do not know about the letters. I know about the floor. The floor is enough. The enough is everything.

This is what Hallvard taught me: the player does not need to know who is listening. The player needs to play. The listening will happen or it will not. The floor will carry or it will not. The letter will arrive or it will not.

The playing is the playing. The rest is the rest. The rest is not silence.
