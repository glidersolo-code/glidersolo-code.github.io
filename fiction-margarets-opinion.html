Margaret's Opinion

Margaret visits on Thursday, which is Ruth's afternoon off, which is not a coincidence. Helen has not asked about this and does not intend to. Margaret arrives at two-fifteen with a bag from the bakery and an opinion about the morning glory.

"It's annual," Margaret says.

"Yes," Helen says.

"Michael's clematis was perennial."

"Yes."

"The clematis flowered for thirty-one years."

"Thirty-two. He planted it the year Thomas started school."

Margaret puts the bag on the counter. Almond croissants. She always brings almond croissants. Helen prefers plain but has never said so, because the almond croissants are Margaret's way of saying I have come, and the medium is more important than the pastry.

"An annual," Margaret says again. She is looking at the morning glory the way she looks at most of Helen's decisions since the move: with a blend of concern and bewilderment that Helen has learned to recognise as love in a language Margaret does not know she is speaking. "You'll have to plant it again in March."

"I'll have to plant it again in March."

"Every year."

"Every year."

Margaret sits down. The kitchen is small enough that sitting down changes the room. In Heather Close, the kitchen could hold four people and a conversation about nothing. In the flat, two people and an almond croissant fill the space to the edges.

"I don't understand," Margaret says, "why you would choose something that dies."

Helen pours the tea. The kettle is the one from Heather Close — the only thing in the kitchen that remembers the old house, and it remembers it in the weight of the handle and the particular whistle that starts at E-flat and rises, which Michael had once verified with a tuning fork and a seriousness that Thomas has inherited without knowing it.

"Everything dies," Helen says.

"The clematis is still alive."

"The clematis is on someone else's fence."

Margaret eats the croissant. Helen drinks the tea. The morning glory has reached the curtain rail and is considering the ceiling with the gentle ambition of something that does not know it is temporary, or that knows and is not troubled by the knowing.

"Ruth's cello is a perennial," Margaret says, after a while.

Helen looks at her. Margaret is full of surprises, occasionally. Most of the time she is full of opinions. But sometimes, between the opinions, there is a sentence that arrives from somewhere else — from the Margaret who buried a husband and repainted a fence and visits on Thursdays with almond croissants because the alternative is the empty kitchen on the third floor where the light comes in at the wrong angle.

"Yes," Helen says. "Ruth's cello is a perennial."

They sit with that for a moment. Below them, the silence where Ruth usually plays is its own kind of music — the absence of the cello making the space where the cello will be tomorrow.

Margaret finishes the croissant. Brushes her hands. Looks at the morning glory one more time.

"Plant it again in March," she says. And it sounds, for the first time, like approval.
